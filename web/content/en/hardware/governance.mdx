# Engineering & Compliance

Between "it runs" and "it sells" lies a wall of compliance. This wall isn't software features — it's hard constraints at the physical and system level: functional safety ensures that failures don't harm people, EMC ensures the device doesn't interfere with others, and certification transforms both into auditable documentation. These three pillars span drones, automotive, and robotics, but the specific standards and entry thresholds differ across each domain.

---

## Functional Safety

The core question of functional safety is: **when the system fails, can it reach a safe state?** Failures here include both random hardware failures and systematic failures (software bugs). Safety-critical systems cannot rely on "it probably won't happen" — they require redundancy and diagnostics to reduce risk to an acceptable level.

**Safety Integrity Levels (ASIL)**: ISO 26262 partitions automotive functional safety into four levels, ASIL A (lowest) through ASIL D (highest). Levels are derived from three factors: **Severity** (S, injury severity), **Exposure** (E, probability of exposure), and **Controllability** (C, controllability). S+E+C determines the target ASIL. For example, thermal runaway in a traction battery carries S3 (life-threatening), E4 (could occur during most driving cycles), and C2 (driver has limited control) — a combination that maps to ASIL C or higher.

**Redundant Architectures**: Safety-critical systems typically employ redundant designs. A 2oo2 (Two-out-of-Two) dual-channel architecture requires both paths to detect a fault before triggering an action, suitable for braking systems. A 1oo2 (One-out-of-Two) architecture triggers on any single anomaly detection, more conservative but prone to false positives. The more advanced 2oo3 (Two-out-of-Three) is used in flight control and other ultra-high-reliability applications. The choice between Fail-operational (continue operating after a failure) and Fail-safe (transition to a safe state after failure) depends on the system's role — steering typically demands Fail-operational, while throttle and parking brakes may accept Fail-safe.

**Robotic Functional Safety** shares similar frameworks with automotive but with different emphases. ISO 10218-1/2 defines industrial robot safety requirements, and ISO/TS 15066 specifies collaborative robot body region force limits. Joint torque sensors and current-sensing collision detection in collaborative arms are hardware-level force limiting mechanisms, not software settings.

## EMC Compatibility

EMC addresses two directions: **emissions** (interference the device radiates outward) and **immunity** (the device's ability to withstand external interference). Any hardware system with fast switching signals — PWM, switching power supplies, wireless transmitters — must satisfy both aspects.

**Conducted and Radiated Emissions**: The high-frequency harmonics generated by MOSFET switching edges in switching power supplies conduct through power cables (conducted emissions) and radiate through space (radiated emissions). Automotive components must meet CISPR 25 radiated emission limits; drone RC/data links must meet FCC Part 15 or equivalent standards. Typical remediation includes input/output EMI filters, ferrite bead cable wrapping, shielded cables, and metal enclosures.

**Immunity Requirements**: Automotive components must pass ISO 11452 series immunity tests, including radiated immunity (resistance to external fields) and Bulk Current Injection (BCI) tests. Whether a drone GPS receiver maintains positioning when an interference source is present nearby is a classic immunity engineering problem. ISO 13766 specifies EMC requirements for construction machinery; aviation equipment must meet DO-160.

**EMC Design Essentials**: In layout, place power inputs near connectors, keep crystal oscillators and high-speed signal lines away from board edges. In layer stacking, use complete ground planes in multi-layer PCBs to separate analog and digital regions. In grounding strategy, use single-point star grounding to avoid ground loops. The three practical questions for EMC remediation: Which path does noise exit through (conducted/radiated)? Which path do sensitive circuits receive interference through (antenna/coupling)? Does the remediation introduce new coupling paths?

## Certification Workflows

Certification transforms functional safety and EMC requirements into auditable evidence. Different markets have distinct access requirements for different products — certification is the prerequisite for market access.

**Drone Compliance Essentials**: Civilian drones must comply with each country's civil aviation authority rules. The FAA requires registration for drones over 250g; EU CE marking requires compliance with RED (Radio Equipment Directive) and RoHS, and the airframe must pass structural strength testing. China's CAAC implements tiered standards for drones, and commercial operators must apply for operating permits. Key references: ASTM F3363 (consumer drones), DO-178C (flight control software — not mandatory but treated as engineering practice baseline).

**Automotive Type Approval**: Vehicles entering a market must obtain type approval, covering three major categories: emissions (EPA/EU standards), safety (FMVSS/GSR), and EMC (UN ECE R10). OEM suppliers must provide ISO 26262 functional safety documentation, ISO 9001 or IATF 16949 quality system certification, and ASIL decomposition reports. Third-party bodies such as VCA (UK Vehicle Certification Agency) or TÜV conduct testing.

**Robotics CE Marking**: Industrial robots entering the EU market must bear the CE mark, covering the Machinery Directive (2006/42/EC), EMC Directive (2014/30/EU), and functional safety references ISO 10218 and EN ISO 13849. Collaborative robots must additionally comply with ISO/TS 15066 and pass functional testing. The certification process includes: technical file preparation (risk assessment, design descriptions, test reports), Declaration of Conformity (DoC), and third-party testing (for SIL 2 and above safety functions).

**Key Certification Documents**: Hardware requires Functional Safety Concept (FSC), Technical Safety Concept (TSC), hardware architecture metrics (SPFM/LFM) calculation reports, and EMC test reports. Software requires software safety requirements specifications, low-level and high-level software architecture descriptions, and unit/integration/system test reports. Production requires Production Process Control Plan (CP Plan) and product traceability records.

## Engineering & Compliance Summary

Functional safety, EMC, and certification form the iron triangle of whole-system compliance. Functional safety answers "is it safe after failure?" EMC answers "does it interfere with others and get interfered with?" Certification uses third-party testing and documentation to transform the conclusions of the first two into trusted market access.

Engineers should incorporate compliance considerations early in design — the architecture phase determines ASIL decomposition and redundancy strategy, the PCB layout phase determines EMC remediation cost, and certification testing discovered late in prototyping becomes expensive to fix.
