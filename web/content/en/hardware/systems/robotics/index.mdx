## How Robotics Differs from Drones / Automotive

- **Kinematic complexity**: Unlike drones (thrust-vectored thrust) or automotive (primarily wheeled), robotics spans serial arms, SCARA, parallel structures, and omnidirectional mobile platforms — each with fundamentally different kinematics and control semantics.
- **Safety**: Collaborative robots (cobots) operate in human-shared workspaces under ISO 10218 and ISO/TS 15066 — force limiting, collision detection, and safety-monitored stops are first-class requirements, not add-ons.
- **System integration**: Robots are almost always part of a larger workflow (MES, PLC, conveyor tracking, vision inspection) — the "whole system" boundary extends to factory-floor orchestration, not just the robot itself.

---

## Configurations (Kinematic Types)

- **Serial manipulators**: 6-DOF articulated arms — most common in industrial assembly, welding, and painting. Forward/inverse kinematics, joint limits, and singularity regions are core design concerns.
- **SCARA**: Selective Compliance Assembly Robot Arm — planar motion, high speed, and precision for pick-and-place on 2D planes.
- **Parallel (Delta/Stewart)**: High-speed pick-and-place; moving platform suspended by three or more parallel chains — excellent for cleanrooms and packaging.
- **Mobile bases**: Differential drive, Ackermann steering, omnidirectional (omni-wheel / Mecanum), and tracked — chosen based on floor environment and maneuverability needs.
- **Composite AGVs / AMRs**: Mobile bases combined with manipulator arms; automated guided vehicles (AGV) follow fixed paths while autonomous mobile robots (AMR) use SLAM for dynamic navigation.

---

## Hardware Key Points

- **Joint reducers**: Harmonic drives (high reduction ratio, zero backlash) and planetary gearboxes — sized by reflected inertia and required torque density.
- **Torque sensors**: Optional joint torque sensing enables admittance control and collision detection; common in cobot end-effectors.
- **End-effector IO**: Tool-changing systems, pneumatic or electric grippers, and force/torque sensors at the wrist — the interface to the actual task.
- **Safety fencing and light curtains**: Industrial场景 requires physical guards or optoelectronic curtains; cobots rely on force-limited joints and safety-monitored stops within the workspace boundary.

## Control, Software, and System Integration

Joint servo control and trajectory planning belong to the **robot controller** — this is fundamentally a **systems integration problem**: the embedded layer still routes back to [Control](/en/hardware/basics/control) and [Communication](/en/hardware/basics/communication) for low-level loops and bus protocols.

- **Robot controller**: Executes trajectory plans (joint-space / Cartesian), manages tool-center-point (TCP) frames, and enforces velocity/acceleration limits.
- **Upper-level scheduling**: Integration with MES, PLC, conveyor tracking, and vision inspection systems — the "whole system" boundary in robotics extends well beyond the robot itself.
- **Safety architecture**: Safety-rated monitored stop (STO), safe torque off (STO), and force limiting per ISO 10218 — these are hardware-level safety functions, not just software settings.

---

## What an Individual Can Do

- **Desktop manipulator kits**: Low-cost 4–6 DOF arms (SG90, MG996, or similar) for learning inverse kinematics, joint-space trajectory planning, and ROS MoveIt.
- **Progression path**: Single-axis PID identification → multi-axis interpolation → integration with gripper and vision → choose mobile base or collaborative arm depending on interest.
- **Research / industrial**: After the kit stage, ISO 10218 safety architecture, robot controller selection (EtherCAT-based), and SLAM for mobile bases represent the transition from prototype to production.
