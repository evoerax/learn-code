# Onboard Networks & Power

The **Onboard network** page covers the electrical backbone of a modern vehicle: power distribution, in-vehicle networking buses, and the diagnostic infrastructure that ties them together. Unlike the simple 5V regulated rails of a hobby drone ESC, automotive power and communication must survive transients, temperature extremes, and decades of vibration — all while meeting functional safety requirements.

---

## Power Supply Architecture

### 12V and 24V Rails

Most passenger vehicles operate on a **12V negative-ground** electrical system, derived from a lead-acid starter battery (typically 40–70 Ah). The alternator regurator maintains 13.8–14.4V during charging. Heavy commercial vehicles (trucks, buses) often use **24V** systems (two 12V batteries in series) to reduce current and cable gauge for the same power.

Key characteristics:
- **Starting surge**: Cranking current can exceed 400A for 1–3 seconds, causing momentary voltage dip to 6–8V. Electronics must tolerate this without resetting.
- **Load dump**: When the alternator is disconnected while charging, a voltage spike of 60–125V can appear on the 12V rail for up to 400ms. TVS diodes or absorptive circuits are mandatory for any node directly connected to the battery rail.
- **Reverse polarity**: A reversed battery connection can destroy unprotected circuits. Automotive modules typically include reverse-polarity protection diodes or MOSFET-based ideal-diode circuits.
- **48V mild hybrid (MHEV)**: Modern architectures introduce a 48V rail (up to 60V maximum) for efficient boost/sailing operation, with a DC-DC converter interfacing to the traditional 12V bus. The 48V domain powers high-current loads like e-turbo or active suspension.

### Distribution and Fusing

Automotive distribution uses **blade fuses** (ATO/ATC standard, color-coded by amperage: yellow=15A, white=20A, blue=25A, etc.) and **Midi/Max fuses** for higher current. Fuse boxes (SJB — smart junction box) often integrate relay control and LIN/CAN interfaces for smart switching. Power routing follows star topology from the battery, with fused branch circuits to each load.

## In-Vehicle Networking Buses

### CAN Bus (Controller Area Network)

CAN is the dominant in-vehicle network, specified as ISO 11898-2 (high-speed, up to 1 Mbps) and ISO 11898-3 (low-speed fault-tolerant, up to 125 kbps). Key concepts:

- **Frame structure**: 11-bit (standard) or 29-bit (extended) identifier, up to 8 bytes data, CRC, ACK, and end-of-frame bits.
- **Bus arbitration**: Non-destructive bit-wise arbitration using dominant (0) and recessive (1) levels on a differential pair. Lower identifier wins — no collisions.
- **Load rate**: Automotive designs typically target a maximum of 30% bus load during peak traffic to preserve latency margins. High-speed CAN at 500 kbps with multiple ECU chatter can approach saturation without careful message scheduling.
- **Termination**: Two 120Ω resistors at the bus ends (one at each extreme node). Missing or incorrect termination causes reflection and communication errors.

Typical CAN use cases: powertrain (engine ECU, transmission), chassis (ABS/ESC, steering angle sensor), body (BCM, HVAC), and diagnostic gateways.

### LIN Bus (Local Interconnect Network)

LIN (ISO 17987) is a low-speed (1–20 kbps), single-wire subsidiary bus used for simple sensors and actuators where CAN is cost-prohibitive. The master polls slave nodes on a schedule table; no collision detection is needed. LIN is commonly used for:
- Interior lighting, window switches, mirror adjusters
- Rain/lights sensors, climate control actuators
- Seat position motors

The LIN physical layer is not fault-tolerant like CAN; a single wire + ground reference is used.

### FlexRay

FlexRay (ISO 17458) is a deterministic, high-speed (up to 10 Mbps) bus for safety-critical applications like x-by-wire (steering, braking). It uses a time-triggered protocol with static and dynamic segments, supporting redundant channel A/B for fault tolerance. Adoption has decreased in favor of Automotive Ethernet for new platforms due to cost and complexity.

### Automotive Ethernet

As ADAS and connected car domains generate massive data (camera streams, LIDAR point clouds), **Automotive Ethernet** (100BASE-T1, 1000BASE-T1 per IEEE 802.3bw/802.3bp) has become the backbone for high-bandwidth sensor aggregation. A **gateway ECU** acts as the hub, bridging CAN/LIN/FlexRay to Ethernet and routing traffic to domain controllers (powertrain, chassis, ADAS, cockpit).

Key differences from office Ethernet:
- **Single-pair cabling**: 100BASE-T1 uses a single twisted pair (not 4 pairs like RJ45), reducing weight and cost — critical for automotive.
- **EMC requirements**: Stringent electromagnetic compatibility ensures no interference with radio receivers.
- **QoS and AVB/TSN**: Audio Video Bridging / Time-Sensitive Networking provides guaranteed latency for time-critical frames.

## Diagnostics and Logging

### UDS and OBD-II

**UDS (Unified Diagnostic Services, ISO 14229)** is the primary diagnostic protocol used in automotive ECUs. It operates over CAN (or Ethernet) and provides services such as:
- **Session control**: Entering extended diagnostic sessions with elevated privileges.
- **Read/Write DTCs**: Reading Diagnostic Trouble Codes (stored fault conditions) and clearing them after repair.
- **Data monitoring**: Real-time reading of live data (sensor values, actuator states).
- **Routine control**: Executing on-board tests (compression test, adaptation relearning).

**OBD-II (On-Board Diagnostics II)** is a US-mandated subset of diagnostics focused on emissions-related monitoring. The OBD-II port (SAE J1962) provides access to the powertrain CAN bus at 500 kbps, with standardized PIDs (Parameter IDs) for emissions monitoring. EOBD (European OBD) is the European equivalent.

### Practical CAN and Power Diagnostics

Capturing CAN traffic and power rail behavior is essential for debugging vehicle systems:

- **CAN capturing**: A USB-CAN adapter (e.g., Kvaser, Vector, or budget options like CANalyze or lawmate clones) connected to the OBD-II port lets you log raw frames. Software like Wireshark (with CANDissector), SavvyCAN, or manufacturer tools (Vector CANalyzer, Bosch CANalyzer) decodes raw IDs to human-readable signals.
- **Power rail probing**: An oscilloscope with differential probes on the 12V rail during engine start reveals cranking dip depth, alternator ripple, and load-dump spike amplitude. This is critical before connecting any aftermarket electronics.
- **Bus load analysis**: Continuous logging during drive cycles identifies peak-load periods. If bus load exceeds 30–40%, message priorities and transmission rates need re-scheduling.

On a test bench (bench setup without a live vehicle), a **CANoe** or its open-source equivalent can simulate ECU chatter, and a bench power supply set to 12V/24V with programmable transient profiles can replicate load-dump and cranking profiles.

## Summary

Automotive onboard networks must meet demanding electrical and protocol requirements far beyond hobby systems. Key takeaways:
- **12V automotive power** is unforgiving: expect 6–8V cranking dips, 60–125V load-dump spikes, and decades of vibration.
- **CAN** is the workhorse bus; LIN handles cheap actuators; FlexRay serves x-by-wire; Automotive Ethernet aggregates high-bandwidth domains.
- **Diagnostics** (UDS over CAN, OBD-II on the DLC port) are the primary debug and service interface — mastering them is essential for any automotive hardware work.
