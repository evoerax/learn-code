# Infosys

Infosys Limited is a global leader in next-generation digital services and consulting, headquartered in Bangalore, Karnataka, India — the city known as India's "Silicon Valley." Founded in 1981 by seven engineers led by N.R. Narayana Murthy with just ₹10,000 (approximately $1,000 at the time), Infosys became the poster child of India's IT revolution and the first Indian company listed on the New York Stock Exchange (1999). As of 2024, Infosys employs approximately 343,000 people worldwide and generated $18.6 billion in annual revenue, making it India's second-largest IT services exporter after TCS and one of the world's most respected technology consulting and services companies. Under CEO Salil Parekh (since 2018), Infosys has pivoted to become a leader in AI-powered digital transformation, cloud services, and enterprise automation.

## Timeline

### 1981–1990: Founding and the Narayana Murthy Story

- **1981**: N.R. Narayana Murthy (then known as N.R. Narayana Murthy), along with six other engineers (**Nandan Nilekani, K. Dinesh, N.S. Raghavan, Shunash Nanda, Ravi Makhija, and Saligrama Seshadri** — later five of the six became partners), founded Infosys with a modest capital of **₹10,000** (~$1,000). Murthy had previously worked at Pathera Software and Systamatic, seeing firsthand the potential for India's engineering talent in software development. The company's early office was a modest room in Murthy's apartment in Bangalore.
- **1981**: Murthy's wife **Sudha Murthy** (renowned author and social worker) played a crucial early role, managing accounts and administrative functions in the nascent company.
- **1982**: Infosys's first client was **Data Basics Corporation**, a small US-based company. The client relationship was built on the concept of "hard work, integrity, and transparency" — values that would define the company's culture.
- **1983**: **Nandan Nilekani** joined Infosys (after leaving Phillips Software), bringing strong technical and management skills. Nilekani would later become Murthy's co-CEO and eventually chairman of UIDAI (Aadhaar).
- **1987**: Infosys established its first international office in **Boston, Massachusetts**, USA — marking the beginning of its global delivery model.
- **1989**: Infosys became one of the first Indian companies to practice **modern software engineering methodologies** — including structured testing, design reviews, and project management processes — years before such practices were standard in Indian IT.

### 1990–1999: US Entry and the NYSE IPO

- **1991**: India's economic liberalization (the reforms of July 1991) transformed India's IT industry. With restrictions on foreign investment easing, Infosys could now pursue large international projects and equity partnerships.
- **1993**: Infosys received its first **large international order** from a Fortune 500 company — a major milestone that validated its global ambitions.
- **1994**: Narayana Murthy became CEO; the company began building enterprise-scale capabilities for global banks and financial institutions.
- **1995**: Infosys opened offices in **Tokyo and Frankfurt** — establishing a presence in two of the world's largest economies outside the US.
- **1999**: On **March 11, 1999**, Infosys became the **first Indian company to list on the New York Stock Exchange (NYSE)**, raising approximately $70 million. The IPO was managed by Merrill Lynch and was considered a landmark event — proving that Indian companies could meet the stringent governance and reporting standards required by US markets. The IPO price was $12 per ADS; the stock closed at $17.25 on day one.
- **1999**: Infosys's NYSE listing made Narayana Murthy and several founding employees million-dollar entrepreneurs — a new phenomenon in India that inspired a generation of tech entrepreneurs.

### 1999–2008: Growth and Narayana Murthy's Legacy

- **2000**: Infosys was added to the **NASDAQ-100** index — the first Indian company to achieve this distinction. This made Infosys visible to global institutional investors tracking the index.
- **2001**: Narayana Murthy stepped down as CEO at age 55, succeeded by **Nandan Nilekani as co-CEO** (along with two other co-CEOs: S. Gopalakrishnan and Kris Gopalakrishnan). This was a carefully planned succession — Narayana Murthy had groomed Nilekani as his successor over many years.
- **2003**: Infosys introduced **Automotive Solutions** practice — expanding its vertical industry model beyond financial services.
- **2005**: Kris Gopalakrishnan became CEO; Infosys scaled to **50,000+ employees** and launched its **Finacle** banking platform — which would become one of the world's most widely deployed core banking solutions.
- **2006**: Infosys opened its **global education center** in Mysore, Karnataka — a 300-acre campus for employee training and skill development. The Mysore campus became one of the largest corporate training facilities in the world.
- **2007**: Revenue crossed **$4 billion**. Infosys was widely recognized as a bellwether of India's IT services boom.

### 2008–2013: Global Financial Crisis and Transformation

- **2008**: The global financial crisis led to a sharp slowdown in IT spending by Western banks and corporations. Infosys responded by expanding into **consulting** (acquiring Portland Group for $570 million in 2008) and non-FSSI sectors like manufacturing, retail, and energy.
- **2009**: Narayana Murthy publicly expressed concern about Infosys's growth slowdown — the company's stock fell sharply as investors worried about the impact of the recession on offshore outsourcing.
- **2010**: **S. Gopalakrishnan** became CEO; the company accelerated its "**Zero Non-Billable Employee**" initiative, pushing all employees toward billable work.
- **2011**: Infosys acquired **Simplus** (a cloud consulting firm) and **Helium** (a digital marketing agency) — building capabilities in emerging digital technologies.
- **2012**: Infosys launched **Cloud Ecosystem** services — positioning itself for the enterprise cloud computing wave.
- **2012**: Infosys won a landmark deal from **Reed Elsevier** — one of the largest outsourcing contracts of the year.

### 2013–2017: The Vishal Sikka Era

- **2013**: **Vishal Sikka**, a German-Indian technologist and former SAP executive board member (he was SAP's head of Products and Platform), became CEO and MD of Infosys — the first non-founder and first person from outside the founding team to lead the company. Sikka brought a vision of transforming Infosys from a traditional IT services firm into an "AI-first" company using design thinking and automation. He was 46 at the time and was widely seen as a bold choice.
- **2014**: Sikka launched the **"Renew and New" strategy** — focusing on high-margin digital services (cloud, AI, analytics) while automating or offshore-routing repetitive coding work. He introduced "**design thinking**" methodology throughout the company.
- **2014**: Infosys launched **Mana** — an AI-powered platform for industrial and enterprise automation, one of the earliest major AI platforms from an Indian IT services company.
- **2015**: Sikka faced criticism from some founders (particularly N.R. Narayana Murthy publicly questioned Sikka's aggressive acquisition strategy and cultural alignment). Murthy, who had remained chairman, became more vocal about governance concerns.
- **2016**: Infosys acquired **WhiteHat Jr** ($300 million) — a children's coding education startup — and **Nice Ltd** ($200 million) — a digital customer experience company. These acquisitions were controversial internally due to their high prices and cultural fit questions.
- **2017**: Vishal Sikka resigned as CEO in August 2017, citing "continuous distractions" from boardroom governance issues with the founders. The resignation was abrupt and sent Infosys's stock down sharply. Salil Parekh, a senior executive, was named interim CEO.
- **2017**: The board appointed **Salil Parekh** as permanent CEO. The Sikka era was widely seen as a failed experiment — the board turbulence and founder interference prevented Sikka from executing his vision.

### 2018–Present: Salil Parekh and the AI-First Pivot

- **2018**: **Salil Parekh** became CEO, bringing stability after two turbulent years. Parekh had spent nearly two decades at Infosys in various leadership roles, including heading the Financial Services and Manufacturing practices. His approach was more consensus-driven and focused on execution over transformation rhetoric.
- **2018**: Parekh launched the **"Refresh" strategy** — emphasizing four key service lines: AI and automation, cloud infrastructure, digital transformation, and enterprise sustainability.
- **2019**: Infosys returned to growth, with revenue increasing by high single digits. The company won major deals from **Verizon, BMW, and Daimler** — positioning itself in the automotive and connected vehicle digital services market.
- **2020**: Infosys achieved **zero debt** on its balance sheet — a milestone that reflected improved cash flow management and a leaner cost structure under Parekh.
- **2020**: COVID-19 pandemic accelerated digital transformation. Infosys launched **Infosys Cobalt** — a set of cloud frameworks and assets to help enterprises migrate to and operate on cloud platforms.
- **2021**: Infosys completed **700+ cloud transformation engagements** and won the **Lloyds Banking Group** digital transformation contract — one of the largest deals in Infosys's history.
- **2022**: Infosys partnered with **Microsoft** on generative AI services, **Google Cloud** on data analytics, and **AWS** on enterprise cloud. The company positioned itself as a neutral integrator for all major cloud platforms.
- **2023**: Infosys launched **Infosys Topaz** — an AI-first platform combining generative AI, data analytics, and automation to accelerate enterprise transformation. The platform was built using proprietary models and integrations with major foundation model providers.
- **2023**: Infosys won a **$1.5 billion 5-year deal from an existing European financial services client** — demonstrating the stickiness of long-term client relationships in IT services.
- **2024**: Infosys reported **FY2024 revenue of $18.6 billion** with net profit of approximately $3 billion. The company employed approximately 343,000 people worldwide across offices in 56 countries.
- **2025**: Infosys continued to focus on its AI-first strategy, with generative AI representing an increasing share of its revenue. The company also faced macro headwinds in BFSI (banking, financial services, insurance) spending but offset this with strong growth in manufacturing, retail, and energy sectors.

## Key Products and Business Segments

### Digital Services
- **Infosys Topaz** — AI-first platform using generative AI and data analytics to accelerate enterprise transformation
- **Infosys Cobalt** — cloud frameworks and assets for hybrid and multi-cloud enterprise environments
- **Edge Delta** — AI-powered observability platform for enterprise IT operations

### Industry Solutions
- **Finacle** — core banking platform used by banks in 100+ countries; one of the world's most deployed banking solutions
- **Banking Platform Solutions** — digital banking, payments, and wealth management platforms
- **Insurance Services** — life and general insurance software and BPM

### Consulting and Technology
- **Enterprise Applications** — SAP, Oracle, Salesforce, and ServiceNow implementations
- **Data and Analytics** — data warehousing, business intelligence, and advanced analytics
- **Cloud and Infrastructure** — migration, transformation, and managed services

## Corporate Governance

- **Chairman**: Nandan Nilekani (since 2017; co-CEO of Infosys 2002-2006, former chairman of UIDAI, India's Aadhaar identity program)
- **CEO**: Salil Parekh (since 2018)
- **CFO**: Nilanjan Roy (since 2019)
- **Global headcount**: ~343,000 employees (2024)
- **Headquarters**: Bangalore, Karnataka, India
- Notable for strong governance practices — Narayana Murthy's founding principles emphasized transparency, which shaped Infosys's board culture.

## Financial Highlights

- **Revenue (FY2024)**: $18.6 billion
- **Net Profit (FY2024)**: ~$3.0 billion (16% margin)
- **Market Cap (early 2025)**: ~$90 billion
- **Operating Margin**: ~20-23%
- **Employees in India**: ~80% of workforce based in India
- **Key clients**: Major banks (HSBC, Barclays, UBS), airlines (Qantas, Lufthansa), and global retailers (Walmart)
- Zero debt on balance sheet (since 2020)
