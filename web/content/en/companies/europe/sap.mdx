# SAP

SAP SE (FWB: SAP; NYSE: SAP) is a German multinational software corporation headquartered in Walldorf, Baden-Württemberg, Germany. As the world's largest enterprise software company, SAP provides a comprehensive suite of enterprise resource planning (ERP) software, applications, and services to organizations across every industry. Founded in 1972 by five former IBM engineers, SAP's products power the operations of more than 425,000 customers in 180 countries, including 99% of the world's 5,000 largest companies. With approximately 110,650 employees globally (2025), €36.8 billion in annual revenue (2025), and a market capitalization exceeding €300 billion, SAP is Europe's largest technology company by both revenue and market value. Its flagship ERP system — originally SAP R/3 and now SAP S/4HANA — forms the digital backbone of global commerce, managing finance, supply chains, human resources, and operations for enterprises from SMBs to governments and multinational corporations.

## Timeline

### 1972–1985: Founding and Early Product Development

- **1972**: SAP founded on **June 1, 1972** in Mannheim, Germany, by five former IBM engineers: **Dietmar Hopp**, **Hans-Werner Hector**, **Hasso Plattner**, **Klaus Tschira**, and **Claus Wellenreuther**. The company name originally stood for **"Systemanalyse und Programmentwicklung"** (System Analysis and Program Development) before the full name was formalized as **SAP AG** in 1981. The founders quit IBM together, believing that standard business software — rather than custom-built systems — would revolutionize enterprise computing.
- **1973**: SAP released its first commercial product, **SAP R/1** (Release 1), containing a **RF (Rechnungswesen und Finanzen)** financial accounting system. The system was built for IBM's mainframe architecture. First customer: **Imperial Chemical Industries (ICI)** in the UK.
- **1976**: SAP moved to new headquarters in Walldorf, Germany — a site that remains SAP's global headquarters today. The Walldorf campus was designed as a "software factory" and became a symbol of German engineering excellence.
- **1979**: SAP launched **SAP R/2** — a major expansion beyond financial accounting to include **materials management (MM)** and **production planning (PP)** modules. R/2 ran on mainframe computers and established SAP's reputation for integrated, module-based enterprise software. By 1980, SAP had grown to 100 employees.

### 1986–1999: Growth and R/3 Revolution

- **1988**: SAP went public on the **Frankfurt Stock Exchange** (FWB: SAP) — one of Germany's largest tech IPOs at the time. This provided capital for international expansion.
- **1990**: SAP acquired UK software company **Baan** — a major competitor in ERP. This acquisition was part of SAP's strategy to expand beyond German-speaking markets. SAP had grown to 3,500 employees by 1990.
- **1992**: SAP launched **SAP R/3** — the product that would define enterprise software for the next two decades. R/3 introduced a **three-tier client-server architecture** (presentation, application, database layers), which was revolutionary compared to R/2's mainframe-centric design. R/3 was available on multiple database platforms (Oracle, IBM DB2, Informix) and operating systems — a major competitive advantage.
- **1993**: SAP established offices in the United States (Walldorf, Germany → US expansion). R/3's success drove explosive growth: revenue grew from €307 million in 1992 to €1.2 billion by 1996.
- **1995**: SAP established **SAP Labs** in Palo Alto, California — signaling its ambition to compete directly with Oracle and Microsoft in the US market. SAP launched **mySAP.com** portal framework — an early attempt at web-based enterprise collaboration.
- **1996**: SAP reached **10,000 employees** globally. The R/3 system had become the de facto standard for large enterprise ERP in Europe, with growing adoption in North America and Asia.
- **1998**: SAP opened development centers in **India** (Bangalore) and **China** (Shanghai) — both critical for accessing engineering talent and serving emerging markets.
- **1999**: SAP launched **SAP R/3 4.5** with **Internet integration** — responding to the dot-com era. Hasso Plattner, one of the founders, took a hands-on role as CEO during this period to steer the company's internet transformation. Revenue reached €5.1 billion.

### 2000–2010: Globalization and Platform Strategy

- **2000**: SAP launched **mySAP ERP** — a comprehensive update to R/3 built for the internet era. SAP began repositioning from "ERP vendor" to "enterprise software platform" with modules for CRM, SCM, and SRM.
- **2001**: SAP listed on the **New York Stock Exchange** (NYSE: SAP) — joining the Dow Jones Euro Stoxx 50. The NYSE listing was critical for accessing US capital markets and strengthening brand credibility against American competitors.
- **2002**: SAP acquired **TopTier Software** for $400 million — the first of many acquisitions in the 2000s. TopTier's portal software became the foundation of **SAP Enterprise Portal**.
- **2003**: SAP's total customer base exceeded **12,000 companies in 50 countries**. The company launched **SAP NetWeaver** — a technical foundation (middleware/integration platform) that enabled SAP and non-SAP systems to work together. NetWeaver was SAP's answer to IBM's WebSphere and Microsoft's .NET.
- **2004**: SAP launched **SAP ERP Central Component (ECC) 5.0** — the successor to R/3, modernizing the architecture while maintaining compatibility with existing customer installations.
- **2005**: SAP acquired **MaxDB** database technology and began offering **SAP DB** (later SAP Sybase ASE) as part of its database strategy to compete with Oracle.
- **2006**: SAP launched **SAP ERP 6.0** (ECC 6.0) — one of the longest-serving ERP releases in enterprise software history. ECC 6.0 remained the dominant SAP release for 16+ years until S/4HANA migration. SAP reached **35,000 employees** and €10 billion in annual revenue.
- **2007**: SAP acquired **Business Objects** for **$6.8 billion** — the largest acquisition in SAP's history at the time. Business Objects was a leader in business intelligence (BI) and reporting software, adding a critical data analytics layer to SAP's portfolio.
- **2008**: SAP acquired **Sybase** for **$5.8 billion** — gaining a mobile enterprise platform and database technology. This acquisition helped SAP compete in mobile enterprise apps and expand its database offerings.
- **2009**: SAP appointed **Jim Hagemann Snabe** and **Bill McDermott** as co-CEOs — a two-person leadership model following Hasso Plattner's departure as CEO. McDermott, an American, brought aggressive sales and marketing experience to the German company.
- **2010**: SAP's revenue exceeded **€12.8 billion**. The company served **76,000+ customers** in 120 countries. SAP announced **HANA** (High-Performance Analytic Appliance) — an in-memory database platform that would become one of the most important products in SAP's history.

### 2011–2018: Cloud Transformation and HANA

- **2011**: SAP launched **SAP HANA** — a revolutionary **in-memory relational database** that dramatically accelerated transaction processing and analytics. HANA could process massive datasets in RAM, enabling real-time analytics that were previously impossible with disk-based databases. SAP positioned HANA as the foundation for all future SAP applications. Revenue: €13.9 billion.
- **2012**: SAP acquired **Ariba** for **$4.3 billion** — a cloud-based **procurement and supply chain management** network. Ariba connected buyers and suppliers globally and became the backbone of SAP's cloud-based business network. This acquisition marked SAP's formal pivot to cloud computing.
- **2013**: SAP launched **SAP S/4HANA** — the next-generation ERP suite built entirely on HANA in-memory database. S/4HANA was a complete re-architecture of SAP's core ERP, replacing the decades-old R/3/ECC data model with a simplified, real-time platform. First customers: 200 early adopters in manufacturing and retail.
- **2014**: SAP acquired **Concur Technologies** for **$8.3 billion** — the largest acquisition in SAP history. Concur provided **cloud-based travel and expense management software**, adding a major SaaS platform to SAP's portfolio. SAP also acquired **Fieldglass** (contingent workforce management) and **SuccessFactors** (HR cloud) — collectively signaling a full commitment to cloud-first strategy.
- **2015**: Bill McDermott became **sole CEO** (Jim Hagemann Snabe stepped down). SAP's cloud revenue grew 87% year-over-year, driven by SuccessFactors, Concur, and Ariba. SAP had 74,000+ employees in 130 countries.
- **2016**: SAP launched **SAP Leonardo** — a digital innovation system combining IoT, machine learning, blockchain, and analytics on the HANA cloud platform. SAP positioned Leonardo as its answer to digital transformation trends.
- **2017**: SAP revenue reached **€23.4 billion**. Cloud revenue exceeded **€3.5 billion** (37% growth). SAP's customer base included **425,000 customers** in 180 countries. SAP announced **Qualtrics** partnership — an early step toward experience management.
- **2018**: SAP acquired **CallidusCloud** for **$2.4 billion** — strengthening its customer experience and sales performance management suite. SAP also acquired **Gigya** (customer identity management) and **C/4HANA** (customer cloud suite) to compete directly with Salesforce in CRM. McDermott resigned in October 2018. Revenue: €24.7 billion.

### 2019–2022: New Leadership and S/4HANA Migration

- **2019**: SAP appointed **Jennifer Morgan** and **Christian Klein** as **co-CEOs** in October 2019. Klein, a longtime SAP executive who had joined in 1999, represented the engineering and product side of the company. Morgan, an American from SAP's US operations, was SAP's first female CEO.
- **2020**: In **April 2020**, Jennifer Morgan departed after only 6 months as co-CEO, citing the need for "single-threaded leadership" during the COVID-19 pandemic. **Christian Klein** became **sole CEO** — a role he has held since. SAP navigated the pandemic by accelerating its cloud transition. Revenue: €27.3 billion.
- **2020**: SAP completed acquisition of **Qualtrics** for **$8.1 billion** — a landmark deal bringing experience management (XM) data into the SAP ecosystem. Qualtrics' SurveyMonkey-competitor platform captured employee experience, customer experience, and product experience data. This acquisition proved transformative: Qualtrics grew to 18,000+ customers and became a standalone publicly traded company (XM) in 2021.
- **2021**: SAP launched **RISE with SAP** — a comprehensive **cloud ERP migration program** offering customers a single contract to move their SAP ERP to S/4HANA in the cloud (partnering with hyperscalers Microsoft Azure, Amazon Web Services, Google Cloud, and Alibaba Cloud). RISE was positioned as the primary mechanism to drive S/4HANA adoption. Revenue: €29.3 billion.
- **2021**: SAP and **Dediq GmbH** formed **SAP Fioneer** — a joint venture focused on financial services digitalization. This strategic partnership enabled faster innovation in banking and insurance verticals.
- **2022**: SAP revenue reached **€30.8 billion** despite a **non-recurring charge of €1.6 billion** related to the Qualtrics integration and post-pandemic customer migration challenges. SAP's backlog grew to €36 billion. Christian Klein's leadership stabilized the company after multiple leadership transitions.

### 2023–2025: AI Integration and Sustained Growth

- **2023**: SAP completed its **S/4HANA migration wave** — accelerating customers from legacy ECC 6.0 to S/4HANA cloud. SAP announced **€2.5 billion investment in AI** via its **SAP Business AI** initiative, embedding generative AI capabilities across the entire portfolio. The company also launched **Joule** — a natural language AI assistant for SAP users, similar to Microsoft Copilot. Revenue: **€31.2 billion** with cloud revenue reaching €13.7 billion.
- **2024**: SAP's cloud business grew **24%** to €16.9 billion, now representing **54% of total revenue**. SAP reached **300+ S/4HANA customers** with over 20,000 customers on the public cloud version. SAP expanded its **partnership with Microsoft** — integrating Joule AI with Microsoft 365 and Copilot. SAP's market cap surpassed €300 billion.
- **2025**: SAP revenue reached **€36.8 billion** with **cloud revenue of €20.5 billion** (27% of total revenue growing 22%). Operating income: €7.3 billion. Net income: **€7.3 billion**. Total assets: €70.4 billion. SAP completed acquisition of **SmartRecruiters** (talent acquisition platform). SAP announced **HANA Cloud** expansion with deeper integration into Microsoft Fabric and AWS. The company had **110,650 employees** in 180 countries. SAP served **98 of the world's 100 largest companies**.

## Key Products

### SAP S/4HANA
- **SAP S/4HANA** — the next-generation ERP suite built on HANA in-memory database, available in cloud (public/private) and on-premise editions. Replaced the legacy SAP ECC 6.0. Covers finance (FI/CO), logistics (MM/SD/PP), human resources (HCM), and project management (PS). Over 20,000 customers on public cloud version (2025).

### SAP HANA
- **SAP HANA** — in-memory relational database and platform. Enables real-time analytics, transactional processing on a single platform, and native AI integration. Offered as cloud service (HANA Cloud) or appliance. The foundation of SAP's entire modern product stack.

### Cloud Applications (SaaS)
- **SAP SuccessFactors** — cloud HR and human capital management (HCM). Covers payroll, talent management, employee experience. Serves 10,000+ customers.
- **SAP Concur** — cloud travel and expense management. Processes $100B+ in travel spend annually. Dominant in mid-large enterprise T&E.
- **SAP Ariba** — cloud procurement and supplier management network. Connects 4.4M+ companies globally.
- **SAP Qualtrics** — experience management (XM) platform. Employee experience, customer experience, brand experience. Acquired for $8.1B in 2020, spun off as XM in 2021.

### Business Technology Platform
- **SAP Business Technology Platform (BTP)** — integration, extensibility, and development platform. Enables customers to build custom applications on SAP infrastructure.
- **SAP Analytics Cloud** — BI, planning, and predictive analytics in the cloud.
