# Nokia

Nokia Corporation (HEX: NOKIA, NYSE: NOK) is a Finnish multinational telecommunications and information technology company headquartered in Espoo, Finland. Founded in 1865 as a single pulp mill business on the Tammerkoski River in southern Finland, Nokia has undergone a remarkable transformation across more than 160 years — from a paper mill to a diversified industrial conglomerate, and then to the world's dominant mobile phone maker at its peak in 2007, before suffering a catastrophic fall from which it never recovered in phones, and subsequently rebuilding as a global telecommunications equipment company. At its peak in 2007, Nokia held approximately **40% of the global mobile phone market** — a dominance that evaporated within five years following the introduction of the Apple iPhone in 2007 and the subsequent rise of Google's Android operating system. Today's Nokia is a major player in **5G network infrastructure**, competing with Ericsson and Huawei to supply hardware and software to global mobile network operators. Nokia's extraordinary arc from a 19th-century pulp mill to the world's largest mobile phone maker to a 21st-century telecommunications equipment company is one of the most dramatic corporate transformations in business history.

## Timeline

### 1865–1915: The Pulp Mill Origins

- **1865 (May)**: Nokia was founded as **Nokia AB** by **Fredrik Idestam** — a mining engineer who had discovered a large deposit of hydropower near the town of Nokia (named after the local Nokia AB pulp mill) on the Tammerkoski River in southern Finland. Idestam established a pulp mill to produce paper from wood pulp. The business was profitable from the start, benefiting from Finland's abundant forests and hydroelectric power.
- **1871**: Idestam brought in his friend **Leopold Machelys** as a partner, and the company was formally incorporated as **Nokia AB**.
- **1898**: The **Finnish Cable Factory** was founded in Helsinki — later to become part of the Nokia group.
- **1902**: Nokia began producing electricity — generating power from the Tammerkoski river rapids.

### 1915–1960: Diversification and the Industrial Conglomerate Era

- **1910s–1920s**: Nokia expanded into multiple industries — rubber boots, tires, cables, and electricity generation. The company became one of Finland's largest industrial conglomerates.
- **1922**: Nokia merged with the **Finnish Cable Factory** and **Finnish Rubber Works** (which made rubber boots and tires). This created the modern Nokia corporate structure — a multi-industry conglomerate.
- **1960s**: Nokia established its **Electronics Division** — the beginning of Nokia's future as a telecommunications company. Nokia recognized early that electronics would be a growth industry.
- **1969**: Nokia introduced the **数字电话交换机** (digital telephone exchange) — an early entry into telecommunications hardware.

### 1970–1992: Telecommunications Pivot and the Jorma Ollila Era

- **1977**: **Jorma Ollila** (then 28) joined Nokia's finance department. He would become Nokia's most transformative leader, serving as CEO from 1992 to 2006.
- **1979**: Nokia formed a joint venture with **Salora** — a Finnish electronics company — to develop mobile phones. This partnership, called **Mobira**, would become Nokia's entry into cellular telecommunications.
- **1981**: Nokia launched the **Mobira Senator** — the first fully automatic car phone, operating on the Nordic Mobile Telephone (NMT) network. This was one of the world's first car phones.
- **1982**: Nokia introduced the **Mobira Talkman** — a portable (though still large) mobile phone.
- **1984**: Nokia acquired **Salora** and the remaining stake in **Mobira**, giving it full control over the mobile phone joint venture.
- **1987**: Nokia introduced the **Mobira Senator** — one of the first truly portable car phones.
- **1989**: Nokia introduced its first handheld mobile phone, the **Mobira Cityman** — weighing 760 grams (nearly 1.7 pounds).
- **1991**: Finland became the first country in the world to launch a commercial **GSM** (2G) mobile network — this was a pivotal moment for Nokia, which had heavily invested in GSM technology. The GSM standard (now used globally) gave Nokia a significant advantage in the emerging global mobile phone market.
- **1992**: **Jorma Ollila** became Nokia's CEO. He faced a company in crisis — Nokia was losing money across its multiple divisions (rubber, cables, electronics). Ollila made the pivotal decision to **focus Nokia entirely on telecommunications** and began divesting non-core businesses. He later said this was the most important strategic decision of his career.

### 1993–1999: The Rise to Global Dominance

- **1994**: Nokia introduced the **Nokia 2110** — its first mass-market mobile phone and the first to feature the iconic Nokia ringtone.
- **1995**: Nokia's mobile phone business became profitable enough to fund rapid expansion.
- **1996**: Nokia launched the **Nokia 9000 Communicator** — a revolutionary device that combined a mobile phone with a full QWERTY keyboard and early smartphone features. It was the world's first PDA-phone combination and cost $800 — but it established Nokia as an innovator.
- **1997**: Nokia launched the **Nokia 6110** — one of its most popular phones of the era, selling millions of units.
- **1998**: Nokia overtook Motorola to become the **world's largest mobile phone maker** — a position it would hold for the next decade.
- **1999**: Nokia introduced the **Nokia 3210** — one of the most successful phones ever made, selling over 160 million units. It was known for its durability and the ability to personalize covers.

### 1999–2007: Peak and the First Signs of Trouble

- **2000**: Nokia introduced the **Nokia 3310** — arguably the most iconic mobile phone ever made. Over **126 million units** were sold, making it the best-selling phone in Nokia's history and one of the best-selling consumer electronics devices ever. The 3310 was famous for its durability — surviving drops and extreme conditions.
- **2001**: Nokia launched the **Nokia 5510** — a phone with a full keyboard and built-in FM radio.
- **2002**: Nokia launched the **Nokia 1100** — a basic, ultra-cheap phone designed for emerging markets. It became the best-selling phone in history at that point, with over **200 million units** sold. Nokia excelled at creating affordable phones for global mass markets.
- **2002**: Nokia introduced the **Nokia 7650** — its first camera phone (with a 0.3MP camera), launching the camera phone revolution.
- **2003**: Nokia launched the **Nokia 1100** (redesign) and introduced the **Nokia 3300** — a music-focused phone.
- **2004**: Nokia's market share reached approximately **30%** globally.
- **2005**: Nokia launched the **Nokia Nseries** — a line of advanced smartphones featuring powerful cameras, music players, and internet capabilities. The **Nokia N95** (2007) was considered the most advanced smartphone of its time — with a 5MP camera, GPS, Wi-Fi, and symbian OS.
- **2006**: Nokia launched the **Nokia 5300** — a popular music phone.
- **2006**: Nokia acquired **NAVTEQ** (for $8.1 billion) — a digital map company that became the foundation of Nokia's HERE maps service.

### 2007: The Year Everything Changed

- **2007 (June 29)**: **Apple launched the iPhone** — a touchscreen smartphone that immediately made Nokia's existing product line look obsolete. The iPhone's full touchscreen, elegant user interface, and seamless integration with iTunes/App Store was a fundamental reimagining of what a phone could be. Nokia's reaction was initially dismissive — a critical mistake.
- **2007**: Nokia's mobile phone market share reached approximately **40%** — its all-time peak. Despite the iPhone's launch, Nokia's sales actually *increased* in 2007, lulling Nokia into a false sense of security.
- **2007**: Nokia launched the **Nokia N95** — widely considered the best feature phone of its era, but it was running the aging **Symbian operating system** which was far inferior to Apple's iOS in terms of user experience, app ecosystem, and developer tools.

### 2008–2011: The Collapse

- **2008**: Nokia's market share began to decline as Android smartphones (led by Samsung, HTC, and Motorola) flooded the market. Google's **Android** operating system was free and open — allowing any manufacturer to build smartphones at various price points.
- **2008**: Nokia launched the **Nokia 5800 XpressMusic** — its first touchscreen phone, running Symbian. It was a commercial failure compared to the iPhone.
- **2008**: Nokia's market share in smartphones began to collapse. The company was producing phones at 50 different price points but couldn't compete on user experience.
- **2009**: Nokia's profits collapsed — the company posted significant losses for the first time in over a decade.
- **2010**: Nokia's new CEO **Stephen Elop** (a Canadian, the first non-Finnish CEO in Nokia's history) announced a strategic partnership with **Microsoft** — Nokia would adopt **Windows Phone** as its primary smartphone operating system, replacing Symbian. This was announced in February 2011 — a decision later called the "burning platform" memo by Elop, who described Nokia as standing on a burning oil platform.
- **2011**: Nokia and Microsoft formally announced their partnership. Nokia committed to using Windows Phone exclusively — a decision that eliminated Nokia's existing investments in the Meego operating system (which had produced the Nokia N9, critically acclaimed but now discontinued).
- **2011**: Nokia's market share in smartphones fell below **15%**. The company was burning cash at an alarming rate.
- **2011**: Nokia released its last major Symbian phone, the **Nokia 808 PureView** — featuring an extraordinary 41MP camera that remains unmatched to this day.

### 2012–2014: Decline and the Microsoft Acquisition

- **2012**: Nokia introduced the **Lumia 920** — the first Windows Phone 8 device, with wireless charging and a PureView camera. Critics loved the hardware but Windows Phone's app ecosystem remained weak.
- **2012**: Nokia's mobile phone market share fell to approximately **3%** in the smartphone segment. The company was in freefall.
- **2013 (September 3)**: Nokia officially **sold its mobile phone business to Microsoft** for **$7.2 billion** — one of the most dramatic corporate collapses in technology history. Nokia had once been the world's largest mobile phone maker; now it sold its entire phones division for less than the value of a single mid-sized startup acquisition. The deal included the Lumia smartphone brand, the Asha feature phone line, and a 10-year license to use the Nokia brand on mobile phones.
- **2013 (December)**: As part of the Microsoft deal, Nokia's headquarters building in Espoo (the "Nokia House") was sold to Microsoft for €200 million.
- **2014**: Nokia, now focused solely on telecommunications equipment, introduced the **Nokia N1** — an Android tablet manufactured by Foxconn. This was the first Nokia-branded Android device.

### 2015–2019: Rebuilding as a Telecommunications Equipment Company

- **2015 (April)**: Nokia sold its **HERE** digital maps business to a consortium of German automotive companies (Audi, BMW, and Daimler) for **$3.07 billion** — a significant profit over the $8.1B Nokia had paid for NAVTEQ in 2006.
- **2015**: Nokia's CEO **Rajeev Suri** led the company's transformation into a pure-play telecommunications equipment company.
- **2016 (November)**: Nokia completed its acquisition of **Alcatel-Lucent** for **€15.6 billion** — a transformative deal that made Nokia a major player in telecommunications network infrastructure. The combined company had significant strength in 2G, 3G, 4G/LTE, and emerging 5G technologies. Alcatel-Lucent's Bell Labs (the legendary research organization that had invented the laser, the transistor, and numerous other foundational technologies) became part of Nokia.
- **2017**: Nokia began deploying its first 5G network equipment — positioning itself as a leader in the 5G era.
- **2019**: Nokia's CEO Rajeev Suri was replaced by **Pekka Lundmark** — who had previously led Nokia's network equipment business before becoming CFO.

### 2020–2025: 5G Era and the New Nokia

- **2020**: Nokia played a key role in **5G network deployments** globally — competing with Ericsson and Huawei to supply base stations, radio access network (RAN) equipment, and core network software to mobile operators.
- **2020**: Nokia was forced to withdraw from the **Indian market** due to geopolitical and competitive pressures — a significant setback.
- **2020**: The US government placed restrictions on **Huawei** (banning it from US 5G networks), which created opportunities for Nokia and Ericsson in Western markets.
- **2021**: Nokia launched its ** refreshed network equipment portfolio** for 5G standalone (SA) networks.
- **2022**: Nokia benefited from the **US government push to remove Huawei equipment** from networks worldwide (the "rip and replace" program), creating new contract opportunities.
- **2022**: Nokia introduced its **5G AirScale** portfolio — a leading radio access network (RAN) platform for 5G networks globally.
- **2023**: Nokia won significant 5G contracts in **India** (with Bharti Airtel and Reliance Jio) and expanded its presence in North American and European 5G deployments.
- **2023**: Nokia launched its **"Network as a Service" (NaaS)** model — offering network capabilities as a service to enterprises.
- **2023**: Nokia reported **€22.2 billion** in revenue — with the majority coming from network infrastructure (approximately 80%) and the remainder from enterprise and licensing.
- **2024**: Nokia continued to be one of the **three dominant players** in global 5G network equipment (alongside Ericsson and Huawei), with particular strength in 5G RAN, optical networks, and cloud-native core networks.
- **2024**: Nokia's market cap was approximately **€20-22 billion** — far below its 2000 peak.

## Key Business Segments

### Network Infrastructure (largest segment, ~80% of revenue)
- **5G Radio Access Network (RAN)** — base stations and radio equipment for 5G networks (AirScale portfolio)
- **Optical Networks** — fiber optic transmission equipment
- **Core Networks** — mobile core, packet core, voice core
- **Cloud-native software** — network functions virtualization (NFV)
- **IP Networks** — routers and switches for service providers

### Enterprise and Licensing
- **Nokia Enterprise** — private wireless networks, industrial IoT, campus networks
- **Nokia Bell Labs** — research and consulting; licenses foundational patents
- **Nokia Technologies** — licensing the vast patent portfolio (approximately 20,000+ patent families)

### Key Customers
- Mobile network operators worldwide: AT&T, Verizon, T-Mobile, Deutsche Telekom, BT, Orange, Bharti Airtel, Reliance Jio, and others

## CEO and Leadership

- **President & CEO**: **Pekka Lundmark** (since 2020). He joined Nokia in 1990 and previously led Nokia's network equipment business before becoming CFO.
- **Previous CEOs**: Jorma Ollila (1992–2006), Olli-Pekka Kallasvuo (2006–2010), Stephen Elop (2010–2014, Microsoft deal era), Rajeev Suri (2014–2019)
- Nokia's history is deeply tied to Finland — it was one of the most important companies in Finnish economic history, accounting for a significant percentage of Finland's GDP and exports at its peak

## Corporate Structure

- **Headquarters**: Espoo, Finland
- **Founded**: 1865 (Nokia AB pulp mill)
- **HEX: NOKIA** on Helsinki Stock Exchange; **NOK** on NYSE (ADRs)
- The current Nokia corporation was formed from the merger of Nokia, Finnish Cable Factory, and Finnish Rubber Works in 1966
- The current network equipment company traces its lineage through Nokia's telecommunications equipment businesses and Alcatel-Lucent (acquired 2016)
