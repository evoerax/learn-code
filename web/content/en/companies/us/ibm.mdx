# IBM

International Business Machines Corporation (IBM, NYSE: IBM) is an American multinational technology and consulting company headquartered in Armonk, New York. Founded in 1911 as the Computing-Tabulating-Recording Company (C-T-R), it was renamed International Business Machines (IBM) in 1924. Known colloquially as "Big Blue" — a nickname derived from the blue color of its early computer hardware — IBM has been a defining force in computing for over a century, pioneering tabulation machines, mainframes, personal computers, and enterprise AI. IBM has operated under seven CEOs across its history and remains one of the world's largest technology companies by revenue, with $63.4 billion in 2024 revenue and approximately 130,000 employees worldwide.

## Timeline

### 1911–1924: Founding as Computing-Tabulating-Recording Company

- **1911**: The **Computing-Tabulating-Recording Company (CTR)** was formed through the merger of four companies: the Tabulating Machine Company (founded 1889 by Herman Hollerith, who invented the punch card tabulator), the International Time Recording Company, the Bundy Manufacturing Company, and the American Record Company. CTR was a holding company for a portfolio of office-equipment businesses.
- **1914**: **Thomas J. Watson** (then 40) joined CTR as general manager. He previously worked at NCR (National Cash Register) where he was a star salesman. Watson became president in 1916 and transformed the company's culture — famously instituting the **"THINK"** motto (reportedly written on a card after Watson asked an employee to "think" about a problem for a week, and the employee replied he had been thinking for a week and could only think of that one word).
- **1924**: CTR was renamed **International Business Machines (IBM)** — Watson believed the new name was more memorable and reflected global ambitions.

### 1925–1950: Punch Cards and the Watson Era

- **1925**: IBM dominated the punch card market, which was the dominant data processing technology before electronic computers. Punch card machines could read, sort, and tabulate data for business and government applications.
- **1930s**: IBM was the dominant force in office automation. The **IBM 80-column punch card** became an industry standard — it was so ubiquitous that the phrase "get your card punched" entered popular language as a euphemism for being accepted or approved.
- **1936**: IBM opened its first factory in San Jose, California — the beginning of IBM's West Coast presence.
- **1940s**: IBM manufactured machines for the US War Department. The company's scale and manufacturing expertise positioned it perfectly for the coming electronic computer era.
- **1944**: IBM built the **Harvard Mark I** (officially the IBM Automatic Sequence Controlled Calculator) — a large electromechanical computer developed in partnership with Harvard University. It was 51 feet long and used 72 mechanical storage registers.

### 1950–1965: Electronic Computing and the System/360 Revolution

- **1952**: IBM introduced its first electronic computer, the **IBM 701** — the company's first commercially produced electronic computer. This marked IBM's transition from electromechanical to electronic computing.
- **1953**: **IBM 704** — introduced random-access memory (magnetic core memory), a significant advancement.
- **1956**: Thomas Watson Jr. (son of the founder) became president. He led IBM through the critical transition to transistorized computers.
- **1957**: IBM introduced the **IBM 608** — the first commercial computer to use transistors instead of vacuum tubes. This was a landmark achievement that gave IBM a decisive technological lead.
- **1961**: IBM began the **System/360** project — the most ambitious computer development program ever undertaken to that point. The development cost was **$5 billion** (equivalent to ~$50 billion today), making it the largest civilian commercial development project in history.
- **1964 (April 7)**: IBM launched the **IBM System/360** — a family of six compatible computers (models 30 through 75) that shared the same instruction set architecture. This was revolutionary: for the first time, businesses could upgrade their computers without rewriting software. The 360 established the concept of **compatible computing** that defined the industry. The launch was one of the most significant moments in computing history.
- **1966–1969**: IBM's revenue doubled from $3.3B to $6.7B, driven by the System/360's commercial success.

### 1970–1985: PC Revolution and Antitrust

- **1975**: IBM introduced the **IBM 5100** — a portable computer weighing 55 pounds, available 5 years before the IBM PC.
- **1981 (August 12)**: IBM launched the **IBM Personal Computer (IBM PC, Model 5150)** — priced at $1,565 (~$5,200 today). It used an Intel 8088 processor, 16KB RAM (expandable to 256KB), and Microsoft's MS-DOS operating system. The IBM PC became the **de facto standard** for personal computing — the open architecture (with published specifications) meant third-party manufacturers could build compatible clones. This inadvertently created the PC clone industry (Compaq, Dell, etc.) and ultimately displaced IBM's own proprietary mini-computer business.
- **1982**: IBM was indicted by the US government for antitrust violations related to its dominance in the computer market. The case was eventually dropped, but it consumed enormous management attention throughout the 1980s.
- **1983**: IBM launched the **IBM PC XT** (with hard disk) and the **IBM PCjr** (a consumer-oriented machine that was a commercial failure).
- **1986**: IBM introduced **OS/2** (with Microsoft) as a replacement for MS-DOS, but it never achieved mass adoption.

### 1985–1993: Crisis Years and the Death of the Mainframe Era

- **1987**: IBM suffered its first annual loss in 1985 — $1.1 billion (the largest corporate loss in US history at the time). The company had grown complacent, bureaucratic, and unable to respond to the personal computer revolution it had itself ignited.
- **1990**: IBM posted $6B in losses and began massive layoffs — cutting 60,000 jobs over several years.
- **1991**: Lou Gerstner, a former McKinsey partner and CEO of RJR Nabisco, was hired as IBM's new chairman and CEO. He was the first external (non-IBM) CEO in company history.
- **1993**: Gerstner saved IBM from potential breakup by convincing the board that IBM's unique value was its **integrated hardware + software + services** model, and that maintaining the company as a single entity was essential. He ended talk of splitting IBM into separate companies. IBM's survival through this period was a significant achievement given the scale of the crisis.

### 1994–2002: Gerstner's Turnaround and the Internet Era

- **1994**: IBM returned to profitability. Gerstner began repositioning IBM from a hardware company to a **services and software** company.
- **1995**: IBM acquired **Lotus Development Corporation** (makers of Lotus Notes) for $3.5B — a major move into collaborative software. IBM also acquired Tivoli Systems (storage management software).
- **1996**: IBM introduced **Deep Blue** — a chess-playing computer. In 1997, Deep Blue famously defeated world chess champion **Garry Kasparov** in a six-game match — a landmark in AI history and a powerful symbol of IBM's computing capabilities.
- **1999**: IBM launched **e-business** branding campaign — positioning IBM as the integrator for the emerging internet economy.
- **2000**: IBM became the world's largest technology services company by revenue.
- **2002**: Sam Palmisano succeeded Lou Gerstner as CEO. IBM acquired **Rational Software** ($2.1B) — a software company specializing in development tools — strengthening IBM's enterprise software portfolio.

### 2003–2012: Smarter Planet and Watson

- **2002**: IBM sold its **PC business to Lenovo** (a Chinese company) for $1.75 billion — Lenovo received the ThinkPad brand and all IBM PC operations worldwide. IBM retained a stake in Lenovo. This divestiture marked IBM's definitive exit from consumer hardware and full pivot to enterprise.
- **2005**: IBM began transforming into a **global services integrator** — winning massive IT outsourcing contracts from governments and corporations worldwide.
- **2008**: IBM launched the **"Smarter Planet"** initiative — positioning IBM as the technology partner for cities, healthcare systems, supply chains, and energy grids being made intelligent through data analytics.
- **2009**: IBM acquired **SPSS Inc.** ($1.2B) — statistical analysis software used by researchers and enterprises.
- **2011 (February)**: IBM's **Watson** computer competed on the American TV show **Jeopardy!** against former champions Ken Jennings and Brad Rutter. Watson won decisively — answering questions in natural language by analyzing massive amounts of data in seconds. The Watson victory was a landmark in artificial intelligence, demonstrating that machines could understand and process human language at a high level.
- **2011**: IBM announced it would invest $100 billion over the next decade in semiconductor research — later producing the world's fastest supercomputers.

### 2013–2020: Cloud, AI, and Leadership Transitions

- **2012**: Virginia "Ginni" Rometty became IBM's first female CEO (succeeded Sam Palmisano). She led IBM through a period of strategic transformation toward cloud and AI.
- **2013**: IBM acquired **SoftLayer Technologies** ($2B) — a cloud infrastructure company that became the foundation of IBM's public cloud offering.
- **2014**: IBM opened **Watson** as a cloud-based AI platform — commercializing Watson's question-answering technology for enterprise use in healthcare, finance, and other industries.
- **2015**: IBM opened its first **IBM Watson Health** headquarters in Cambridge, Massachusetts — applying Watson to medical diagnosis and healthcare data analysis. IBM subsequently acquired **Truven Health Analytics** ($2.6B) to build the Watson Health data platform.
- **2018**: IBM acquired **Red Hat** for **$34 billion** — IBM's largest acquisition in history. Red Hat is the world's leading provider of open-source enterprise software (Linux, Red Hat Enterprise Linux, OpenShift container platform). The deal was completed in 2019. This acquisition positioned IBM as a major force in hybrid cloud — allowing enterprises to run workloads across on-premises data centers, private clouds, and multiple public clouds.
- **2019**: Ginni Rometty stepped down as CEO; **Arvind Krishna** (who had led IBM's cloud and AI strategy) became CEO.

### 2021–2024: Hybrid Cloud, AI, and the Post-Pandemic Era

- **2021 (November)**: IBM completed the **spinoff of Kyndryl** — an independent managed infrastructure services company with ~90,000 employees. Kyndryl was the result of IBM's 2020 announcement to separate its managed infrastructure services business. IBM received $19.6B in cash from the Kyndryl separation.
- **2021**: IBM acquired **TeHxE** (edge computing) and **WDG Automation** (流程自动化) to strengthen its automation and edge AI portfolio.
- **2022**: IBM's revenue was approximately **$60.6 billion**. The company had returned to modest growth after years of revenue decline, driven by cloud and AI services.
- **2023 (July)**: IBM launched **watsonx** — a next-generation AI and data platform designed for enterprises to build, tune, and deploy AI models using foundation models (large language models) and ML ops tooling. watsonx was IBM's direct response to the generative AI wave sparked by OpenAI's ChatGPT. IBM's approach emphasized **trusted, governable AI** for regulated industries like banking, healthcare, and government — contrasting with the more general-purpose approach of Microsoft/OpenAI or Google.
- **2024 (April)**: IBM acquired **HashiCorp** ($6.4B) — infrastructure-as-code software company, strengthening IBM's hybrid cloud DevOps tooling.
- **2024**: IBM and **Microsoft** announced a major strategic partnership — Microsoft would offer IBM's consulting services to Microsoft cloud customers, and IBM would adopt Microsoft Azure as a preferred cloud platform. This was significant: IBM, once a dominant computing platform, now acts as a services layer on top of cloud hyperscalers.
- **2024**: IBM CEO Arvind Krishna indicated IBM was focusing on **IT modernization** (helping enterprises migrate from legacy mainframe and middleware to cloud) as a major growth vector.
- **2024**: IBM reported **$63.4 billion** in annual revenue and approximately **$8.2 billion** in net income**.

## Key Products and Business Segments

### Hybrid Cloud (IBM Cloud)
- **IBM Cloud** — hybrid cloud platform combining private, on-premises infrastructure with public cloud (AWS, Azure, Google Cloud as partners)
- **Red Hat OpenShift** — enterprise Kubernetes container orchestration platform (the foundation of IBM's hybrid cloud strategy)
- **IBM Cloud Pak for Data** — data fabric and AI platform built on Red Hat OpenShift
- **IBM Cloud Object Storage** — enterprise object storage service
- **IBM mainframe** (IBM Z) — continues to be a significant business; IBM Z mainframes process ~$3 trillion in daily commercial transactions globally

### AI and Data (watsonx)
- **watsonx.ai** — platform for building, training, and deploying AI foundation models (Granite language models)
- **watsonx.data** — data lakehouse platform for enterprise analytics
- **watsonx.governance** — AI governance and compliance toolkit
- **IBM watson Assistant** — conversational AI for customer service
- **IBM Maximo** — AI-powered asset management and predictive maintenance

### Software
- **IBM Rational** — software engineering and DevOps tools
- **IBM WebSphere** — application server and integration software
- **IBM Db2** — database management system
- **IBM MQ** — message-oriented middleware
- **Red Hat Enterprise Linux (RHEL)** — dominant enterprise Linux distribution

### Services and Consulting
- **IBM Consulting** — global professional services (formerly IBM Global Business Services), ~130,000 consultants
- Specializes in enterprise IT modernization, cloud migration, AI implementation, and cybersecurity

### Hardware (IBM Systems)
- **IBM Z** (mainframe) — IBM's flagship mainframe computer family; runs critical banking, insurance, and government systems worldwide
- **Power Systems** — Unix servers running AIX operating system
- **Storage** — enterprise flash storage and tape storage systems

## CEO and Leadership

- **Chairman & CEO**: **Arvind Krishna** (CEO since 2020, Chairman since 2022). He joined IBM in 1990 and led the 2019 Red Hat acquisition. Prior to becoming CEO, he was IBM's Senior Vice President for Cloud and Cognitive Software.
- **Previous CEOs**: Thomas J. Watson (1914–1956), Thomas J. Watson Jr. (1956–1971), John R. Opel (1971–1986), John F. Akers (1986–1993), Lou Gerstner (1993–2002), Sam Palmisano (2002–2012), Virginia Rometty (2012–2020)
- IBM has only had 9 CEOs in its 115+ year history — remarkable stability for a company of its scale
