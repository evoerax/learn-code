# Apple

Apple Inc. (Nasdaq: AAPL) is an American technology company headquartered in Cupertino, California, founded in 1976 by **Steve Jobs**, **Steve Wozniak**, and **Ronald Wayne**. It designs, manufactures, and markets the iPhone (world's most profitable product, ~$200 billion annual revenue), the Macintosh line of personal computers, the iPad tablet, the Apple Watch, AirPods, and the Vision Pro spatial computer. Apple also operates the **iOS App Store**, **Apple TV+** streaming service, **Apple Music** (~100 million subscribers), **iCloud**, and develops the **iOS, macOS, watchOS, and visionOS** operating systems. Apple has been the world's largest company by market capitalization since 2011, reaching **$1 trillion in August 2018** (first ever), **$2 trillion in August 2020**, **$3 trillion in January 2025**, and **$4 trillion in October 2025**. Under CEO **Tim Cook** (who succeeded Steve Jobs in 2011), Apple has grown revenue from $108 billion (2011) to **$416 billion (FY2025)** — more than doubling in size. Apple's strategic advantage lies in the tight integration of hardware, software, and services (the "walled garden" ecosystem), combined with exceptional supply chain management under Cook's leadership.

## Timeline

### 1976–1980: Garage Founding and Apple II

- **April 1, 1976**: Apple Computer Company was founded in the **garage of Steve Jobs' parents' home** at 2066 Crist Drive, Los Altos, California by **Steve Jobs** (21, Reed College dropout), **Steve Wozniak** (26, Hewlett-Packard engineer), and **Ronald Wayne** (33, Atari engineer). Jobs sold his Volkswagen Bus for $1,500; Wozniak sold his HP-65 calculator for $500 — together they raised $2,000 in seed capital. The Apple I was sold as a bare circuit board with no case, keyboard, or power supply — hobbyists had to build these themselves.
- **July 1976**: The **Apple I** debuted at the **Homebrew Computer Club** in Palo Alto. Wozniak showed his prototype; the response was lukewarm. Jobs realized the commercial potential and convinced a local computer store (Byte Shop) to order 100 units — the first major computer retail order.
- **January 3, 1977**: Apple was formally incorporated as **Apple Computer, Inc.** (later shortened to Apple Inc. in 2007). **Mike Markkula** — a retired Intel and Fairchild Semiconductor engineer — invested **$250,000** and became a key early investor and chairman. He also provided business expertise and credibility.
- **April 16, 1977**: Released the **Apple II** — designed primarily by Wozniak. The Apple II was one of the first highly successful mass-produced microcomputers. It included color graphics, an audio cassette interface, and an open architecture with expansion slots. Crucially, it ran **VisiCalc** (the first spreadsheet program) — making it the "killer app" for business use. Apple II sold over 5 million units over its lifespan.
- **December 12, 1980**: Apple went **public on NASDAQ** — selling 4.6 million shares at **$22 per share**, raising over **$100 million**. This was the most successful tech IPO since Ford Motor Company in 1956. The IPO made approximately **300 Apple employees millionaires**. Steve Jobs, at 25, became a multi-millionaire.

### 1981–1985: The Mac and Jobs' Departure

- **January 1981**: Jobs joined the Macintosh division full-time. The Mac team (about 100 people) worked in a separate building, with a pirate flag motto: "It's better to be a pirate than join the Navy."
- **1983**: Released the **Apple Lisa** — Apple's first computer with a graphical user interface (GUI). At $9,995, it was too expensive for most users and was a commercial failure. However, the Lisa team contained many engineers who later worked on the Macintosh.
- **1984 (January 24)**: Released the **Apple Macintosh** — the first commercially successful personal computer with a mouse and GUI. The debut was accompanied by the legendary **"1984"** Super Bowl advertisement directed by Ridley Scott. The Mac sold well initially but tapered off after the first year due to slow performance, limited software, and the high price of **$2,495**.
- **1985**: The poor Mac sales triggered a power struggle between Jobs and CEO **John Sculley** (whom Jobs had recruited from Pepsi-Cola in 1983). On May 24, 1985, Apple's board sided with Sculley. Jobs resigned from Apple on **September 16, 1985** — taking several Apple employees (including design chief Jony Ive) to his new company: **NeXT**.
- **After Jobs' departure**: Apple struggled. Without Jobs' vision, Apple made a series of strategic errors — including opening Mac architecture to clone manufacturers (75 distinct Mac clone models by 1996), which cannibalized Apple's own hardware margins.

### 1986–1997: Decline and Near-Bankruptcy

- **1986**: Apple acquired **NeXT** (Jobs' new company) in **December 1996** for $400 million — this acquisition brought Jobs back to Apple and gave Apple a modern object-oriented operating system (NeXTSTEP/OpenStep) that became the foundation of **Mac OS X**.
- **1991**: Released **PowerBook** — Apple's first successful laptop, establishing the clamshell design language that influenced all future notebooks.
- **1993**: Apple released **Newton** — an early personal digital assistant (PDA) with touchscreen and handwriting recognition. The Newton was a commercial failure but pioneered concepts that would later appear in the iPhone.
- **1994**: Apple, IBM, and Motorola formed the **AIM alliance** to develop the PowerPC processor architecture — a RISC-based alternative to Intel's x86. The PowerPC 601 powered Macs from 1994.
- **1996**: **Gil Amelio** became CEO — he implemented deep cost cuts and began searching for a way to license the Mac OS. He also negotiated the acquisition of NeXT.
- **1997**: By mid-1997, Apple was **within 90 days of bankruptcy**. The company had not had a hit product in years; market share had collapsed to ~3%. Microsoft's Windows 95 and Intel-based PCs dominated the industry.

### 1997–2001: Jobs Returns, iMac, and iPod

- **July 1997**: Jobs staged a **boardroom coup** — the board forced out Gil Amelio, and Jobs returned as **interim CEO** (later permanent CEO). He immediately cancelled 70% of Apple's product lines (ending the Newton, clone programs, etc.) and cut **3,000 jobs**.
- **August 1997**: Jobs negotiated a critical **$150 million investment from Microsoft** — Microsoft's stake gave Apple survival capital and ended a patent dispute. In exchange, Microsoft committed to continue Microsoft Office for Mac for 5 years and Internet Explorer for Mac. Many Apple fans were furious about the Microsoft deal.
- **November 1997**: Launched the **Apple Store** online — pioneering build-to-order manufacturing and direct-to-consumer sales. This became one of Apple's most successful retail innovations.
- **May 6, 1998**: Released the **iMac G3** — Jobs' first major product as returning CEO. The iMac was a translucent, all-in-one consumer computer with a CRT display. It sold **800,000 units in its first 5 months** — Apple's first breakout hit in years. The iMac revived Apple's design reputation and financial health.
- **1999**: Released **iBook** — the consumer laptop counterpart to iMac, with the same translucent design.
- **2000**: Apple posted **$309 million profit** — its first annual profit since 1995.
- **2001**: Released **Mac OS X** (March 24) — a complete rewrite of the Mac operating system, built on NeXTSTEP, OpenStep, and BSD Unix. Mac OS X was dramatically more stable, secure, and modern than the classic Mac OS. It became the foundation of all Apple platforms for the next 20+ years.

### 2001–2007: iTunes, iPod, and the Apple Store Ecosystem

- **October 23, 2001**: Released the **iPod** — a 5GB MP3 player that could hold ~1,000 songs. The iPod was not the first MP3 player, but its intuitive scroll-wheel interface and iTunes integration made it the first mainstream success. The original iPod sold 125,000 units in its first holiday quarter.
- **2002**: Released **iMac G4** ("the lamp") — a revolutionary design with a hemispherical base and a flat-panel display mounted on an adjustable arm.
- **2003**: Launched the **iTunes Store** (April) — selling individual songs for **$0.99** each, with over 200,000 tracks available at launch. Within 2 years, iTunes became the world's largest music retailer. The iTunes Store made Apple the dominant force in digital music distribution.
- **2004 (August)**: Steve Jobs underwent surgery for pancreatic cancer (a rare islet cell neuroendocrine tumor, not the more common adenocarcinoma). He recovered and continued as CEO.
- **2005**: Steve Jobs announced that Apple would transition from PowerPC to **Intel processors** (like PCs) — a shocking reversal. The first Intel Macs (MacBook Pro, iMac) shipped in January 2006; the transition completed by August 2006. This made Macs capable of running Windows natively (via Boot Camp).
- **2005**: Released **iPod Nano** (2nd generation) and **iPod Shuffle** — expanding the iPod lineup. iPod became so dominant it generated more revenue than all of Apple's other products combined.

### 2007–2011: iPhone Revolution and the Post-PC Era

- **January 9, 2007**: At Macworld Expo, Steve Jobs announced **iPhone** — calling it "a revolutionary product." The original iPhone combined a phone, an iPod, and an internet communicator with a revolutionary multi-touch interface. It sold **270,000 units in the first 30 hours**. The iPhone immediately disrupted Nokia, Motorola, BlackBerry, and the entire mobile phone industry.
- **2007**: Apple renamed itself from **Apple Computer, Inc.** to **Apple Inc.** — reflecting that it was now a consumer electronics company, not just a computer company.
- **July 2008**: Launched the **App Store** (for iPhone 3G) — enabling third-party developers to build and distribute apps. The App Store launched with 500 apps; within a month, 60 million apps had been downloaded. The App Store created the mobile app economy and became a massive recurring revenue stream for Apple.
- **2009 (June)**: Released **iPhone 3GS** — the first iPhone capable of video recording.
- **2010 (January)**: Released **iPad** — a tablet computer that Jobs positioned between a smartphone and a laptop. The iPad sold **300,000 units on its first day** and 500,000 by the end of the first week. It created and dominated the modern tablet market.
- **June 2010**: Released **iPhone 4** — the first iPhone with FaceTime video calling and multitasking. The iPhone 4 had a major antenna design flaw ("Antennagate") — Apple offered free bumpers to address it, and Jobs famously downplayed the issue at a press conference.
- **2011 (January 17)**: Steve Jobs took his third medical leave of absence.
- **June 2011**: Jobs unveiled **iCloud** — Apple's cloud storage and synchronization service, replacing MobileMe. iCloud was launched in October 2011.
- **August 24, 2011**: Steve Jobs **resigned as CEO**, effective immediately. He became **Chairman of the Board**. **Tim Cook** became CEO.

### 2011–2016: Tim Cook Era, Apple Watch, and Services Growth

- **October 5, 2011**: **Steve Jobs died** at age 56, after a long battle with pancreatic cancer. Cook publicly acknowledged employees and the public; Apple stores became gathering places for memorial tributes.
- **2012**: Apple released **iPhone 5** (September) — the first iPhone with a 4-inch display and Lightning connector (replacing the 30-pin dock connector). Pre-orders hit **2 million in 24 hours** — a record at the time.
- **September 2012**: Released **iPhone 5** and **iOS 6**. Apple Maps replaced Google Maps as the default maps application — a deeply controversial decision as Apple Maps had numerous accuracy problems.
- **2013 (September)**: Released **iPhone 5S** (with Touch ID fingerprint scanner) and **iPhone 5C** (colored plastic budget model). Touch ID became a flagship feature.
- **2014 (September)**: Released **iPhone 6 and 6 Plus** — Apple's first "phablets" with 4.7" and 5.5" displays. The iPhone 6 became the best-selling iPhone model of all time (222 million units sold). It also introduced Apple Pay.
- **2014 (June)**: Announced **Apple Watch** — Apple's first new product category since iPad. Released in April 2015 (after delays), Apple Watch was initially marketed as a fashion accessory (with luxury partner bands). It evolved into a serious health/fitness device and became the world's best-selling wearable.
- **May 2014**: Apple confirmed acquisition of **Beats Electronics** for **$3 billion** — Apple's largest acquisition at the time. Beats gave Apple Beats Music (which became Apple Music) and Beats headphones (a premium audio brand).
- **2015**: Apple released **iPhone 6S** and **iPad Pro** (first iPad with keyboard accessory and Apple Pencil).
- **2016**: Apple announced that **over 1 billion Apple devices were in active use worldwide** — a massive installed base for services revenue.

### 2017–2020: AR, Services, and Apple Silicon

- **2017**: Released **iPhone X** (October) — a major redesign with edge-to-edge OLED display, Face ID (replacing Touch ID), and the notch. The iPhone X started at $999 — establishing the premium pricing tier that became standard for flagship iPhones.
- **2017 (June)**: Apple's WWDC became the stage for **ARKit** — Apple's augmented reality framework for iPhone. Apple positioned AR as the next major computing platform after smartphone.
- **2017 (June)**: Apple announced **HomePod** smart speaker (released February 2018) — competing with Amazon Echo and Google Home, though with superior audio quality but less-capable Siri.
- **2018**: Apple became the **first US company** to reach **$1 trillion market cap** (August 2, 2018).
- **2019**: Released **Apple TV+** streaming service (November) — Apple's entry into the crowded streaming wars. Apple TV+ launched with only 5 original shows/movies. It has since grown to a significant content library.
- **2019**: Released **AirPods Pro** — which became a massive cultural and commercial hit, making wireless earbuds mainstream.
- **2020 (June)**: Announced **transition from Intel to Apple Silicon** — custom ARM-based processors designed in-house (M1 chip). This was the biggest shift in Mac architecture since the PowerPC transition. Apple claimed 2-3x CPU performance improvement and all-day battery life.
- **November 2020**: Released **MacBook Air M1, MacBook Pro M1, and Mac Mini M1** — the first Macs with Apple Silicon. They received rave reviews for performance and battery life.
- **December 2020**: Apple became the **first US company** to reach **$2 trillion market cap** (August 2020 actually, about 2 months after $1T).

### 2021–2023: Spatial Computing and Services Dominance

- **2021**: Released **iPhone 13**, **iPad Mini 6** (with new design), and **MacBook Pro M1 Pro/Max**.
- **April 2021**: Released **AirTag** — a Bluetooth tracker for physical items.
- **October 2021**: Released **MacBook Pro M1 Pro/Max** and **iPhone 14** lineup (with Dynamic Island on Pro models).
- **2022**: Released **iPhone 14** (standard and Pro), **Apple Watch Ultra**, **iPad Pro M2**, **MacBook Air M2**.
- **2022**: Apple's services revenue (App Store, Apple Music, iCloud, Apple TV+, Apple Pay, licensing) reached **$80 billion annually** — representing ~22% of total revenue and ~35% of gross profit. Services became Apple's second-largest revenue segment after iPhone.
- **2023**: Released **iPhone 15** (all USB-C, replacing Lightning), **Apple Watch Series 9**, **MacBook Pro M3**.
- **2023**: Apple Music reached **100 million** monthly active subscribers. Apple TV+ won multiple Academy Awards — Ted Liang's "The Boy, The Mole, The Fox and the Horse" won Best Animated Short.

### 2024–2026: Vision Pro and AI Era

- **February 2024**: Released **Vision Pro** — Apple's first spatial computer (mixed reality headset). At $3,499, it was positioned as an "early adopter" device for developers and enterprise. Vision Pro featured eye tracking, hand gestures, and voice control — a fundamentally new input paradigm. Apple called it "the most advanced consumer electronics device ever created."
- **2024**: Apple introduced **Apple Intelligence** — a comprehensive AI system integrated across iOS, macOS, and watchOS. Apple Intelligence included Writing Tools (AI text generation), Image Playground (AI image generation), Genmoji (AI emoji), Siri improvements with ChatGPT integration, and system-wide AI features. Apple positioned it as privacy-focused (most AI processing on-device, with "Private Cloud Compute" for heavier workloads).
- **2024**: Released **iPhone 16** lineup — the first iPhones designed around AI (Apple Intelligence) with a new Camera Control button and Action Button.
- **October 2025**: Apple became the **first publicly traded US company** to exceed **$4 trillion market cap**.
- **2025**: Apple launched **Apple Intelligence** in more languages (including Chinese) and expanded AI features across iPhone, iPad, and Mac. The AI features drove a noticeable upgrade cycle, particularly in markets where AI capabilities were emphasized.
- **2026**: Apple announced **visionOS 3** with improved productivity features for Vision Pro. Developer adoption for spatial computing apps accelerated.

## Key Products and Business Segments

### iPhone (~$200B annual revenue)
- The world's most profitable product line. ~250 million iPhones sold annually.
- iPhone 16 Pro/Pro Max (A18 Pro chip, titanium frame, Camera Control button, Apple Intelligence).
- Standard iPhone 16 (A18 chip, Aluminum frame).
- SE 4 (2026 expected) — bringing Apple Intelligence to the budget tier.

### Services (~80B annual revenue)
- **App Store** — ~$100B+ annual developer payouts; Apple's 30% commission generates ~$20-25B annually.
- **Apple Music** (~100M subscribers), **Apple TV+**, **Apple Arcade**, **Apple News+**.
- **iCloud** (~850M+ paying subscribers) — 200GB, 2TB, 6TB plans.
- **Apple Pay** — contactless payments integrated into iPhone and Apple Watch.
- **Licensing** — Google pays Apple ~$15-20B annually to remain the default search engine on Safari (a major DOJ antitrust focus).

### Mac (~$30B annual revenue)
- MacBook Air (M3/M4 chips) — the most popular laptop globally.
- MacBook Pro (M3/M4 Pro/Max chips) — professional mobile workstation.
- iMac, Mac Mini, Mac Studio, Mac Pro — desktop lineup.
- Apple Silicon gives industry-leading performance per watt.

### Wearables (~$40B annual revenue)
- **Apple Watch** (Series 10, Ultra 3) — world's best-selling smartwatch; ECG, blood oxygen, sleep tracking,Crash Detection, Fall Detection.
- **AirPods** (Pro, standard, Max) — wireless earbuds with ANC and spatial audio.
- **Vision Pro** — spatial computing headset (niche but strategically important).

### iPad (~$20B annual revenue)
- iPad Pro (M4 chip, OLED display, Apple Pencil Pro).
- iPad Air, iPad mini, iPad (standard) — covering all price tiers.

## CEO and Leadership

- **CEO**: **Tim Cook** (since August 24, 2011) — succeeded Steve Jobs. Cook, an operations and supply chain expert (previously COO), transformed Apple's financial scale (revenue grew from $108B to $416B) while maintaining product quality. He has been credited with mastering global supply chain logistics and expanding Apple's services revenue.
- **Design Chief**: **Jony Ive** (Sir Jonathan Ive) — the legendary British designer who designed the iMac, iPod, iPhone, and iPad. Ive left Apple in 2019 to found LoveFrom design studio; Apple remained a client.
- **COO**: **Jeff Williams** (since 2015) — oversaw Apple Watch development and health initiatives.
- **SVP of Machine Learning**: **John Giannandrea** (joined 2018 from Google) — leads Apple Intelligence.
- **Founder**: **Steve Jobs** (1955–2011) — CEO (1976–1985, 1997–2011); Chairman (2011). His second tenure (1997–2011) produced iMac, iPod, iPhone, iPad, iTunes, App Store, Mac OS X, and Apple Stores — transforming Apple from near-bankruptcy to the world's most valuable company.

## Financial Highlights

- **FY2025 Revenue**: ~$416 billion
- **FY2025 Net Income**: ~$112 billion (26.9% net margin — among the highest in any industry)
- **FY2025 Operating Income**: ~$133 billion
- **Gross Margin**: ~47% (services margin ~74%, hardware margin ~36%)
- **Cash reserves**: ~$162 billion (mostly held in US Treasuries)
- **Employees**: ~166,000 (2025)

- iPhone accounts for ~50% of revenue; Services ~22%; Mac ~8%; Wearables ~10%; iPad ~5%.
- Apple has been the world's largest company by market cap since 2011.

## Manufacturing

Apple is a **fabless** company for processors — it designs its own chips (A-series for iPhone, M-series for Mac, S-series for Apple Watch) but they are manufactured by **TSMC** (Taiwan Semiconductor Manufacturing Company) using advanced nodes (3nm for A18 Pro, 3nm for M4). Most Apple hardware (iPhone, iPad, Mac, AirPods, Watch) is assembled by **Foxconn** (Hon Hai Precision Industry) in China and increasingly in India and Vietnam. Apple has been diversifying its supply chain to India (Tata Group assembling iPhone) and Vietnam (for AirPods and other accessories) in response to geopolitical tensions and labor cost increases.
