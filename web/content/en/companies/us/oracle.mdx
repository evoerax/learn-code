# Oracle

**Oracle Corporation** (NYSE: ORCL) is the world's largest enterprise software company, specializing in relational database management systems (RDBMS), cloud infrastructure, and enterprise application software. Founded in **1977** by Larry Ellison, Bob Miner, and Ed Oates — three engineers who left a consulting firm to build a commercial relational database — Oracle is the inventor of the **Oracle Database**, the world's most widely used enterprise database system, running the vast majority of the world's business data. Headquartered in Austin, Texas (relocated from Redwood Shores, California in 2020), Oracle has grown from a database company into a comprehensive cloud computing provider competing with Microsoft Azure, Amazon Web Services, and Google Cloud Platform. The company serves over 400,000 customers in 180 countries, including 100% of the Fortune 100. FY2025 revenue was **$57.4 billion** with **$12.44 billion** net income, and approximately **164,000 employees**.

## Company History & Timeline

### Founding and the Relational Database Vision (1977–1983)

**Larry Ellison**, Bob Miner, and Ed Oates left **Ampex Corporation** in **1977** to start **Software Development Laboratories (SDL)** in Santa Clara, California. The company was funded by a small venture capital investment and by the founders' own savings. The name "Oracle" came from a project they were working on for the CIA (the CIA's project was called "Oracle").

The founding team had read a **1976 paper by Edgar F. Codd** — a researcher at IBM — describing the theory of **relational database management systems (RDBMS)**. Codd's paper described a revolutionary idea: that data should be stored in **tables (relations)** with rows and columns, and that queries should be based on mathematical set theory rather than navigational record access. This relational model was in stark contrast to the dominant hierarchical and network database systems of the era (like IBM's IMS).

Ellison recognized immediately that Codd's relational model was the future of databases. SDL's first product was an **Oracle RDBMS** based on Codd's papers — one of the first commercial relational database implementations. IBM took years to commercialize its own RDBMS (DB2, released in 1983), giving Oracle a critical head start.

The company went through several name changes:

- **1977–1979**: Software Development Laboratories (SDL)
- **1979–1983**: Relational Software, Inc. (RSI)
- **1983–1995**: Oracle Systems Corporation
- **1995–present**: Oracle Corporation

In **1979**, under the name RSI, Oracle released **Oracle V2** — one of the first commercially available RDBMS products. The company had no real competition for three years until IBM finally shipped DB2 in 1983. By then, Oracle had established a strong customer base and a reputation for fast, portable database software.

### Growth and the Sun Microsystem Acquisition Strategy (1984–1999)

Oracle went public on **NASDAQ in 1986**, raising $52 million at a valuation of about $270 million. The stock price rose 300% on the first day of trading. Larry Ellison was now a billionaire on paper and became one of the most prominent figures in Silicon Valley.

The 1980s and 1990s were a period of rapid growth and controversy for Oracle:

- **1990**: Oracle's revenue reached $1 billion but the company faced a serious crisis — rapid growth had outpaced its financial controls, and Oracle had to restate its earnings. The stock fell 50% in one day. This crisis led to the installation of professional management and a restructuring that saved the company.
- **1991**: **Ray Lane** joined as President and COO, bringing much-needed operational discipline. Lane would become one of the most influential figures at Oracle for two decades.
- **1995**: Oracle introduced **Oracle Parallel Server** — enabling multiple database instances to access the same database simultaneously, dramatically improving performance and availability.

Oracle's dominant position in databases made it a target for government scrutiny. In **1997**, the US Department of Justice began an investigation into Oracle's practices, examining whether Oracle's "first and second" maintenance fee structure (charging for support even if customers had discontinued using the software) was anticompetitive. The investigation was eventually dropped.

### Internet Era and the "Composable" Vision (1999–2005)

When the internet exploded in the late 1990s, Oracle was uniquely positioned: every web application needed a database, and Oracle's reputation for reliability made it the default choice for high-traffic websites. Yahoo, eBay, and Amazon all ran early versions of Oracle databases.

Ellison's vision for the "**Network Computing**" era — articulated in a famous 1995 strategy paper — predicted that the internet would replace the PC as the primary computing device, and that Oracle would be at the center by providing database and application services over the network. While this vision was ahead of its time (the cloud computing era wouldn't arrive for another decade), it shaped Oracle's product strategy.

In **1999**, Oracle launched **Oracle 8i** — the first Oracle database optimized for internet applications with built-in Java support. In **2001**, **Oracle 9i** introduced Real Application Clusters (RAC), allowing multiple servers to run the same database with automatic load balancing.

### The Hostile Takeover of PeopleSoft (2003–2004)

One of the most controversial episodes in Oracle's history was the **hostile takeover attempt of PeopleSoft**. PeopleSoft was a competitor in enterprise HR and financial software, and Oracle launched a hostile bid in **2003** — offering $5.1 billion in cash after PeopleSoft's board rejected the offer as too low.

Oracle's bid triggered a dramatic 18-month legal and proxy battle. PeopleSoft adopted a **"poison pill" customer assurance program** — effectively guaranteeing customers refunds of up to twice the license fee if the acquisition caused service disruption. This was designed to make the acquisition financially unattractive. Oracle eventually raised its offer several times and ultimately acquired PeopleSoft for **$10.3 billion** in **December 2004**.

The PeopleSoft acquisition was Oracle's largest at the time and transformed Oracle from a pure database company into an enterprise applications company. It also established a pattern: Oracle would use aggressive M&A to enter new markets, rather than building competitive products organically.

### Ellison's Succession and the Rise of Safra Catz (2005–2014)

**Larry Ellison stepped down as CEO in 2005**, replaced by ** Safra Catz** (President) and **Mark Hurd** (CEO). Both had joined Oracle from HP (Catz) and HP's printer division (Hurd). Ellison remained as Chairman and CTO (Chief Technology Officer).

This succession was notable: Oracle had chosen professional managers rather than promoting from within its engineering culture. Hurd was known for his brutal operational efficiency and relentless focus on earnings per share — a sharp contrast to Ellison's visionary, flamboyant leadership style.

Under Hurd and Catz (2005–2019), Oracle executed a massive M&A program to build out its application and cloud portfolio:

- **2005**: Acquired **Retek** ($653 million) — retail software
- **2006**: Acquired **TimesTen** ($419 million) — in-memory database (now Oracle TimesTen Application-Tier Database Cache)
- **2007**: Acquired **Hyperion Solutions** ($3.3 billion) — performance management and business intelligence
- **2008**: Acquired **BEA Systems** ($8.5 billion) — middleware and application server
- **2010**: Acquired **Sun Microsystems** ($7.4 billion) — the most transformative acquisition, giving Oracle its own hardware, OS (Solaris), Java, MySQL, and storage systems

### The Sun Acquisition: Transforming Oracle (2010)

The **$7.4 billion acquisition of Sun Microsystems** in **January 2010** was Oracle's most consequential. Sun had been founded in 1982 by Vinod Khosla, Andy Bechtolsheim, and Bill Joy — three Stanford graduate students. Sun's products included:

- **SPARC processors** and **Solaris** Unix operating system
- **Java** (acquired by Sun in 1995) — the world's most widely used programming language
- **MySQL** — the world's most popular open-source database (Sun acquired MySQL for $1 billion in 2008)
- **Storage systems** (Sun StorageTek)
- **OpenOffice** (open-source office suite)

The acquisition was immediately controversial. Sun's revenue had been declining (from $18.8 billion in FY2005 to $13.9 billion in FY2009), and Oracle paid a significant premium. But the strategic value was clear: Oracle now controlled the entire software stack from hardware to operating system to database to applications.

Within months of the acquisition, Oracle discontinued or significantly changed several Sun products, laid off thousands of Sun engineers, and raised prices for Sun's software significantly. The open-source community was alarmed when Oracle changed MySQL's licensing terms and was slow to release patches for security vulnerabilities. A group of former Sun engineers forked MySQL into **MariaDB** — creating an ongoing tension between Oracle MySQL and the open-source community.

Oracle's hardware business (servers, storage) has remained a smaller but strategically important part of the company, providing integrated systems (Exadata, Exalogic, SPARC SuperCluster) optimized for Oracle Database performance.

### Mark Hurd's Death and Return of Larry Ellison (2019)

**Mark Hurd died suddenly in October 2019** at age 62, forcing a management reorganization. Larry Ellison returned as **Chairman** (he had remained CTO) and assumed a more active role. **Safra Catz** continued as President and CFO, becoming effectively the day-to-day operating leader.

In **2019**, Oracle's board appointed **Larry Ellison's son, David Ellison**, as Executive Vice President of Product Development, signaling a potential succession plan. David had founded a cloud gaming company ( autonomous software) and had been leading Oracle's cloud infrastructure development.

### Cloud Transformation and Competition with AWS (2012–2025)

Oracle's biggest strategic challenge has been transitioning from on-premise software licensing (its traditional model) to cloud computing. Amazon AWS launched in 2002 and was generating billions in revenue by 2010 while Oracle was still focused on selling database licenses.

Oracle launched its first true cloud offering — **Oracle Cloud** — in **2016**, significantly later than AWS (2006), Azure (2010), and Google Cloud (2013). Oracle's cloud strategy centered on its unique advantage: **Oracle Database** and **Oracle applications** running natively in Oracle's own cloud, giving performance advantages for customers using Oracle databases.

Key cloud products:

- **Oracle Cloud Infrastructure (OCI)**: IaaS — virtual machines, storage, networking (launched 2016)
- **Oracle Autonomous Database**: The world's first self-driving, self-repairing database — eliminates human database administrators by automating tuning, patching, and backup (launched 2017–2018)
- **Oracle SaaS**: Fusion Cloud ERP, HCM, CX, SCM (replacing on-premise PeopleSoft, Siebel, etc.)
- **Oracle Gen 2 Cloud**: Launched 2019 with Exadata Cloud@Customer and dedicated region cloud options

Oracle's cloud revenue has grown substantially but remains a distant fourth behind AWS, Azure, and Google Cloud in overall cloud market share. However, Oracle's **database-as-a-service** offering has been highly profitable and has a loyal customer base among large enterprises that already run Oracle databases on-premise.

### Cerner Acquisition and Healthcare Push (2022)

In **June 2022**, Oracle completed the **$28.3 billion acquisition of Cerner Corporation** — one of the largest acquisitions in healthcare technology history. Cerner was the second-largest electronic health records (EHR) company in the US (after Epic Systems), with systems used in over 35,000 healthcare facilities worldwide.

The acquisition was driven by Larry Ellison's vision of using Oracle's AI and database technology to revolutionize healthcare:

- **Oracle Health** (the new name for Cerner) aimed to build an AI-powered health records platform
- Ellison argued that Oracle's database technology could process health data at scale in ways that Cerner's legacy systems couldn't
- The combined company would compete with Epic Systems in the hospital EHR market

The integration of Cerner proved difficult. In **2023**, Oracle wrote down approximately **$885 million** of Cerner's value due to integration challenges and slower-than-expected cloud migration. Oracle's healthcare ambitions faced strong competition from Epic's deeply entrenched hospital relationships.

### Expansion to AI Infrastructure and Nvidia Partnership (2023–2025)

In **2023–2024**, Oracle made a major strategic move into **AI infrastructure**, recognizing that the AI boom required massive GPU compute clusters that Oracle's cloud could host. Oracle announced partnerships with **Nvidia** (for H100 and GH200 GPU clusters), **Microsoft Azure** (interconnect between Oracle and Microsoft clouds to enable hybrid AI), and **Google Cloud** (cross-cloud connectivity).

Oracle's **OCI Supercluster** — launched in 2024 — was designed to compete with AWS, Azure, and Google Cloud for AI training and inference workloads. Oracle claimed its RDMA (Remote Direct Memory Access) networking and Exadata hardware provided advantages for large-scale AI workloads.

In **2024**, Oracle announced it would deploy ** Nvidia GB200 NVL72 racks** — massive GPU clusters of 72 Blackwell GPUs interconnected with NVLink — for AI training workloads. Oracle signed agreements with **xAI** (Elon Musk's AI company), ** Cohesity**, and others to run AI workloads on OCI.

Larry Ellison described Oracle's AI strategy as focusing on **"AI infrastructure for AI companies"** rather than building AI models — partnering with model companies (xAI, Cohesity, etc.) rather than competing with them.

### Nashville Headquarters and Ongoing Strategy (2024–2025)

In **April 2024**, Oracle announced it would relocate its headquarters to **Nashville, Tennessee** — near the new Oracle Health campus being built in Nashville and reflecting Oracle's growing focus on healthcare. Larry Ellison had already moved his personal residence to Nashville in 2022.

Oracle's FY2025 revenue of **$57.4 billion** represented approximately 8% growth YoY, driven primarily by cloud services (OCI and SaaS) growth and the Cerner/Oracle Health business. The company's operating income was $15.4 billion, and net income was $12.44 billion — one of the highest in the software industry.

## Products and Business Segments

### Oracle Database and Technology

Oracle's core product:

- **Oracle Database** (21c, 23ai): The world's most advanced relational database with JSON/XML support, in-memory processing, blockchain tables, and ML capabilities
- **MySQL** (acquired with Sun): World's most popular open-source RDBMS, used by Facebook, Twitter, YouTube
- **Oracle Exadata**: Hardware-software engineered system optimized for Oracle Database performance
- **Oracle Autonomous Database**: Self-driving, self-patching, self-securing cloud database service
- **Oracle NoSQL Database**: Document/key-value database for cloud-native applications

### Cloud Infrastructure (OCI)

Oracle's IaaS/PaaS cloud:

- **OCI Compute**: Bare metal servers, VMs, GPU instances (A100, H100, GB200)
- **OCI Storage**: Object storage, block storage, file storage
- **OCI Networking**: FastConnect (dedicated connectivity), VPN Connect, RDMA networking
- **OCI Superclusters**: Large-scale GPU clusters for AI training
- **Oracle Cloud VMware Solution**: VMware-compatible cloud environment

### Enterprise Applications (Fusion Cloud)

Oracle's SaaS suite:

- **Oracle Fusion Cloud ERP**: Financials, procurement, project management, supply chain management
- **Oracle Fusion Cloud HCM**: Human capital management, payroll, talent management
- **Oracle Fusion Cloud CX**: Sales, marketing, service, commerce
- **Oracle Fusion Cloud SCM**: Supply chain planning, manufacturing, logistics

### Oracle Health (Cerner)

Healthcare technology:

- **Oracle Health EHR**: Electronic health records system for hospitals and clinics
- **Oracle Health Data Platform**: AI-ready health data platform for population health analytics
- **Fusionargs**: Clinical data aggregation and analytics

### Hardware and Systems

From Sun Microsystems acquisition:

- **Oracle SPARC servers** (T-series, M-series)
- **Oracle Exadata** (engineered database system)
- **Oracle Exalogic** (Java middleware optimized system)
- **Oracle ZFS Storage Appliances**
- **Pillar Data Systems** (storage)

## Corporate Structure and Leadership

**Larry Ellison** (co-founder, age 80 in 2025) has been Chairman and CTO since stepping down as CEO in 2005. He remains deeply involved in Oracle's technical direction and strategy. Ellison owns approximately **40% of Oracle's stock**, making him one of the wealthiest individuals in the world (~$150B net worth).

**Safra Catz** (President) has been effectively the COO of Oracle since 2019, managing day-to-day operations across all business units. She has been President since 2005.

**Jeff Henley** (Vice Chairman) has been on Oracle's board since 1995 and served as CFO from 1999 to 2019.

**David Ellison** (Executive Vice President, Product Development) leads Oracle's product development and is widely seen as the likely successor to lead Oracle in the future.
