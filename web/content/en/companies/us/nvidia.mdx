# NVIDIA

NVIDIA Corporation (Nasdaq: NVDA) is an American semiconductor and technology company founded in 1993 and headquartered in Santa Clara, California. It is the world's leading manufacturer of graphics processing units (GPUs), dominating the gaming GPU market with the GeForce brand and the AI training chip market with the A100, H100, and Blackwell architectures. Founded by Jensen Huang, Chris Malachowsky, and Curtis Priem — who met at a Denny's roadside diner — NVIDIA has grown from a $40,000 startup to the most valuable semiconductor company in history, reaching a $5 trillion market cap in October 2025, making it the first company ever to reach each of the $1T, $2T, $3T, $4T, and $5T milestones.

## Timeline

### 1993–1996: Founding and Early Struggles

- **April 5, 1993**: NVIDIA founded by **Jensen Huang** (then 30, Taiwanese-American electrical engineer, previously at LSI Logic and AMD), **Chris Malachowsky**, and **Curtis Priem** (both former Sun Microsystems engineers). The three co-founders met at a **Denny's roadside diner** in San Jose, California, where they sketched the company's first business plan on a napkin. Initial funding: **$40,000** — primarily from co-founder savings and small personal contributions.
- **1994**: First chip design partnership with **Sega** — NVIDIA was to produce a 3D graphics processor (NV1) for Sega's Saturn console. The partnership provided critical early revenue.
- **1995**: NVIDIA released the **NV1** — its first consumer graphics card. It was technically innovative (integrating 2D, 3D, and audio on one chip) but commercially disappointing due to incompatibility with dominant graphics standards. Poor sales nearly bankrupted the company.
- **1996**: **Sega dropped the partnership**, leaving NVIDIA without its primary customer and unable to make payroll. NVIDIA laid off **half its staff** (from ~100 to ~50 employees). This period is called the "Sega crisis" — the company nearly collapsed.

### 1997–2000: RIVA Breakthrough and IPO

- **1997**: NVIDIA released the **RIVA 128** — a 3D graphics chip that was affordable, fast, and compatible with Direct3D and OpenGL standards. It sold **one million units in four months** — NVIDIA's first major commercial success.
- **1998**: Released **RIVA TNT** — further improved performance and cemented NVIDIA's reputation for delivering high-quality PC graphics drivers.
- **January 22, 1999**: NVIDIA went **public on NASDAQ** (NVDA), raising ~$120 million. Jensen Huang has been CEO since founding — making him one of the longest-tenured tech CEOs in Silicon Valley.
- **1999 (September)**: Released the **GeForce 256** — the world's first marketed **GPU (Graphics Processing Unit)**. NVIDIA coined the term "GPU" and positioned the product as a "transforming computer industry" innovation. It offloaded geometry calculations from the CPU, enabling more complex 3D graphics. This was the product that made NVIDIA famous.
- **1999**: Won the contract to produce graphics hardware for **Microsoft's Xbox** — receiving a **$200 million advance payment**. This was a transformative deal that provided massive capital for R&D.

### 2000: 3dfx Acquisition and Market Dominance

- **2000**: Acquired the intellectual assets of **3dfx Interactive** — the leading 3D graphics company of the late 1990s, famous for the Voodoo Graphics series. This acquisition eliminated NVIDIA's primary competitor and gave NVIDIA access to key patents and talent.
- **2000**: Released the **GeForce 2** series — further extending the performance lead over competitors.
- **2000**: Established itself as the dominant GPU maker for PC gaming, a position it has never surrendered.

### 2001–2006: Expansion and CUDA Vision

- **2001**: Released **GeForce 3** — first consumer GPU with programmable shader support, laying groundwork for GPU computing.
- **2003**: Acquired **MediaQ** (~$70 million) — wireless and mobile graphics chip maker.
- **2004**: NVIDIA's GPUs began being used in **supercomputing** clusters — the company recognized that its parallel processing architecture was ideally suited for scientific computing.
- **2006**: Released **GeForce 8800** (G80 architecture) — a major architectural leap. This was the first GPU to fully support **CUDA** (Compute Unified Device Architecture).
- **2006**: The **CUDA platform** was officially launched — a software layer allowing developers to program NVIDIA GPUs for general-purpose computing. NVIDIA invested **over $1 billion** in developing CUDA over the early years. CUDA enabled GPUs to be used for AI training, scientific simulation, and other non-graphics tasks. This was Jensen Huang's pivotal strategic bet — it would take a decade before CUDA became commercially essential for AI.

### 2007–2012: Forbes Company of the Year and the AI Winter

- **2007**: **Forbes magazine named NVIDIA "Company of the Year"** — recognizing its GPU technology leadership.
- **2008**: Acquired **Ageia Technologies** — acquiring PhysX physics simulation technology, which NVIDIA integrated into CUDA.
- **2008**: Released **GeForce 9** series and the **GTX 200** series (first using the Tesla architecture).
- **2010**: Released **GeForce Fermi** (400 series) — next-generation CUDA architecture.
- **2010**: Won the contract to power the **Titan supercomputer** at Oak Ridge National Laboratory (using NVIDIA Tesla GPUs) — demonstrating GPU computing's potential for scientific research.
- **2011**: Acquired **Icera** (~$367 million) — baseband processor and RF technology for mobile devices.

### 2012–2018: Deep Learning Revolution and Gaming Boom

- **2012**: A team at the University of Toronto (led by Alex Krizhevsky) used NVIDIA GPUs with CUDA to train **AlexNet** — which won the ImageNet computer vision competition by a massive margin. This sparked the **deep learning revolution** — suddenly, researchers worldwide realized that NVIDIA GPUs were the ideal hardware for training neural networks. NVIDIA's CUDA ecosystem and GPUs became the de facto standard for AI research.
- **2012**: Released **Kepler** architecture (GeForce 600/700 series) — designed for both gaming and GPU computing.
- **2013**: Released **Maxwell** architecture (GeForce 900 series) — improved power efficiency and performance.
- **2015**: Released **GeForce GTX 980** (Maxwell second generation) — widely considered one of the best gaming GPUs ever made.
- **2016**: Released **Pascal** architecture (GeForce 1000 series) — introduced **GDDR5X** memory and significant performance jumps.
- **2017**: Released **Volta** architecture (V100 GPU) — the first AI training chip designed specifically for deep learning, manufactured on TSMC's 12nm process. The V100 had 21.1 billion transistors and delivered 120 teraflops of deep learning performance.
- **2018**: Released **Turing** architecture (GeForce RTX 2000 series) — introduced **real-time ray tracing** (RT cores) and **deep learning super sampling (DLSS)**. The RTX 2080 Ti became the flagship consumer GPU. Turing was the first GPU architecture to include dedicated ray-tracing hardware.

### 2019–2021: Data Center Dominance and AI Infrastructure

- **2019**: Acquired **Mellanox Technologies** for **$6.9 billion** — a pivotal acquisition giving NVIDIA high-speed InfiniBand and Ethernet networking technology essential for connecting thousands of GPUs in AI training clusters (like the NVIDIA DGX SuperPOD systems).
- **2020 (May)**: Released the **Ampere architecture** — the A100 GPU became the dominant AI training chip worldwide. The A100 offered a 20x performance improvement over the V100 for AI inference workloads and became the standard GPU for data center AI workloads at Google Cloud, AWS, Azure, and others.
- **2020 (September)**: Announced intention to acquire **Arm Limited** from SoftBank for **$40 billion** — the largest ever semiconductor acquisition. The deal faced intense regulatory scrutiny and was ultimately abandoned in February 2022 due to opposition from Arm licensees and regulators in the US, UK, and EU.
- **2021 (April)**: Released the **RTX 3090** (Ampere, gaming) and began volume shipments of **H100 GPU** (Hopper architecture, officially launched in 2022) — the chip at the center of the global AI race.

### 2022–2023: Geopolitics and the $1 Trillion Club

- **2022**: The US government imposed **export controls** on NVIDIA's H100 and A100 chips to China, prohibiting their sale to Chinese entities without a license. This was part of broader US semiconductor export controls aimed at slowing China's AI development. NVIDIA responded by creating the **A800 and H800** — downgraded versions of the A100 and H100 specifically designed for the Chinese market.
- **2023 (May 30)**: NVIDIA became the **seventh US company** to reach **$1 trillion market capitalization** — driven by explosive demand for AI training chips from hyperscalers and generative AI companies building large language models.
- **2023**: Released **Grace Hopper** — a CPU-GPU hybrid chip combining the new Grace CPU with the H100 GPU, connected via NVLink-C2C.

### 2024–2025: Blackwell Era and the $5 Trillion Milestone

- **March 2024**: Announced the **Blackwell architecture** — B100 and B200 GPUs designed for "transformers at scale." The B200 delivers ~2.5x the AI training performance of the H100. NVIDIA also announced the **GB200 NVL72** — a rack-scale system with 72 Blackwell GPUs interconnected, delivering 1.4 exaflops of AI compute.
- **June 2024**: NVIDIA reached **$3 trillion** market cap, briefly becoming the world's most valuable company (surpassing Apple and Microsoft briefly).
- **2024**: Jensen Huang presented the **NVIDIA DRIVE** platform — the company's system-on-chip for autonomous vehicles, competing with Mobileye and Qualcomm's Snapdragon Ride.
- **July 2025**: Reached **$4 trillion** market cap.
- **October 2025**: Reached **$5 trillion** market cap — becoming the first company ever to reach each of the $1T through $5T milestones.
- **March 2026**: Announced the **Vera Rubin** architecture — next-generation GPU platform following Blackwell, continuing the cadence of annual architecture announcements.

## Key Products and Business Segments

### Gaming (GeForce)
- **GeForce RTX 4090** — flagship consumer GPU (Ada Lovelace architecture, 2022)
- **GeForce RTX 5090** — latest flagship (Blackwell architecture, 2025)
- **RTX 4070, 4080, 4060** series — mid-range to high-end gaming GPUs
- **Laptop GPUs** — mobile versions for gaming notebooks
- **GeForce NOW** — cloud gaming streaming service

### Data Center / AI
- **H100 GPU** (Hopper) — dominant AI training chip, priced at $25,000–$40,000 per unit
- **H200 GPU** — enhanced H100 with more HBM3e memory
- **B100/B200 GPUs** (Blackwell) — next-gen AI accelerators
- **Grace CPU** — data center CPU announced 2021, manufactured on TSMC 4N
- **DGX systems** — turnkey AI training supercomputers (DGX H100, DGX GB200)
- **NVLink and NVSwitch** — high-speed GPU interconnect technology

### Automotive
- **NVIDIA DRIVE Orin** — system-on-chip for autonomous vehicles (powers vehicles from BYD, Mercedes-Benz, Jaguar Land Rover, etc.)
- **NVIDIA DRIVE Thor** — next-gen automotive chip (2025, delivering 2,000 TOPS)
- **DRIVE Sim** — autonomous vehicle simulation platform

### Professional Visualization
- **RTX 6000 Ada** — professional workstation GPU
- **Omniverse** — real-time 3D collaboration and simulation platform for industrial digital twins

### Networking
- **Mellanox InfiniBand** — high-speed networking for AI clusters
- **BlueField DPU** (Data Processing Unit) — offloads networking, storage, and security tasks from CPUs

## Manufacturing

NVIDIA is a **fabless** company — it designs chips but does not manufacture them. All NVIDIA chips are manufactured by **TSMC** (Taiwan Semiconductor Manufacturing Company) using advanced nodes including N4 (4nm) and N3 (3nm) processes. This reliance on TSMC became a strategic concern during the 2020–2023 global chip shortage and amid geopolitical tensions over Taiwan.
