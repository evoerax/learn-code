# Cisco

**Cisco Systems, Inc.** (NASDAQ: CSCO) is the world's largest networking equipment company, providing the hardware, software, and services that power the internet and enterprise communications. Founded in **1984** by Leonard Bosack and Sandy Lerner — two Stanford University computer scientists — Cisco is credited with inventing the commercial multi-protocol router, the device that enabled different computer networks to communicate with each other and formed the technical foundation of the modern internet. Headquartered in San Jose, California, Cisco's products span enterprise networking (routers, switches, wireless), security, collaboration (Webex), data center, and IoT. The company has made over 250 acquisitions in its history, using M&A to expand into new markets. FY2025 revenue was **$56.65 billion** with **$10.18 billion** net income, and approximately **86,200 employees**.

## Company History & Timeline

### Founding and the Router Invention (1984–1990)

**Leonard Bosack** and **Sandy Lerner** were computer scientists working at Stanford University in the early 1980s. Frustrated that Stanford's administrative computer network (using the DECnet protocol) could not communicate with its research network (using a different protocol), Bosack and Lerner built a multi-protocol router in 1984 — a device that could translate between different network protocols and enable communication across heterogeneous networks. This invention was foundational: the modern internet requires routers to direct traffic between different networks, and Cisco's router design became the template for all subsequent commercial routers.

The company was formally founded on **December 10, 1984**, by Bosack and Lerner, who invested $9,000 of their personal savings. They named the company Cisco — after San Francisco — though initially without the capital C. The company was incorporated as Cisco Systems, Inc. in **December 1986** after the founders found external funding.

Their first product was the **Advanced Gateway Server** (AGS) released in **1986**, followed by the **Cisco 2500** series routers in 1986–1987. The company grew slowly at first, selling primarily to universities and research institutions. Bosack served as CTO and Lerner as VP of Engineering, with a famously intense working relationship that would later contribute to their departure from the company.

### IPO and Early Growth (1990–1995)

In **1990**, Cisco went **public on NASDAQ** with a market capitalization of $224 million — one of the most anticipated tech IPOs of the year. The stock closed at $18 on day one. By this time, Cisco had about 100 employees and $25 million in annual revenue.

The founders' management style created conflict within Cisco. In **1990**, after Bosack and Lerner attempted to fire a VP of Engineering who was undermining the company's direction, the board (which had been brought in with outside investment) forced the founders out. This was one of the earliest and most dramatic examples of a venture-capital-backed board removing company founders — a pattern that would become common in Silicon Valley. Bosack left immediately; Lerner departed shortly after.

John T. Chambers, who had joined Cisco in 1991 as Senior VP of Worldwide Sales and Operations, became President and CEO in **1995**. Chambers would lead Cisco for the next 22 years (1995–2017), becoming one of the most successful CEOs in tech history.

### Acquisition Strategy and Market Leadership (1991–2000)

Chambers recognized early that Cisco's technology could be leapfrogged by faster, more innovative startups, and that the best strategy was to **acquire the best companies** rather than try to build everything internally. This M&A-driven strategy became Cisco's defining characteristic and shaped the entire tech industry's approach to growth.

Key early acquisitions under Chambers:

- **1993**: **Crescendo Communications** (for $89 million) — bringing Ethernet switch technology
- **1994**: **Kalpana** (for $225 million) — Ethernet switching pioneer
- **1995**: **Grand Junction Networks** (for $345 million) — faster Ethernet switches
- **1996**: **Cerent Corporation** ($7 billion) — optical networking, Cisco's largest acquisition at the time
- **1999**: **Scientific-Atlanta** ($6.9 billion) — cable television set-top boxes and content delivery

By **1996**, Cisco had become the world's leading supplier of network routing equipment, with market share exceeding 80% in routers and switches for enterprise networks. The company was generating over $4 billion in annual revenue.

### Dot-Com Peak and the Crash (1999–2001)

In **2000**, during the dot-com bubble peak, Cisco became the **most valuable company in the world** with a market capitalization exceeding **$500 billion** — briefly surpassing Microsoft as the most valuable company in history at that point. Cisco's stock price had multiplied over 1,000% from its IPO price in 1990.

However, the dot-com crash in 2000–2001 devastated Cisco's business. Internet companies — a major customer segment — collapsed, and enterprise spending on network infrastructure froze. Cisco's revenue peaked at $22.3 billion in FY2000 (ended July 2000) and then declined for two consecutive years. The company was forced to write down billions in inventory (unsold network equipment), and its market cap fell by over 90% from peak.

Chambers responded by cutting 8,500 employees (18% of workforce) in 2001 — one of the largest corporate layoffs in history at that time. He also halted acquisitions temporarily and refocused Cisco on its core enterprise networking business. The aggressive cost-cutting during the downturn positioned Cisco well for the recovery.

### Post-Crash Recovery and New Markets (2002–2014)

After the dot-com crash, Chambers began expanding Cisco into adjacent markets:

- **2003–2005**: **Linksys** home networking acquisition ($500 million) — Cisco entered the consumer/SMB router market
- **2005**: **Scientific-Atlanta** ($6.9 billion) — already acquired in 1999, fully integrated into the cable set-top box business
- **2007**: **WebEx** ($3.2 billion) — web conferencing and online meeting software, competing with Microsoft's Office Communicator and Google's enterprise tools
- **2007**: **Tandberg** ($3.4 billion) — video conferencing equipment

In **2006**, Cisco rebranded with the "**Human Network**" advertising campaign — a significant marketing effort to position Cisco beyond hardware into services and software. The campaign was successful but criticized for being vague about Cisco's actual value proposition.

In **2008**, Cisco sold its **Linksys** home networking division to Belkin — effectively exiting the consumer router market it had entered just 5 years earlier, acknowledging that Belkin and Netgear were better positioned to compete in low-margin consumer hardware.

In **2011**, Cisco announced it would acquire **NDS Group** ($5 billion) — a UK/Israel company specializing in video compression and pay-TV software — to strengthen Cisco's service provider video business. In **2012**, Cisco acquired **Sourcefire** ($2.7 billion) — a cybersecurity company specializing in intrusion detection systems — signaling Cisco's entry into the security market.

### CEO Transition: Chuck Robbins (2015–Present)

John Chambers stepped down as CEO in **July 2015** after 20 years as CEO, transitioning to Executive Chairman. **Chuck Robbins**, who had joined Cisco in 1998 and served as Senior VP of Worldwide Operations, became CEO. Chambers remained Executive Chairman until **2017**.

Robbins inherited a company with solid but slow growth, facing disruption from software-defined networking (SDN) and cloud computing. Cisco's traditional hardware switching and routing business faced pressure from white-box switch manufacturers (like Arista Networks) using merchant silicon (Broadcom chips), and from cloud providers building their own networking equipment.

Robbins' strategy focused on:

1. **Recurring revenue**: Shifting from one-time hardware sales to software and subscription models (Cisco ONE, Cisco DNA)
2. **Security**: Building a comprehensive security portfolio through acquisitions
3. **Collaboration**: Strengthening Webex to compete with Zoom and Microsoft Teams
4. **Cloud and Data Center**: Expanding ACI (Application Centric Infrastructure) and HyperFlex hyperconverged infrastructure

Key acquisitions under Robbins:

- **2015**: **OpenDNS** ($635 million) — cloud-delivered DNS security
- **2017**: **BroadSoft** ($1.9 billion) — cloud-based UCaaS (unified communications)
- **2018**: **Duo Security** ($2.35 billion) — zero-trust network access (ZTNA)
- **2018**: **Accompany** ($270 million) — AI-powered relationship intelligence
- **2020**: **ThousandEyes** ($1.2 billion) — network intelligence and monitoring
- **2020**: **Modvian** (voice AI, undisclosed)
- **2022**: **Valtix** (cloud security, undisclosed)
- **2023**: **Splunk** ($28 billion, completed March 2024) — the largest acquisition in Cisco's history, transforming Cisco into a major data and observability platform

### Splunk Acquisition and AI Era (2023–2025)

The **$28 billion Splunk acquisition** — announced in **September 2023** and completed in **March 2024** — was the largest in Cisco's history and represented a fundamental strategic shift. Splunk's platform for machine data ingestion, indexing, and analysis (logs, events, metrics, sensor data) made it a leader in cybersecurity SIEM (Security Information and Event Management), IT operations (AIOps), and observability.

The acquisition had several strategic rationales:

1. **Recurring revenue**: Splunk's cloud subscriptions generated predictable recurring revenue, addressing investor concerns about Cisco's lumpy hardware revenue
2. **AI/ML foundation**: Splunk's data lake contained petabytes of enterprise operational data — ideal for training AI models for security threat detection and IT operations automation
3. **Competitive positioning**: Cisco could bundle Splunk with its networking hardware to provide "network as a sensor" security — a unique differentiator vs. pure-play security vendors
4. **Market expansion**: Splunk's $800M+ annual cloud revenue gave Cisco credibility in the fast-growing cloud-native observability market

In **2024–2025**, Cisco repositioned Splunk as the core of its **"AI-ready network"** strategy — arguing that as enterprises deploy AI workloads, they need both the network infrastructure to carry AI traffic and the observability tools to monitor AI application performance. Cisco introduced **Cisco AI Network** features across its switching and routing portfolio, using Splunk data to enable proactive network optimization for AI workloads.

In **2024**, Cisco introduced **Cisco Silicon One** G200 — its latest network silicon designed specifically for AI/ML workload traffic in data center clusters. The company claimed G200 could handle 4x the throughput of competing merchant silicon for AI training workloads.

## Products and Business Segments

### Networking (Switching, Routing, Wireless)

Cisco's core business, generating the majority of revenue:

- **Enterprise Switching**: Cisco Catalyst 9000 series (wired switching), spanning access, aggregation, and data center switches
- **Routing**: Cisco ISR (Integrated Services Router) 4000 series, ASR (Aggregation Services Router) 9000 series for service providers
- **Wireless**: Cisco Meraki wireless access points (cloud-managed), Cisco Catalyst wireless controllers, WiFi 6E and WiFi 7 access points
- **Silicon One**: Cisco's own merchant silicon ASIC family (P4-capable), used in both Cisco and white-box switches

### Security

Built through 20+ acquisitions since 2013:

- **Cisco Umbrella / OpenDNS**: Cloud-delivered secure web gateway (SWG), DNS-layer security
- **Duo Security**: Zero-trust network access (ZTNA) for hybrid work
- **Cisco ISE (Identity Services Engine)**: Network access control (NAC)
- **Cisco Firepower / NGFW**: Next-generation firewall appliances
- **Cisco Sourcefire**: Intrusion detection/prevention (IDS/IPS)
- **Cisco Talos**: One of the world's largest threat intelligence organizations, feeds protection to all Cisco security products
- **Splunk**: SIEM, SOAR, IT operations, AIOps, observability (post-acquisition)

### Collaboration

- **Webex**: Video conferencing (Webex Meetings, Webex Events), team messaging (Webex Teams), contact center (Webex Contact Center)
- **Cisco Unified Communications Manager (CUCM)**: On-premise IP telephony
- **BroadSoft**: Cloud UCaaS platform for service providers

### Data Center and Compute

- **Cisco UCS (Unified Computing System)**: Servers, hyperconverged infrastructure (HyperFlex)
- **Cisco Application Centric Infrastructure (ACI)**: SD-Access / intent-based networking for data centers
- **Cisco Nexus switches**: Data center switching (100G/400G/800G)

### IoT and Transportation

- **Cisco IoT**: Industrial networking for manufacturing, energy, transportation
- **Cisco Kinetic**: IoT data management platform
- **Cisco Jasper**: Connected vehicle IoT platform

## Corporate Structure and Leadership

**Chuck Robbins** has been CEO since July 2015. Prior to becoming CEO, he spent 17 years at Cisco in various roles including leading the Americas sales organization and worldwide operations. He has led over 20 acquisitions and pivoted Cisco toward software and recurring revenue.

**John T. Chambers** served as Executive Chairman from 2015 to 2017, and remains a prominent figure in tech and US-India relations (he is a major investor in Indian startups).

**Other key executives**: **Oliver T. Boyer** (Chief Sustainability Officer), **Francine Katsoudas** (Chief People Officer), **Jeff Rice** (Chief Customer Officer), **Maria J. Penedo** (Chief Financial Officer).
