# Intel

Intel Corporation, headquartered in Santa Clara, California, is the world's leading semiconductor company and the creator of the x86 processor architecture that defined modern computing. Founded in 1968 by Robert Noyce and Gordon Moore (co-inventor of Moore's Law), Intel employed 116,900 people globally as of 2024 and generated $54.2 billion in revenue. After decades as the undisputed leader of the semiconductor industry — supplying the CPUs that powered every IBM PC and subsequent PC generation — Intel faced a dramatic reckoning in the 2020s as TSMC pulled ahead in advanced manufacturing, AMD gained server market share, and NVIDIA dominated the AI chip boom. Intel is now executing an ambitious "IDM 2.0" strategy to regain process leadership and build a global foundry business.

## Timeline

### 1968–1975: Founding and the Microprocessor Revolution

- **July 18, 1968**: Robert Noyce and Gordon Moore leave Fairchild Semiconductor and found **Intel Corporation** (originally named "NM Electronics") with $2.5 million in venture capital from仙童's venture capital arm. Noyce serves as chairman; Moore as vice chairman.
- **1969**: Intel's first product is the **3101 Schottky TTL memory** — a high-speed RAM chip. Competes directly with bipolar RAM market dominated by Fairchild and Signetics.
- **1971**: Intel introduces the **4004** — the world's first commercially available microprocessor (4-bit, 740 kHz, 2,250 transistors, designed for Busicom calculator). Sold to Busicom for $60,000 plus royalties. The 4004 proved that multiple transistors could fit on a single silicon chip, inventing the microprocessor concept.
- **1972**: **8008** processor (8-bit) released — first 8-bit microprocessor. Used in early terminals and embedded systems.
- **1972**: **1101** — first CMOS memory chip with silicon gate technology (replaced the earlier MOS technology).
- **1974**: **8080** released — a radical step forward (8-bit, 2 MHz, 6,000 transistors). Priced at $150. The 8080 powered the Altair 8800 (first personal computer kit, 1975), directly inspiring the PC revolution and Bill Gates/Paul Allen's BASIC interpreter for Altair. It also powered the original arcade game Space Invaders.
- **1974**: AMD founded as a "second source" Intel chip manufacturer — a partnership that would become deeply competitive.

### 1976–1985: x86 Architecture and the IBM PC Deal

- **1976**: **8085** (8-bit) released — simplified 8080 with integrated serial communication. Became enormously popular in embedded industrial applications.
- **1978**: **8086** released — Intel's first 16-bit microprocessor (5 MHz, 29,000 transistors). Marked the beginning of the x86 architecture lineage.
- **1979**: **8088** — a cost-reduced variant of 8086 with 8-bit external data bus, chosen by IBM for the **IBM PC** (1981). The 8088's selection as the IBM PC's CPU was a defining moment — it established x86 as the world's dominant computing standard and locked in Intel's position for decades. IBM's choice was partly pragmatic: Intel agreed to sell the 8088 at $18 per chip while Motorola's 68000 cost $138.
- **1982**: **80286** released — the IBM PC AT's CPU. Supported protected mode memory addressing.
- **1982**: **Intel Above the PC** strategy launched — a marketing campaign to sell Intel chips for non-PC applications (embedded, industrial). Preceded the Intel Inside campaign.
- **1982**: IBM pressured Intel to license the 80286 architecture to AMD — creating the x86 licensing structure that would later allow AMD to become a major competitor.

### 1985–1996: DRAM Exit and the Microsoft Partnership

- **1985**: Under CEO Andy Grove, Intel makes the **strategic decision to exit the DRAM memory business**. Japanese manufacturers (NEC, Toshiba, Hitachi) had flooded the market with DRAMs at below-cost prices, making Intel's DRAM business unprofitable. This pivot freed Intel to focus entirely on microprocessors — the decision that defined Intel's next three decades. Grove later called this "the most important strategic move" Intel ever made.
- **1985**: **80386** released — the first 32-bit x86 processor (275,000 transistors). Introduced the "386" as a category; the term "IBM PC/AT compatible" became the standard for all future PCs.
- **1986**: Intel posts first quarterly loss. Under Grove's leadership, Intel undergoes major restructuring, laying off 7,500 workers (about 15% of workforce). A critical moment that steeled Intel's management culture.
- **1987**: **Andy Grove** becomes president and CEO. Grove was a refugee from Hungary who survived the Holocaust; his management philosophy (focused on strategic inflection points) shaped Intel's culture.
- **1989**: **80486** released (25 MHz, 1.2 million transistors). The 486 was the first Intel chip with a built-in math coprocessor (FPU), dramatically speeding up floating-point calculations. The DX2 overdrive chip introduced clock-doubling technology.
- **1989**: Intel and Microsoft begin the formal **Wintel** partnership — coordinated development to ensure Windows ran best on Intel processors. This informal alliance defined PC computing for 20 years and drew antitrust scrutiny from the US and EU.

### 1993–2000: Pentium, the Internet Boom, and Peak Dominance

- **1993**: **Pentium** (80586) released, named after billions (penta-). 60-66 MHz, 3.1 million transistors, built in 0.8 micron process. The Pentium brand became synonymous with Intel's computing dominance. Originally priced at $1,200 for the 60 MHz version.
- **1993**: **586** processor officially renamed "Pentium" after Intel could not trademark numbers (80586). A critical lesson in IP strategy.
- **1994**: **Pentium FDIV bug** — a floating-point division error in certain calculations. Intel initially dismissed it, then recalled the chip at a cost of $475 million. This was Intel's first major PR crisis and foreshadowed later stumbles.
- **1994**: IBM signs landmark agreement to use Intel processors and Microsoft Windows as the standard PC configuration — cementing the Wintel ecosystem.
- **1997**: **Pentium II** released (7.5 million transistors, slot cartridge design). Intel's processor divisions became organized around the "Intel Architecture Labs" (IAL) and "Microprocessor Products" groups.
- **1997**: **Intel Inside** advertising program launched — a joint marketing program with PC OEMs (Dell, HP, Compaq, IBM) that made "Intel Inside" one of the world's most recognized brands. At its peak, Intel spent $500M/year on co-marketing. This fundamentally changed consumer expectations about processor brands.
- **1997**: Intel acquired **Digital Equipment Corporation's semiconductor division** for $625 million — gaining the StrongARM processor architecture (which later became the basis for Intel's XScale processors used in early Palm PDAs and Windows Mobile devices).
- **1998**: Andy Grove steps down as CEO; **Craig Barrett** becomes CEO. Grove remains chairman until 2005.
- **1999**: **Pentium III** released (9.5 million transistors, 0.18 micron). Introduction of SIMD instructions (SSE) for multimedia acceleration.
- **1999**: Intel's market capitalization briefly exceeds **$300 billion**, making it the world's most valuable company.
- **2000**: **Pentium 4** launched (42 million transistors, NetBurst architecture). Intel's peak market cap reached **$200 billion** during the dot-com bubble. The Pentium 4 ran at increasingly high clock speeds (up to 3.8 GHz) but AMD's Athlon often matched or beat it in real-world performance.
- **2000**: **Itanium** processor (joint with HP) released — a 64-bit architecture designed for enterprise servers. The Itanium project cost Intel billions and consumed years of R&D, but ultimately failed commercially as x86-64 (AMD's competing 64-bit extension) won the market. The Itanium debacle is considered one of Intel's most costly strategic mistakes.

### 2001–2010: The Multi-Core Era and the Mobile Miss

- **2001**: Intel's McAfee acquisition (announced $7.68B, completed 2011 after regulatory delays) — entering the security software market. McAfee was later sold to TPG in 2016 for $4.2B, one of Intel's few unsuccessful acquisitions.
- **2005**: **Paul Otellini** becomes CEO — the first non-immigrant American to lead Intel. Otellini joined Intel in 1974 and rose through the sales and marketing organization, bringing a consumer-oriented perspective.
- **2005**: Apple announces **Macs will switch from PowerPC to Intel processors** (Jobs at WWDC 2005). This was a landmark deal — Apple had been a RISC/PowerPC architecture holdout. Intel paid Apple hundreds of millions of dollars in incentives per the biography "Steve Jobs" by Walter Isaacson.
- **2006**: Intel's **Core 2 Duo** released (Yonah microarchitecture). Widely considered the best processor Intel had made in years, recapturing performance leadership from AMD's K8 architecture. The Core 2 Duo also had dramatically lower power consumption, enabling thin-and-light laptops.
- **2006**: **Justin Fitzpatrick** (notable Intel executive) drives IDF (Intel Developer Forum) as the premier semiconductor industry event.
- **2006**: Intel announces **$3.5 billion** investment in Vietnam — one of the largest foreign investments in Vietnam's history.
- **2007**: Intel launches **Atom** processor — a low-power chip designed for netbooks and mobile devices. However, Intel missed the smartphone revolution entirely; Atom chips were too power-hungry and expensive compared to ARM-based chips from Qualcomm, Samsung, and Apple.
- **2007**: **Ultrabook** specification launched — Intel's $300M fund to push PC makers toward thin, light, fast laptops similar to Apple's MacBook Air. This was Intel's response to the threat from tablets and smartphones.
- **2009**: Intel fined $1.45 billion by EU for anti-competitive rebates given to OEMs who exclusively used Intel chips — the largest antitrust fine in EU history at the time.
- **2010**: Otellini turns down **Apple's request to manufacture Apple's A-series chips** (designed by Apple's own silicon team). According to reports, Otellini declined because the margins were too thin compared to Intel's PC processor business. This decision cost Intel the chance to manufacture chips for the iPhone and iPad — a market that Apple eventually dominated. One of the most consequential missed opportunities in semiconductor history.

### 2011–2019: Leadership Crisis and the AMD Comeback

- **2011**: **John "Brian" Krzanich** becomes COO, later CEO (2013). A manufacturing-oriented executive who spent decades running Intel's fab operations.
- **2012**: Intel **IDM (Integrated Device Manufacturer) model** begins showing strain. TSMC had closed the process technology gap with Intel's 22nm node by 2012.
- **2013**: Brian Krzanich becomes CEO. He immediately ends Intel's participation in the Windows RT program (ARM-based Windows 8 tablets) — signaling continued mobile strategy failures.
- **2014**: Intel's **14nm process** launch was delayed by over 6 months — the first major process node delay in Intel's history. This allowed TSMC to catch up more quickly than expected.
- **2015**: Intel acquires **Altera** for $16.7 billion — the largest acquisition in Intel's history. Altera provided field-programmable gate arrays (FPGAs), which Intel planned to integrate with Xeon processors for data center acceleration.
- **2016**: Intel acquires **Nervana Systems** ($350M) — an AI chip startup. This was Intel's first major move into AI hardware. Nervana's chips were later overshadowed by NVIDIA's GPU dominance in AI training.
- **2016**: Intel's market cap falls to **$140 billion** — down from $300B at peak — as investors worried about process technology delays and mobile failures.
- **2017**: Brian Krzanich resigns amid a scandal involving a consensual relationship with an Intel employee. **Bob Swan** becomes interim CEO (later permanent CEO 2019).
- **2017**: Intel launches **Xeon Scalable** processor family (Skylake-SP) for data center — now the backbone of cloud computing.
- **2017**: Intel ships **Intel Core i9** — the first consumer 18-core/36-thread desktop processor, aimed at gaming and content creation.
- **2018**: Intel celebrates **50th anniversary** of the microprocessor (4004). At an event, Intel CEO Bob Swan acknowledges the challenges Intel faces in process technology.
- **2019**: AMD's **Zen 2 architecture** (Ryzen 3000 series) launched on TSMC's 7nm process — dramatically outperforming Intel's 14nm+++ Cascade Lake processors in both performance and efficiency. AMD began taking meaningful server market share from Intel for the first time in over a decade.

### 2020–Present: The Pat Gellinger Era and Foundry Ambitions

- **2020**: Intel's market cap fell to **$220 billion** (from $300B peak), overtaken by NVIDIA (which reached $300B). Intel's 7nm process was delayed again, now expected for 2022-2023.
- **2020**: Intel announces intention to **outsource some chip production to TSMC and Samsung** — a stunning reversal for a company that had long prided itself on internal manufacturing. The decision acknowledged that Intel's fabs had fallen behind TSMC's 5nm process.
- **2021**: **Pat Gellinger** becomes CEO (January). A longtime Intel executive who previously ran the Data Center Group, Gellinger launched Intel's "IDM 2.0" strategy — maintaining internal manufacturing while also using external foundries and building a new Intel Foundry Services (IFS) business.
- **2021**: Intel announces **$20 billion** investment in new Arizona fabs (two new fabs in Chandler, Arizona).
- **2021**: Intel announces **$80 billion** investment in European semiconductor manufacturing — including a major new fab in Magdeburg, Germany (announced 2022, planned for 2023 construction start).
- **2022**: Intel's **Sapphire Rapids** Xeon Scalable processors launched (4th Gen Xeon Scalable). Late to market and faced competitive pressure from AMD EPYC Genoa.
- **2022**: Intel's **Alder Lake** 12th Gen Core processors (hybrid architecture with performance and efficiency cores) launched for desktops — recapturing some gaming performance leadership from AMD.
- **2023**: Intel's **4th Gen Xeon Scalable (Sapphire Rapids)** ramped up data center deployments. Intel launched **Gaudi 2** AI accelerator — a direct challenge to NVIDIA's H100 GPU for AI training workloads. Gaudi 2 showed promising performance but had limited market share.
- **2023**: Intel's **Intel Foundry Services (IFS)** secured **$10 billion** in US government funding under the CHIPS Act — part of a broader effort to reshore semiconductor manufacturing to the US.
- **2024**: Intel's revenue was **$54.2 billion** (up 9% YoY) with net income of $4.6 billion. However, Intel's data center business continued facing headwinds from AMD and custom silicon from hyperscalers (Google TPU, Amazon Graviton, Microsoft Maia).
- **2024**: Intel's foundry business (IFS) posted an operating loss of **$7 billion** — highlighting the enormous challenge of building a competitive contract manufacturing business against TSMC.
- **2025**: Intel announced a partnership with **Microsoft** to manufacture Microsoft-designed chips using Intel's 18A process node. This was a significant win for Intel Foundry Services and a test of whether Intel could compete at the leading edge.
- **2025**: Intel's market cap was approximately **$85-90 billion**, roughly equal to AMD's market cap — a far cry from the company's historical dominance over AMD.
- **2025**: Intel announced **plans to spin off or sell its Altera FPGA business** — continuing to divest non-core assets.
- **2026**: Intel continued to invest heavily in AI-optimized processors (Lunar Lake for AI PCs, Granite Rapids for servers) while its foundry ambitions remained the central strategic question for the company's future.

## Key Products and Business Segments

### Client Computing Group (CCG)
- **Core processors** (i3, i5, i7, i9) — consumer and business desktops and laptops
- **Pentium and Celeron** — entry-level desktops and laptops
- **Atom** — low-power processors for netbooks, tablets (discontinued), and embedded systems
- **Xeon W** — workstation processors

### Data Center and AI (DCAI)
- **Xeon Scalable** (4th Gen Sapphire Rapids, 5th Gen Emerald Rapids) — the backbone of enterprise servers and cloud computing
- **Xeon CPU Max** — high-bandwidth memory integrated CPUs
- **Gaudi 2 and Gaudi 3** — AI training accelerators positioned against NVIDIA H100/H200
- **Intel DL Boost** — built-in AI acceleration in Xeon processors
- **FPGA products** (Intel Agilex, Stratix) — from Altera acquisition

### Network and Edge
- **Intel Atom P4/P5** — network processors for 5G infrastructure
- **Intel Ethernet** — network interface controllers and adapters
- **Edge platforms** — IoT and edge computing processors

### Intel Foundry Services (IFS)
- **18A process node** — Intel's most advanced process, targeting 2025 production
- **Intel 3/Intel 4** — EUV-based processes for advanced chips
- **Advanced packaging** — Foveros 3D stacking, EMIB interconnect technology
