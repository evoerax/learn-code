# Broadcom

Broadcom Inc. is a global infrastructure technology company headquartered in San Jose, California, built through one of the most aggressive acquisition strategies in corporate history. The company traces its roots to **Hewlett-Packard's Semiconductor Products Division**, which was spun off in 1961 as **HP Labs** and eventually became **Agilent Technologies** in 2000. In 1999, HP spun off its chip business as **Avago Technologies**, which then became the foundation of the current Broadcom through a relentless series of acquisitions. In 2016, Singapore-based Avago Technologies acquired Broadcom Corporation (the original US semiconductor company founded in 1991) for $37 billion, and renamed the combined entity Broadcom Inc. The resulting company — led by CEO Hock Tan — became one of the world's largest semiconductor companies by revenue. The acquisition spree continued with CA Technologies (2018, $19B), Symantec (2019, $10.7B), and most dramatically VMware (2023, $69B — the largest technology acquisition in history at the time). As of 2024, Broadcom generates $47.6 billion in annual revenue and has become a dominant player in enterprise infrastructure — spanning semiconductors, networking, storage, mainframe software, and cloud virtualization.

## Timeline

### 1961–1999: HP Origins and the Avago Spin-Off

- **1961**: HP's Semiconductor Products Division was established as part of Hewlett-Packard. This division designed and manufactured custom semiconductors for HP's own test equipment, computers, and medical devices.
- **1999**: HP spun off its chip division as **Agilent Technologies** in an IPO. Agilent retained HP's test and measurement instruments business while the chip division operated within Agilent. James G. Cullen was CEO of Agilent.
- **2000**: Agilent's semiconductor business was spun off again and acquired by a private equity consortium led by **Kohlberg Kravis Roberts (KKR)** and **Silver Lake Partners** for $2.7 billion. This spin-off was named **Avago Technologies** and was headquartered in Singapore.
- **2005**: **Hock Tan** (a Malaysian-born, MIT-educated finance executive who had been CEO of Avago since 2005) led Avago through a dramatic restructuring after a 54% stock price crash in 2005. Tan dramatically cut costs and transformed Avago from a broad semiconductor company into a focused infrastructure semiconductor supplier.

### 2006–2015: The Aggressive Acquisition Strategy

- **2008**: Avago acquired **Infineon Technologies' Wireless Chip Business** for $5.5 billion — positioning itself in mobile phone chips (Bluetooth, GPS, RF front-end modules). This acquisition became foundational to Avago's wireless business, which later supplied Apple's iPhone with RF filtering components.
- **2013**: Avago acquired **LSI Corporation** for $6.6 billion — a major deal that added storage controller chips (used in data centers) and network chips to Avago's portfolio. LSI was the company that made the SAS and SATA controller chips used in virtually every server.
- **2014**: Avago acquired **PLX Technology** (a PCIe switching chip company) for $300 million — adding high-speed data center interconnect chips.
- **2015**: Avago acquired **Emulex Corporation** (a data center networking and storage connectivity company) for $609 million — further expanding Avago's data center infrastructure chip business.

### 2016: The Broadcom Merger — A $130 Billion Company

- **2016**: The pivotal moment: Singapore-registered **Avago Technologies** acquired **Broadcom Corporation** (the original US semiconductor company founded in 1991 by Hock Kwan) for **$37 billion** in cash and stock. The combined company was renamed **Broadcom Inc.** and moved its headquarters to San Jose, California.
- The deal was financed through $17 billion in new debt and $10 billion in cash from Avago's balance sheet. Broadcom Corporation's founder Hock Kwan became CEO of the combined entity.
- The merger combined Avago's wireless and infrastructure chip businesses with Broadcom Corporation's networking, broadband, and set-top box chip businesses — creating the world's largest semiconductor company by revenue at the time.
- **2016**: After the merger, Hock Tan announced he would **move Broadcom's domicile to the United States** — reincorporating as a US company. This was a significant corporate tax optimization strategy (Singapore had higher corporate tax rates than the US at the time).

### 2017–2020: Software Acquisitions and the McAfee Episode

- **2017**: Broadcom attempted to acquire **Qualcomm** for **$130 billion** — which would have been the largest technology acquisition in history. President Donald Trump blocked the acquisition in March 2017 via an executive order citing national security concerns (Broadcom had ties to Singapore and concerns about 5G leadership). This was a major setback for Hock Tan's M&A strategy.
- **2017**: Broadcom announced the acquisition of **Qualcomm's rival** through a different route, then pivoted to software.
- **2018**: Broadcom acquired **CA Technologies** for **$19 billion** — a software company that made mainframe and enterprise software (developer tools, database management, IT operations). The deal was controversial because it seemed like a major strategic pivot (hardware to software) and was criticized by analysts as an unusual move for a chip company. Hock Tan's logic: CA's profitable software business would provide stable cash flow to fund more chip acquisitions.
- **2018**: Broadcom acquired **Symantec Corporation's** enterprise security business for **$10.7 billion** — Broadcom's second major software acquisition in a year. This move into cybersecurity was equally surprising to analysts but followed the same logic: profitable software with sticky enterprise customers.
- **2019**: Broadcom sold the **Symantec enterprise security business** to **Broadcom** (as a new entity backed by investors including Thoma Bravo) — after failing to integrate it profitably. This was one of Broadcom's rare divestitures.
- **2019**: Broadcom's revenue crossed **$30 billion** — driven largely by the CA and Symantec additions.

### 2020–2023: COVID, AI Boom, and the VMware Deal

- **2020**: COVID-19 pandemic caused an initial slowdown but also accelerated cloud adoption, which benefited Broadcom's data center chip and storage businesses.
- **2020**: Broadcom launched a **$20 billion share buyback** program — returning capital to shareholders as the pandemic boosted semiconductor demand (from data centers and remote work infrastructure).
- **2021**: Broadcom's revenue reached **$27.5 billion** as the AI chip boom began benefiting its data center networking business.
- **2021**: Broadcom's chips were found in **all major cloud providers' AI infrastructure** — Ethernet switches, SerDes chips, and custom ASICs for AI acceleration.
- **2022**: Broadcom developed a **custom AI accelerator chip for Google** (the TPU successor architecture) — deepening its role as a key chip supplier to hyperscalers. Broadcom became known as the "stealth" AI chip winner — supplying custom chips to Google, Meta, and others while its competitors focused on standard products.
- **2022**: Broadcom's market cap briefly exceeded **$500 billion** — making it one of the most valuable semiconductor companies in the world.

### 2023: The VMware Acquisition

- **2023**: Broadcom completed its acquisition of **VMware** for **$69 billion** — the largest technology acquisition in history at the time (larger than even Microsoft's Activision Blizzard deal). The deal was announced in May 2022 and closed in November 2023 after regulatory approval in the US, UK, EU, and China.
- **VMware** was the pioneer of server virtualization (founded 1998 by Diane Greene, Mendel Rosenblum, Scott Devine, and Edouard Bugnion at Stanford). VMware's vSphere hypervisor fundamentally changed how enterprise data centers operated — allowing multiple virtual machines to run on a single physical server, dramatically improving hardware utilization.
- Broadcom paid **$138.23 per VMware share** — a 44% premium over VMware's pre-announcement price.
- The acquisition was financed through a combination of new debt ($28B) and cash on hand. Broadcom took on significant leverage, with total debt reaching approximately $40 billion post-acquisition.
- **2023**: Post-acquisition, Broadcom announced plans to **raise VMware prices significantly** and bundle its own infrastructure software with VMware's virtualization platform — leading to widespread customer backlash. Many VMware customers began evaluating alternatives (including Nutanix, Red Hat, and open-source alternatives like Proxmox).

### 2024–2025: Post-VMware Integration and AI Infrastructure

- **2024**: Broadcom's FY2024 revenue was **$47.6 billion** — with VMware contributing approximately $10 billion of that. The company reported strong free cash flow of approximately $17 billion.
- **2024**: Broadcom's AI-related semiconductor revenue (custom ASICs for hyperscalers, Ethernet switches for AI clusters, and networking) reached **$11+ billion** — representing the fastest-growing segment.
- **2024**: Broadcom's custom AI chips were used in **major hyperscaler AI deployments** — Google, Meta, and other cloud providers used Broadcom's chips in their AI training and inference infrastructure. Broadcom was widely regarded as the leading beneficiary of AI infrastructure spending after NVIDIA.
- **2024**: Broadcom CEO Hock Tan became one of the **highest-paid CEOs in America** — with total compensation exceeding $150 million in FY2023 (making him one of the highest-paid executives in the S&P 500).
- **2025**: Broadcom faced increasing regulatory scrutiny in the EU over its VMware pricing practices. European cloud providers filed complaints about Broadcom's new licensing terms, prompting an EU investigation into potential abuse of a dominant market position.
- **2025**: Broadcom announced it was exploring options for its **mainframe software business** (CA Technologies heritage) — potentially spinning it off or selling it — as part of portfolio optimization post-VMware.
- **2025**: Broadcom's market cap was approximately **$550-600 billion** — making it one of the top 10 most valuable US companies.

## Key Products and Business Segments

### Semiconductor Solutions
- **Custom ASICs** — chips designed for specific hyperscalers (Google, Meta, others); Broadcom is the leading merchant vendor of custom AI chips
- **Ethernet Switches and Silicon** — for data center networking (Tomahawk, Jericho series); used in AI training clusters
- **SerDes and Physical Layer Chips** — for high-speed data transmission in data centers
- **RF Front-End Modules** — for smartphones (Broadcom is Apple's primary RF filter supplier for iPhone)
- **FBAR Filters** — Bulk Acoustic Wave filters for mobile devices (key differentiator in 5G phones)
- **Network Adapters and Controllers** — for servers and storage

### Infrastructure Software
- **VMware** (post-2023 acquisition) — vSphere, vSAN, NSX, VMware Cloud Foundation; dominant hypervisor and virtualization platform
- **CA Technologies** (Mainframe software) — mainframe management and security software (JCL, scheduler, database tools)
- **Broadcom's Enterprise Software Portfolio** — Test and Quality Management, API Management, DevOps tools

## Corporate Governance

- **CEO**: Hock Tan (since 2005; architect of the Avago/Broadcom acquisition strategy)
- **Chairman**: Hock Tan
- **Headcount**: ~40,000 employees (2024), including approximately 13,000 from VMware
- **Headquarters**: San Jose, California, USA (reincorporated in US in 2018 from Singapore)
- Notable for Hock Tan's aggressive financial discipline: Broadcom has undergone 7 CEOs in 15 years, reflecting Tan's M&A-driven leadership approach and high executive turnover.

## Financial Highlights

- **Revenue (FY2024)**: $47.6 billion
- **Free Cash Flow (FY2024)**: ~$17 billion
- **Net Debt**: ~$40 billion (post-VMware acquisition)
- **Market Cap (early 2025)**: ~$580 billion
- **AI-related semiconductor revenue**: ~$11 billion (2024)
- **Key customers**: Apple (RF filters for iPhone), Google (custom ASICs), Meta (custom ASICs), all major cloud providers (networking chips)
- Broadcom has one of the highest operating margins in the semiconductor industry (~55-60% on semiconductor segment).
