# Microsoft

Microsoft Corporation (Nasdaq: MSFT) is an American technology company founded in 1975 and headquartered in Redmond, Washington. It is the world's largest software company by revenue and the third-most valuable company by market capitalization (~$3 trillion as of 2025). Founded by **Bill Gates** and **Paul Allen**, Microsoft dominated personal computing through its Windows operating system and Microsoft Office productivity suite. Under CEO **Satya Nadella** (since 2014), the company executed a historic transformation from a software licensor into a cloud computing powerhouse, with **Microsoft Azure** becoming the world's second-largest cloud platform (after AWS). Key segments include: Intelligent Cloud (Azure, Server Products), Productivity (Microsoft 365, LinkedIn), and More Personal Computing (Windows, Xbox, Surface, Bing). The 2023 acquisition of **Activision Blizzard** for $68.7 billion made it the largest gaming acquisition in history. Nadella's leadership also drove a cultural shift toward openness (open-source, Linux) and AI partnership (OpenAI's ChatGPT integrated deeply across Microsoft products).

## Timeline

### 1975–1984: Founding and the IBM Contract

- **April 4, 1975**: Microsoft was founded by **Bill Gates** (19, Harvard dropout) and **Paul Allen** (22, MIT graduate) in Albuquerque, New Mexico — originally named "Micro-Soft" (microcomputer software). Their first product: **Altair BASIC** — an interpreter for the Altair 8800 (the first personal computer), which they wrote in just 8 weeks.
- **1975**: Microsoft sold its first product — Altair BASIC — for $3,000 per copy (or $30 per copy royalty), earning ~$18,000 in first-year revenue.
- **1979**: Microsoft moved headquarters from Albuquerque to Seattle, Washington.
- **1980**: **August 12, 1980**: Microsoft signed a pivotal contract with **IBM** to provide the operating system for the upcoming **IBM PC** — **MS-DOS** (sourced from Seattle Computer Products, renamed MS-DOS after Microsoft bought QDOS for $50,000). IBM's PC became the industry standard, and MS-DOS became the dominant PC operating system. This was Microsoft's ticket to dominance — every PC manufacturer needed MS-DOS.
- **1981**: Microsoft incorporated as **Microsoft Corporation** on April 25, 1981.

### 1985–1994: Windows and the Office Empire

- **November 20, 1985**: Released **Microsoft Windows** 1.0 — a graphical operating environment that ran on top of MS-DOS. It took several more years before Windows 3.0 (1990) made the GUI mainstream.
- **1986**: Microsoft relocated to its current campus in **Redmond, Washington**. March 13, 1986: Microsoft went **public on NASDAQ** at $21 per share, raising $61 million. The IPO made many employees millionaires. Gates retained ~45% ownership.
- **1990**: Released **Windows 3.0** — a massive commercial success that made Windows the dominant PC GUI. Also released **Microsoft Office** (initially for Macintosh, then Windows) — bundling Word, Excel, and PowerPoint.
- **1990**: Microsoft revenue reached **$1 billion** for the first time.
- **1993**: Released **Windows NT** — an enterprise-grade operating system designed for business networks. Windows NT eventually merged with the consumer Windows line (Windows XP in 2001).
- **1994**: US **Department of Justice** filed an antitrust lawsuit against Microsoft — alleging bundling of Internet Explorer with Windows was anticompetitive. This was the first major legal challenge to Microsoft's market power.

### 1995–2000: The Internet Memo and Ballmer Era

- **May 26, 1995**: Bill Gates issued the famous **"Internet Tidal Wave" memo** to all Microsoft managers — declaring the internet the highest priority and redirecting company strategy almost overnight. This was a pivotal moment: Microsoft had initially dismissed the internet as unimportant, and Gates personally corrected course.
- **August 24, 1995**: Released **Windows 95** — the most significant Microsoft product release since DOS. It sold **1 million copies in 4 days** and included the Start button, taskbar, and plug-and-play. Windows 95 established Microsoft's OS dominance for a decade.
- **1996**: Microsoft released **Internet Explorer** (bundled free with Windows 95) — within 3 years it dominated the browser market, nearly putting Netscape Navigator out of business and triggering the famous "Browser Wars" antitrust case.
- **1997**: Bill Gates promoted **Steve Ballmer** (joined Microsoft in 1980) to president, making him effectively Gates' right-hand man.
- **1998**: Released **Windows 98** — first Windows designed for consumer internet use.
- **1999**: Released **Windows 2000** — major enterprise OS built on NT technology.
- **2000**: **Steve Ballmer** became CEO on January 13, 2000, replacing Bill Gates (who remained as chairman and "chief software architect"). Ballmer ran Microsoft for 14 years.

### 2001–2007: Xbox, Windows XP, and Vista

- **October 25, 2001**: Released **Windows XP** — one of Microsoft's longest-lived and most-used operating systems (mainstream support lasted until 2009; extended support until 2014). Windows XP shipped with **Internet Explorer 6** and became the dominant OS for business and consumer PCs worldwide.
- **November 15, 2001**: Released the first **Xbox** game console — Microsoft's entry into the $30 billion gaming market. The original Xbox competed with Sony's PlayStation 2 and Nintendo's GameCube. Microsoft invested billions in Xbox hardware (selling each console at a loss initially) to secure a seat in the living room. Acquired game studios to build exclusive titles.
- **2002**: Released **Windows XP Professional x64** — first Windows for 64-bit processors.
- **November 2002**: Released **Xbox Live** — the first major online gaming network for consoles. Xbox Live became a huge success and established Microsoft as a serious player in online gaming services.
- **2005**: Released **Xbox 360** — a major leap over the original Xbox with HD graphics and a strong slate of exclusive games (Halo, Gears of War, Forza). Xbox 360 sold ~84 million units.
- **January 30, 2007**: Released **Windows Vista** — the successor to Windows XP. Vista was widely criticized for performance issues, UAC prompts, and driver incompatibility. It is considered one of Microsoft's less successful OS releases.
- **2007**: Microsoft attempted to acquire **Yahoo!** for $44.6 billion — Yahoo rejected the offer. Microsoft then walked away rather than pursue a hostile takeover.

### 2008–2014: Cloud First, Mobile Miss

- **October 27, 2008**: Released **Windows Azure** (later renamed **Microsoft Azure**) — Microsoft's entry into cloud computing. Azure originally launched as "Windows Azure" and was designed to compete with Amazon Web Services (AWS) which had launched in 2002 and was already dominant.
- **2009**: Released **Windows 7** (October 22) — the direct successor to Vista, fixing most of Vista's performance and usability issues. Windows 7 was extremely well-received and became the dominant business OS for a decade.
- **October 2010**: Released **Windows Phone 7** — Microsoft's re-entry into smartphone OS. Despite innovative design (metro UI, live tiles), Windows Phone never gained significant market share against Android and iOS. Microsoft eventually exited the smartphone hardware business.
- **2011**: Acquired **Skype** for approximately **$8.5 billion** — Microsoft's largest acquisition at the time. Skype was integrated into Xbox, Outlook, and Windows Phone, but the integration was not always smooth and Skype never fully delivered on its promised synergies.
- **2012**: Released **Windows 8** — a dramatic redesign that removed the Start button and emphasized touch-friendly tiles. Windows 8 was divisive and widely disliked by desktop users; Microsoft had to release **Windows 8.1** to restore the Start button and desktop-oriented features.
- **June 18, 2012**: Released the first **Microsoft Surface** tablet — a hardware reference design to showcase Windows 8 capabilities. The Surface Pro (full Windows PC in tablet form) became a successful enterprise device. Surface revenue grew to over $6 billion annually.
- **September 3, 2013**: Announced acquisition of **Nokia's devices and services division** for approximately **$7.2 billion** — bringing Nokia's Lumia smartphone line under Microsoft. Microsoft discontinued the Nokia brand shortly after and eventually wrote off most of the acquisition value (2015: ~$7.6 billion impairment charge). This was one of Microsoft's least successful acquisitions.

### 2014–2017: Satya Nadella Era — Cloud and Open Source

- **February 4, 2014**: **Satya Nadella** became CEO — replacing Steve Ballmer. Nadella, a 22-year Microsoft veteran who had led the Azure cloud and Server & Tools businesses, signaled an immediate cultural shift with his first book: "**Hit Refresh**" (2017). Nadella's three strategic pillars: mobile-first/cloud-first, embracing open source, and building compassionate AI.
- **July 29, 2015**: Released **Windows 10** — designed as "one Windows for all devices" (PC, tablet, phone, Xbox, HoloLens). Windows 10 was offered as a free upgrade for Windows 7/8 users to help accelerate adoption. It became the most widely-used Windows version.
- **2015**: Microsoft crossed **$1 trillion market cap** for the first time (April 2019 actually, though the date varies by accounting of stock price crossing the threshold).
- **September 2014**: Acquired **Mojang** (creator of Minecraft) for **$2.5 billion** — Minecraft became one of the best-selling video games of all time (~300 million copies sold).
- **June 13, 2016**: Acquired **LinkedIn** for **$26.2 billion** — Microsoft's second-largest acquisition. LinkedIn (with ~450 million users at the time) operated as an independent subsidiary under Microsoft, with tight integration into Microsoft 365 (Outlook, Teams). LinkedIn has since grown to over 1 billion members and became profitable.
- **2016**: Microsoft joined the **Open Source Initiative** and began shipping Linux inside Windows (Windows Subsystem for Linux/WSL). This was a dramatic reversal from Gates' famous 2001 "Linux is a cancer" memo. Nadella declared Microsoft "loves Linux."

### 2018–2021: GitHub, OpenAI, and the Gaming Empire

- **June 4, 2018**: Acquired **GitHub** for **$7.5 billion** — the world's largest software development platform (25 million developers). GitHub operated independently under Microsoft, preserving its open-source-friendly culture despite acquisition.
- **July 2018**: Microsoft's market cap reached **$800 billion** — recovering from the mobile-first missteps of the Ballmer era.
- **2019**: Microsoft invested **$1 billion** in **OpenAI** — the San Francisco AI research company founded by Elon Musk, Sam Altman, and others. This investment gave Microsoft exclusive rights to commercialize OpenAI's technologies.
- **2020**: Released **Microsoft Teams** (a Slack competitor) — it became the dominant video conferencing and collaboration platform during the COVID-19 pandemic. Teams grew from ~44 million daily active users in March 2020 to over 300 million by 2023.
- **September 2020**: Agreed to acquire **ZeniMax Media** (parent of Bethesda Softworks, id Software, etc.) for **$7.5 billion** — the deal closed in March 2021. This gave Microsoft ownership of major gaming franchises (The Elder Scrolls, Fallout, Doom, Wolfenstein, Starfield) and significantly strengthened its Xbox Game Studios portfolio.
- **2020**: Microsoft became the **second-largest gaming company** by revenue (after Sony) through organic growth + acquisitions.
- **2021**: Released **Windows 11** (October 5) — the first new Windows major version since 2015. Windows 11 featured a redesigned Start menu, centered taskbar, Snap layouts, and Android app support (via Amazon Appstore).

### 2022–2023: Activision Blizzard and AI Integration

- **January 18, 2022**: Microsoft announced the acquisition of **Activision Blizzard** for **$68.7 billion** — the largest acquisition in gaming history and Microsoft's largest acquisition ever. Activision Blizzard owned Call of Duty, World of Warcraft, Overwatch, Candy Crush, Diablo, and many other iconic franchises.
- **July 2022**: Microsoft CEO Satya Nadella testified in the **FTC vs. Meta/Google** antitrust case, arguing that the gaming and AI markets required scrutiny of Google's dominance.
- **December 2022**: Released **ChatGPT** (by OpenAI) — which quickly became the fastest-growing consumer application in history. Microsoft quickly integrated ChatGPT technology into Bing Search (February 2023) and later into Microsoft 365 Copilot.
- **February 2023**: Microsoft invested an additional **~$10 billion** in OpenAI — deepening the partnership that gave Microsoft exclusive cloud computing rights to run OpenAI's models on Azure. This investment reportedly valued OpenAI at ~$29 billion.
- **February 2023**: Released **Microsoft 365 Copilot** — AI-powered assistant integrated into Word, Excel, PowerPoint, Outlook, and Teams. This was a major product bet on generative AI augmenting knowledge workers.
- **2023**: Released **Bing Chat** (later Copilot) — AI-powered search assistant built on GPT-4, integrated into Bing and Edge. Microsoft positioned it as an AI-first alternative to Google Search.
- **October 13, 2023**: Completed acquisition of **Activision Blizzard** — after the UK's Competition and Markets Authority (CMA) reversed its blocking decision and approved the deal. This made Microsoft the **third-largest gaming company by revenue** (after Sony and Tencent).
- **November 2023**: Microsoft unveiled custom **AI chips** — the **Maia 100** AI accelerator (for Azure AI workloads) and the **Cobalt 100** CPU (for general cloud computing). This was part of Microsoft's strategy to develop its own silicon to reduce dependence on NVIDIA GPUs.
- **November 2023**: Microsoft hired **Sam Altman** and **Greg Brockman** for a new AI research team after OpenAI's board suddenly fired Altman. Within 5 days, Altman was reinstated as OpenAI CEO after overwhelming pressure from Microsoft (major investor) and OpenAI employees (97% threatened to leave). This incident demonstrated Microsoft's enormous leverage over OpenAI.

### 2024–2025: AI Infrastructure and Copilot Everywhere

- **2024**: Microsoft integrated **GPT-4o** into Copilot across Windows, Bing, Edge, and Microsoft 365. The AI assistant became increasingly deeply embedded across all Microsoft products.
- **2024**: Microsoft Azure announced **$10+ billion** in AI infrastructure investments — building massive data centers with NVIDIA H100 and GB200 GPUs to meet demand for AI training and inference workloads.
- **2024**: **GitHub Copilot** reached **1 million paying users** — AI-assisted code generation became mainstream for developers.
- **May 2024**: Microsoft's market cap briefly surpassed **$3 trillion** — joining Apple and NVIDIA as the only companies to reach that milestone.
- **2025**: Microsoft launched **Copilot+ PCs** — a new category of Windows PCs with dedicated NPUs (Neural Processing Units) for on-device AI. These featured the **Recall** feature (AI-powered screenshot memory) and Live Captions with real-time translation.
- **2025**: Microsoft announced **Azure Maia 2** and **Cobalt 2** — second-generation custom AI silicon.
- **2025**: Microsoft committed **$35 billion** to AI infrastructure through 2025 — signaling sustained massive investment in AI compute capacity.

## Key Products and Business Segments

### Intelligent Cloud
- **Microsoft Azure** — world's second-largest cloud platform (after AWS), with ~24% market share. Competes directly with AWS and Google Cloud Platform. Azure has ~200 data center regions globally.
- **Azure AI** — including OpenAI Service (GPT-4, DALL-E 3, Codex), Azure Machine Learning, Cognitive Services, and the AI model service.
- **SQL Server, Azure SQL** — database products.
- **Visual Studio, GitHub Enterprise** — developer tools.

### Productivity and Business Processes
- **Microsoft 365** (formerly Office 365) — the dominant productivity suite (Word, Excel, PowerPoint, Outlook, Teams). Over 400 million commercial users as of 2024.
- **LinkedIn** — professional social network with 1+ billion members, now profitable with ~$15 billion annual revenue.
- **Dynamics 365** — ERP and CRM suite competing with Salesforce.
- **Teams** — video conferencing and collaboration platform (~320 million monthly active users as of 2024).

### More Personal Computing
- **Windows** — still the dominant PC operating system (~72% desktop OS market share). Windows 11 shipped on new PCs.
- **Surface** — Microsoft's own hardware line (Surface Pro, Surface Laptop, Surface Studio, Surface Go).
- **Xbox** — gaming console (Xbox Series X/S), game subscriptions (Game Pass Ultimate with 25+ million subscribers), and cloud gaming.
- **Bing** — search engine now integrated with AI (Copilot). Bing has ~3% global search market share but is strategically important as a challenger to Google Search.

## CEO and Leadership

- **CEO**: **Satya Nadella** (since February 4, 2014) — previously led Azure and Server & Tools businesses. Instrumental in shifting Microsoft's strategy toward cloud computing and open source.
- **Chairman**: **Bill Gates** stepped down as chairman in March 2020, remaining on the board until 2020. **Reid Hoffman** (LinkedIn co-founder) became chairman in 2022.
- **CFO**: **Amy Hood** (since 2013) — one of the few female CFOs at a Fortune 100 company.
- **Former CEO**: **Steve Ballmer** (2000–2014) — replaced Gates as CEO in 2000. Ballmer expanded Microsoft's mobile efforts but missed the cloud and AI waves early. Ballmer later acquired the **Los Angeles Clippers** NBA team for $2 billion.
- **Founder**: **Bill Gates** — chairman and CEO until 2000, then "chief software architect" until 2006. Gates left day-to-day operations in 2006 but remained on the board until 2020.

## Manufacturing

Microsoft is primarily a **software and services company** — it does not manufacture hardware at scale. Xbox consoles and Surface devices are manufactured by partners (Flex, Celestica, etc.) in China and other Asian countries. Microsoft's cloud infrastructure (Azure) runs on servers built by Dell, HP Enterprise, and Microsoft itself, using INTEL, AMD, and NVIDIA processors, as well as Microsoft's own Maia and Cobalt chips.
