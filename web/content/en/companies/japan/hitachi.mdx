# Hitachi

Hitachi, Ltd. (株式会社日立製作所, TYO: 6501) is one of Japan's oldest and most diversified industrial conglomerates, headquartered in Tokyo. Founded in 1910 by electrical engineer Namihei Odaira in Hitachi, Ibaraki, Hitachi began by producing industrial motors for mining before expanding into railways, consumer electronics, semiconductors, power systems, and digital infrastructure. The company's motto "Making the world better through technology" (温故創新 — onko shinshin) reflects its engineering-driven culture. With FY2023 revenue of ¥9.7 trillion, approximately 268,655 employees, and operations spanning digital systems, energy, rail, healthcare, and industry, Hitachi is Japan's fourth-largest company by market capitalization (~¥16.9 trillion) and a constituent of the Nikkei 225 and TOPIX indices. A distinctive feature of Hitachi is its tradition of all CEOs having engineering backgrounds — all 12 CEOs in company history were engineers, eight from the University of Tokyo.

## Timeline

### 1910–1945: Founding and Early Industrial Era

- **1910**: **Namihei Odaira** (速度定性, 1870–1951), a graduate of Tokyo Imperial University, founded **Hitachi Works** in the mining town of Hitachi, Ibaraki Prefecture, to manufacture electrical machinery. His first product was a **4-kilowatt induction motor** for copper mining in the Nikkō Copper Mine — immediately proving the reliability and commercial viability of his designs.
- **1911**: Hitachi Works became independent from its founding sponsor, establishing itself as a fully self-governing manufacturing enterprise.
- **1918**: Hitachi relocated its headquarters to Tokyo — moving closer to Japan's industrial and political center.
- **1920**: The company officially adopted the name **Hitachi, Ltd.** (Hitachi meaning "daylight" — representing the goal of illuminating the world through electrical technology).
- **1924**: Hitachi built **Japan's first mainline electric locomotive** — a landmark in Japan's modernization of its railway system. This established Hitachi's decades-long leadership in railway technology.
- **1932**: Hitachi expanded into consumer products — launching Japan's first **electric refrigerator** and **home elevators**, bringing industrial manufacturing quality into the domestic market.
- **1940s**: During WWII, Hitachi shifted to military production, manufacturing radar equipment, communications gear, and aircraft components. Post-war restructuring and the Allied occupation forces removed founder Odaira from leadership in 1946, though he returned briefly in 1951 before his death that year.

### 1946–1970: Post-War Recovery and Electronics Expansion

- **1949**: Hitachi listed on the Tokyo Stock Exchange following post-war reconstruction.
- **1950s**: Hitachi expanded aggressively into the growing electronics market, developing television sets, radios, and household appliances.
- **1959**: Hitachi America established — the company's first significant overseas subsidiary, beginning Hitachi's international expansion.
- **1960**: Hitachi developed the **MARS-1** system — the world's first seat reservation system for electric trains, later adopted by Japanese National Railways and influencing global rail ticketing systems.
- **Hitachi's role in Shinkansen**: Hitachi was one of the key industrial partners in developing Japan's iconic bullet train (Shinkansen) system, providing electrical systems and components that enabled the 210 km/h service launched in 1964.
- **1970s**: Hitachi established its semiconductor division, becoming a major player in DRAM and other memory chips during the 1970s-1980s.

### 1971–2000: Semiconductor Leadership and Global Expansion

- **1970s**: Hitachi pioneered semiconductor technology with the **Twin-Well Hi-CMOS** process, becoming a major global semiconductor manufacturer. The company was one of the eight Japanese, US, and European companies that controlled the global DRAM market in the 1980s.
- **1980s**: Hitachi conducted the **famous electron double-slit experiment** in quantum mechanics — one of the few corporate labs to make fundamental physics contributions of this nature.
- **1985**: Hitachi emerged as a major player in the global semiconductor industry, competing with NEC, Toshiba, and Mitsubishi in Japan's dominant position in global DRAM manufacturing.
- **1990s**: As the global DRAM market consolidated following the 1996-1997 price crash, Hitachi strategically withdrew from commodity memory chips, focusing on specialized semiconductors and system-level integration.

### 2001–2015: Restructuring and Strategic Pivot

- **2001**: Toshihiko Matsuda became CEO and began a major restructuring program to address losses in consumer electronics and semiconductors.
- **2008**: Hitachi recorded a **record loss of ¥787.3 billion** (approximately $7.5 billion) in FY2008 — the largest loss in the company's history — driven by the global financial crisis, write-downs in semiconductor assets, and losses in consumer electronics (particularly plasma TVs and mobile phones). CEO Takashi Kawamura led an emergency restructuring.
- **2009-2010**: Under Kawamura's "Hitachi Restructuring Initiative," the company sold off numerous non-core businesses, reduced the workforce significantly, and focused on the three strategic pillars of Social Innovation Business (power/energy, healthcare, and mobility).
- **2011**: Hitachi sold its **hard disk drive (HDD) business, HGST** (formerly IBM's HDD division, acquired by Hitachi 2002), to **Western Digital for $4.3 billion** — one of the most significant tech acquisitions in Japan at the time. This exit came as the HDD market was consolidating rapidly.
- **2012**: Hitachi acquired **Horizon Nuclear Power** (UK) — marking an ambitious entry into nuclear power generation, particularly targeting the British market.
- **2014**: Hitachi formed **Mitsubishi Hitachi Power Systems (MHPS)** — a joint venture with Mitsubishi Heavy Industries combining gas and geothermal turbine businesses, creating one of the world's largest power equipment manufacturers.

### 2016–2025: Digital Transformation and Global Technology Leadership

- **2017**: Hitachi entered the EV motor market through a **partnership with Honda** — supplying traction motors for Honda's hybrid and electric vehicles. This expanded Hitachi's automotive components business significantly.
- **2018**: Hitachi acquired **80% of ABB's Power Grids division for $6.4 billion** — the largest acquisition in Hitachi's history. The unit was renamed **Hitachi-ABB Power Grids** (now **Hitachi Energy**) and gave Hitachi a dominant position in global power transmission and distribution (HVDC technology, grid automation, and electrical substations).
- **2020**: Hitachi completed the ABB acquisition and introduced the **L0-950 maglev train** — capable of 600 km/h, showcasing Hitachi's continued leadership in rail technology.
- **2021**: Hitachi formed **Hitachi Astemo** (after acquiring Honda's 33.4% stake in the existing automotive components JV) — a major global automotive components supplier specializing in electric powertrains,底盘, and advanced driver assistance systems (ADAS).
- **2021**: Hitachi acquired **GlobalLogic** for **$9.6 billion** — one of the largest software acquisitions by a Japanese company. GlobalLogic (based in California, with R&D centers in India, Eastern Europe, and Latin America) significantly strengthened Hitachi's digital services capabilities, adding 20,000+ software engineers to the group.
- **2021**: The same year, Hitachi acquired **Transport Engineering company** and expanded its digital consulting practice.
- **2024**: **Bosch announced the acquisition of the Johnson Controls-Hitachi air conditioning joint venture** (Hitachi held 40%) — a transaction that valued the AC joint venture at over ¥6 billion and was expected to close in 2025.
- **2024**: Hitachi announced it would **acquire infrastructure software firm** to strengthen its digital systems portfolio.

## Key Products and Business Segments

### Digital Systems & Services (~21.9% of FY2022 revenue)
- **Hitachi Vantara**: Storage systems (flash, hybrid), data infrastructure, hybrid cloud platforms — the heir to Hitachi's legacy HDD business
- **GlobalLogic**: Digital product engineering, UX design, and software development services
- **Hitachi Solutions**: Microsoft-based digital transformation consulting
- **Hitachi Digital**: AI and IoT platform for enterprise customers

### Green Energy and Mobility (~22.9% of FY2022 revenue)
- **Hitachi Energy** (formerly Hitachi-ABB Power Grids): HVDC transmission systems, grid automation, electrical substations, power quality solutions — the global leader in HVDC technology with 40%+ market share
- **Hitachi Rail**: Signaling systems, rolling stock, maintenance services; supplies Shinkansen and international rail operators
- **Mitsubishi Hitachi Power Systems (MHPS)**: Gas turbines, geothermal turbines, steam turbines, power plant solutions

### Connective Industries (~27.3% of FY2022 revenue)
- **Hitachi Construction Machinery**: Excavators, dump trucks, wheel loaders — global top-3 construction equipment maker
- **Building Systems**: Elevators (global top-3), escalators, building management systems
- **Healthcare**: MRI systems, CT scanners, particle therapy systems (Hitachi's Nobel Prize-linked cancer treatment technology), diagnostic equipment
- **Automotive (via Hitachi Astemo)**: EV powertrains, ADAS sensors,底盘 systems for major automakers

### Other Notable
- **Defense equipment**: Radar systems, communications equipment for Japanese Self-Defense Forces
- **Industrial components**: Bearings, motors, pumps
