# Fujitsu

Fujitsu Limited is Japan's largest IT services company and one of the world's leading providers of technology solutions, headquartered in Kawasaki, Kanagawa. Originally founded as Fuji Telecommunications Equipment Manufacturing in 1935, Fujitsu has transformed from a telecommunications equipment maker into a comprehensive computing and services powerhouse. The company manufactured Japan's first computer (FACOM 100 in 1954), developed the FR80 mainframe architecture that dominated Japanese banking systems for decades, and built the world's most powerful supercomputer Fugaku in 2020. With FY2024 revenue of ¥3.76 trillion and approximately 124,000 employees worldwide, Fujitsu operates across IT services, computing platforms, telecommunications, and electronics. The company is a constituent of the Nikkei 225 and TOPIX indices.

## Timeline

### 1935–1970: Founding and Early Growth

- **June 20, 1935**: **Fuji Telecommunications Equipment Manufacturing** established in Kawasaki, Kanagawa, as a joint venture between Fuji Electric and two other partners, focusing on telecommunications equipment manufacturing.
- **1938**: The company moved to header manufacturing for the Imperial Japanese Army — a controversial chapter that ceased after WWII.
- **1954**: Fujitsu manufactured Japan's first computer, the **FACOM 100** — a vacuum-tube computer developed in partnership with the University of Tokyo. This established Fujitsu as Japan's computing pioneer and created a decades-long computing roadmap.
- **1958**: Developed the **FACOM 220** — Japan's first transistorized computer, dramatically improving reliability over vacuum tubes.
- **1967**: Company officially renamed **Fujitsu** (dropping "Fuji Telecommunications Equipment Manufacturing"). The name change reflected the company's broadening beyond pure telecommunications into general electronics.
- **1970s**: Fujitsu established the **FACOM M series** mainframes — competing directly with IBM in the Japanese market. The FR80 architecture (launched 1972) became the de facto standard for Japanese banking and government computing for over 20 years.

### 1980–2000: Global Expansion and Restructuring

- **1986**: Established **Kainos**, a joint venture with Queen's University Belfast — Fujitsu's first significant European R&D presence.
- **1990**: Fujitsu acquired **80% of ICL** (International Computers Limited), the UK's largest computer company, for $1.29 billion. ICL was the UK's answer to IBM and had deep ties to the British government. This was one of Japan's largest tech acquisitions in Europe at the time.
- **1992**: Fujitsu introduced the world's first **21-inch full-color plasma display** — a pioneering display technology that anticipated the flat-panel TV revolution.
- **1993**: Fujitsu formed a **flash memory joint venture with AMD** — named Spansion (later spun off as a separate company and later merged into Cypress Semiconductor). This gave Fujitsu early exposure to the memory chip market.
- **1997**: Fujitsu acquired the remaining **58% of Amdahl Corporation** for approximately $850 million. Amdahl had been founded by Gene Amdahl (creator of IBM's first mainframe) and was a major IBM-compatible mainframe competitor in the US.
- **1999**: Formed **Fujitsu Siemens Computers** joint venture with Siemens AG — combining Fujitsu's computing platforms with Siemens' enterprise sales channels across Europe, creating one of Europe's major computer companies.

### 2000–2015: Client Computing and Convergence Era

- **2002**: Fujitsu sold its microelectronics division to a Matsushita-led consortium, forming **Spansion** as a separate flash memory company.
- **2004**: Fujitsu acquired the Australian subsidiary of Atos Origin — expanding its European services footprint.
- **2007**: Acquired **Infinity Solutions Ltd** in New Zealand.
- **2009**: Sold its **hard disk drive (HDD) business to Toshiba** for approximately ¥20 billion — exiting storage hardware as the market consolidated around fewer players. Simultaneously, Fujitsu acquired **Siemens' stake in Fujitsu Siemens Computers** for approximately €450 million, gaining full control and renaming it **Fujitsu Technology Solutions**.
- **2013**: Fujitsu cut **5,000 jobs** amid significant losses in its mobile phone and PC businesses. The company merged its LSI chip business with Panasonic to form **Socionext** — a new fabless semiconductor company.
- **2014**: Fujitsu sold its semiconductor manufacturing subsidiary **Mie Fujitsu Semiconductor** to **United Microelectronics Corporation (UMC)** — further exiting chip manufacturing.
- **2015**: Acquired **UShareSoft** — a cloud migration and deployment software company, strengthening Fujitsu's cloud portfolio.

### 2016–2025: Transformation and AI Leadership

- **2017**: Fujitsu established a **Client Computing joint venture with Lenovo** (51% Lenovo, 44% Fujitsu, 5% Development Bank of Japan) — combining Fujitsu's PC and notebook manufacturing with Lenovo's global distribution. The move positioned Fujitsu to exit low-margin consumer PCs while retaining enterprise computing capabilities.
- **2019**: Fujitsu began delivering **5G telecommunications equipment to NTT DOCOMO** — re-engaging with the telecom infrastructure market after years of focus on computing services.
- **2020**: Fujitsu's **Fugaku supercomputer** (developed jointly with RIKEN) became the **world's most powerful supercomputer** — a position it held for consecutive Top500 rankings. Fugaku was based on Fujitsu's proprietary **A64FX** processor and was used extensively for COVID-19 research and drug discovery.
- **2020**: Fujitsu created **Fujitsu Japan** as a new subsidiary consolidating its domestic service businesses.
- **2021**: Fujitsu launched its **Fujitsu Uvance** brand — a cross-industry initiative applying AI and hybrid IT to address societal challenges including climate change, healthcare, agriculture, and manufacturing efficiency.
- **2022**: Ricoh acquired **80% of PFU Limited** (Fujitsu's document scanner subsidiary) — Fujitsu had owned PFU since the 1990s; the divestment reflected shifting focus toward services.
- **2023**: Fujitsu announced the **Fujitsu Shimane Factory** — a new manufacturing facility for computing hardware, reinforcing domestic production capabilities.
- **2024**: Fujitsu relocated its headquarters from **Shiodome, Tokyo to Kawasaki, Kanagawa** — consolidating operations closer to its 1935 founding location and signaling a return to its roots.
- **2025**: Fujitsu announced **Kaidden** — a next-generation AI-driven IT services platform integrating its global delivery capabilities with generative AI automation.

## Key Products and Services

### Computing Platforms
- **PRIMERGY servers**: x86-based enterprise servers (塔式、机架式、刀片式) — Fujitsu's core server brand competing with Dell and HPE
- **ETERNUS storage**: Enterprise storage arrays and data protection solutions
- **Fujitsu Server PRIMEQUEST**: Mission-critical x86 servers for SAP ERP and similar workloads
- **FujitsuHPC**: High-performance computing solutions built around Fugaku-derived technologies

### IT Services
- **Infrastructure Services**: Data center management, cloud migration, managed services
- **Business Consulting**: Digital transformation consulting across financial services, manufacturing, retail, government
- **Security Services**: Fujitsu's Cybersecurity Cloud and managed security operations
- **Global Delivery**: Delivery capabilities across 90+ countries through Global Delivery Centers

### Telecommunications
- **5G network equipment**: Radio access network (RAN) solutions for telecom operators
- **Optical networking**: Fiber optic transmission systems
- **Network security**: Combined telecom/security offerings for carriers

### Software and Platforms
- **Fujitsu Enterprise Postgres**: PostgreSQL-based relational database optimized for enterprise workloads
- **Fujitsu Cloud Initiative**: Hybrid cloud management platform
- **Systemwalker**: Fujitsu's long-standing systems management software family

### Consumer/Embedded Products
- **LIFEBOOK / AMILO**: Consumer and business notebooks (through Lenovo JV)
- **fi-series / ScanSnap document scanners**: High-end business scanners (through PFU, now Ricoh-majority)
- **Microelectronics**: SoC andASIC solutions through Socionext (minority stake retained)

## Corporate Structure

- **Parent**: Furukawa Group (Furukawa Electric is the largest shareholder)
- **Major divisions**: Fujitsu Limited (parent), Fujitsu Japan, Fujitsu Client Computing (44%-owned JV with Lenovo), Fujitsu General (42%-owned, air conditioning)
- **Headquarters**: Kawasaki, Kanagawa, Japan (relocated from Tokyo Shiodome in 2024)
- **Employees**: 124,000 (2024)
- **Listed**: Tokyo Stock Exchange (TYO: 6702); Nagoya Stock Exchange
- **Revenue**: ¥3.76 trillion (FY2024); Operating income ¥160.26B; Net income ¥254.48B
