# SoftBank Group

SoftBank Group Corp. (SBG) is a Japanese multinational conglomerate holding company founded in 1981 by Masayoshi Son, headquartered in Minato-ku, Tokyo. Originally a software distributor (selling software forNEC and other PC platforms in Japan), SoftBank grew through aggressive acquisitions into a global investment giant and the world's largest technology investor. The company's defining chapter came in 2016 when it spent $31 billion to acquire ARM Holdings (UK semiconductor IP licensor), followed by the launch of the $100 billion SoftBank Vision Fund in 2017 — the world's largest technology-focused investment fund. SoftBank's investment portfolio spans Alibaba (its legendary $20M investment in 2000 returned ~$60B+), Sprint (US telecom), ByteDance, DiDi, WeWork, and hundreds of others. The Vision Fund's massive bets on unlisted tech startups made it a symbol of the 2017-2021 tech boom and 2022-2023 downturn. With $47.8 billion FY2024 revenue and approximately ¥13 trillion (~$87B) in assets under management, SoftBank remains one of the world's most influential technology investment vehicles. Masayoshi Son personally owns ~26% of SoftBank and is its Chairman and CEO.

## Timeline

### 1981–1994: Origins as Software Distributor

- **September 1981**: SoftBank founded by Masayoshi Son in Osaka, Japan, at age 24, with ¥10 million in capital. Initial business: selling software (operating systems and applications) for NEC and other Japanese PC makers. The name "SoftBank" was chosen as a counterpart to "Nihon Kogyo" (Japan's IBM), reflecting Son's ambition to build a massive company.
- **1982**: Masayoshi Son met Jerry Yang (Yahoo co-founder) in Tokyo — the beginning of a lifelong relationship with Silicon Valley.
- **1986**: SoftBank's software distribution business reached ¥3 billion in sales, making it Japan's leading PC software distributor.
- **1990**: Masayoshi Son invested in Gold Bond (laterYahoo Japan) — early foray into Internet businesses.
- **1994**: SoftBank went public on Japan's JASDAQ market at ¥8,500/share. Masayoshi Son was 30 years old and became a billionaire on paper.

### 1995–2000: Yahoo Japan and Entry into Internet

- **1995**: Masayoshi Son invested $1 million in Yahoo! (US) — one of the first major Silicon Valley investments by a Japanese investor. Later expanded to $350M for ~35% of Yahoo, and separately launched Yahoo! Japan in 1996 (jointly owned by SoftBank and Yahoo US).
- **1996**: Yahoo! Japan launched — within 2 years it became Japan's #1 website, eventually generating more profit than Yahoo US itself. SoftBank's shares in Yahoo Japan became enormously valuable.
- **1999**: SoftBank invested $20 million in Alibaba (Jack Ma's B2B e-commerce startup) for ~37% equity — this single investment would become the most profitable venture capital bet in history, worth $60-100 billion at peak.
- **1999**: Masayoshi Son became a driving force behind the Japanese broadband revolution, pushing for fiber optic infrastructure.

### 2001–2006: Broadband, Yahoo Japan, and IPO

- **2001**: Yahoo! BB launched — SoftBank's broadband Internet service in Japan. Son offered ¥24/month service with ¥980 installation, undercutting NTT's pricing by 80%, then bundled Yahoo! services. Despite early losses, this established SoftBank as a major consumer ISP.
- **2003**: Yahoo! Japan listed on TSE — SoftBank's stake worth ¥1 trillion at listing. Became Japan's most visited website.
- **2004**: Yahoo! Japan became profitable — contributed majority of SoftBank Group's earnings for years.
- **2005**: SoftBank invested ¥11 billion in retail broker Monex — early fintech investment.
- **2005**: Masayoshi Son personally invested in SmartNews — one of his rare direct consumer startup bets.

### 2006–2016: Mobile, Sprint, and ARM Acquisition

- **April 2006**: SoftBank announced acquisition of Vodafone Japan (mobile carrier) for ¥1.75 trillion ($17.5B) — a controversial move as Vodafone Japan had only 4.5M subscribers and was Japan's weakest mobile carrier. Most analysts thought Son was overpaying. Rebranded as SoftBank Mobile.
- **2008**: SoftBank Mobile grew to 21M subscribers following iPhone exclusivity deal with Apple (2007) — Son personally negotiated the iPhone deal in 45 minutes with Steve Jobs, a pivotal strategic move that transformed SoftBank Mobile from laggard to leader.
- **October 2012**: SoftBank announced $20 billion acquisition of 70% of Sprint Nextel (US #3 carrier) — Masayoshi Son personally guaranteed the deal. Combination created the third-largest wireless carrier in the US.
- **April 2013**: Sprint acquisition completed for $21.6 billion. Sprint had 50M subscribers but was heavily indebted.
- **2013-2016**: Sprint's debt load proved problematic. SoftBank attempted to merge Sprint with T-Mobile US but regulators blocked the deal in 2017. Sprint became a chronic underperformer, costing SoftBank billions.
- **July 2014**: Masayoshi Son became Chairman of Sprint (stepped down from CEO) — remained deeply involved in strategy.
- **2016**: Masayoshi Son spent $4.9 billion for a 44% stake in Finnish game company Supercell — the same company Tencent would later acquire for $8.6B in 2016.

### 2016–2019: Vision Fund and the Peak

- **June 2016**: SoftBank completed acquisition of **ARM Holdings** for £24.3 billion ($31.4 billion) — the largest European tech acquisition ever at the time. ARM's chip designs (Cortex-A, Cortex-M, Mali GPU) are in virtually every smartphone and an increasing number of IoT and data center chips globally.
- **October 2016**: Saudi Arabia's Public Investment Fund (PIF) committed $45 billion to the Vision Fund, with SoftBank committing $28 billion and Abu Dhabi's Mubadala $15 billion.
- **May 2017**: **SoftBank Vision Fund I** launched with $100 billion capital — the world's largest technology-focused investment fund. PIF was the anchor LP ($45B). Other LPs included Apple, Foxconn, Qualcomm, Sharp, and others.
- **2017**: Vision Fund invested massively in WeWork ($4.4B), ByteDance ($3B), OYO ($1B), Cruise ($2.25B), Wag, Katerra, etc. — many at valuations that proved wildly optimistic.
- **2018**: SoftBank invested $2.8 billion in ByteDance (TikTok parent) — one of the Vision Fund's best-performing investments.
- **2019**: WeWork's IPO failed spectacularly in August 2019 — $47B valuation collapsed to ~$8B, forcing SoftBank to bail out the company with a $9.5B rescue package. Masayoshi Son's reputation took a significant hit.

### 2020–2022: WeWork Crisis, COVID, and Recovery

- **2020**: COVID-19 pandemic — Vision Fund portfolio companies (WeWork, OYO, Cruise) hit hard by lockdowns. SoftBank reported $12.7B loss for FY2019 (ending March 2020).
- **March 2020**: SoftBank sold $14.5 billion of its Alibaba shares to repurchase stock and raise cash — began reducing its Alibaba stake after two decades.
- **2020**: Masayoshi Son changed strategy — Vision Fund II raised $5.5 billion (mostly SoftBank's own capital) and shifted toward backing more mature, later-stage companies with revenue.
- **2021**: ARM settlement with Intel — ARM agreed to modify some licensing terms to settle Intel's objections.
- **2021**: SoftBank sold a 25% stake in Sprint's assets to T-Mobile as part of the Sprint-T-Mobile merger ($3.6B) — partially exiting the US telecom market.
- **2022**: NASDAQ's decline and rate hikes crushed Vision Fund portfolio valuations. SoftBank reported record $27B loss for FY2022 (ending March 2022).
- **2022**: Masayoshi Son cut Vision Fund investing — "defensive mode" — reduced new commitments to near zero, focusing on existing portfolio management.

### 2023–2025: Arm IPO and New Chapter

- **February 2023**: SoftBank announced intention to take ARM public — after failed sale to NVIDIA ($66B deal collapsed due to regulatory opposition in the US, UK, and China), an IPO became the path forward.
- **September 14, 2023**: **ARM Holdings** listed on NASDAQ at $51/share, raising $4.87 billion in the biggest semiconductor IPO in history. SoftBank retained ~90% ownership. ARM's market cap at IPO: ~$54 billion.
- **2023**: SoftBank posted ¥962 billion ($6.6B) net profit for FY2023 — strong recovery driven by ARM's valuation uplift and Vision Fund portfolio gains (particularly Alibaba revaluation).
- **2024**: SoftBank Vision Fund invested $2.8 billion in AI infrastructure (Perplexity AI,伺服大脑) — Son declared AI as SoftBank's top priority for the next decade. $3 billion commitment to AI data centers announced.
- **2025**: SoftBank is reportedly negotiating to sell a minority stake in ARM to Samsung Electronics or other investors — potential $5-10B transaction.
- **2025**: SoftBank posted ¥1.2 trillion ($8.1B) net profit for FY2024 (ending March 2025), with Vision Fund gains continuing.

## Key Products and Business Segments

### Investment Holdings
- **ARM Holdings** — UK semiconductor IP licensor; designs Cortex-A (application processors), Cortex-R (real-time), Cortex-M (microcontrollers), Mali GPU, and Ethos NPU. Every smartphone (Apple A-series, Qualcomm Snapdragon, Samsung Exynos) and billions of IoT chips use ARM architecture. SoftBank owns ~90% post-IPO.
- **SoftBank Vision Fund I** ($100B, 2017) — world's largest tech VC fund; invested in Alibaba, ByteDance, WeWork, OYO, Cruise Automation, DoorDash, Uber, Klarna, etc.
- **SoftBank Vision Fund II** ($5.5B, 2019) — smaller, more defensive fund; focused on growth-stage tech companies

### Domestic Japanese Operations
- **SoftBank Corp** (独立上市公司, TYO: 9434) — Japan's #3 mobile carrier with ~55M subscribers; SoftBank Group owns ~40% of listed SoftBank Corp. Provides mobile, broadband, and enterprise network services.
- **Yahoo! Japan** (now Z Holdings, TYO: 4689) — Japan's #1 portal; SoftBank divested majority stake in 2019 but remains a significant shareholder
- **Rakuten** stake (~21%) — SoftBank transferred its Rakuten shares to Vision Fund as part of restructuring

### Strategic Investments (selected)
- **Alibaba** — SoftBank holds ~0.5% (down from 34% peak after two decades of sales); original $20M investment returned $60-100B+ cumulatively
- **ByteDance/TikTok** — Vision Fund invested $3B in 2018; stake now managed in Vision Fund portfolio
- **Berkshire Hathaway** — SoftBank holds a minority stake in Warren Buffett's company (2018 investment)
- **T-Mobile US** — partial exit via Sprint-T-Mobile merger; retains ~3% stake

## Masayoshi Son: The Investor

Masayoshi Son's investment philosophy is distinctive: he looks for "information革命的すあま" (information revolution) companies that can achieve network-effect monopolies, and he is willing to make extraordinarily large bets. His $20M Alibaba investment in 2000 is the greatest VC trade in history — generating 3,000x+ returns at peak. His $31B ARM acquisition in 2016 was also visionary — positioning SoftBank at the center of every chip supply chain. But his $100B Vision Fund also produced WeWork, OYO, and other high-profile failures that cost investors billions. Son's 300-year thinking: he has said he thinks about SoftBank's strategy on a 300-year timescale, which partly explains his willingness to make bets that seem irrational in the short term.
