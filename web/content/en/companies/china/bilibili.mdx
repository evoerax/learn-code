# Bilibili

Bilibili, often called China's "YouTube for Gen Z," is a video-sharing website and online entertainment platform founded in 2009 that has grown into a dominant cultural destination for China's youth. The platform is built on a unique "bullet comment" (danmaku) system where viewers send floating commentary that scrolls across videos in real-time, creating a shared viewing experience. Bilibili started as an anime and ACG (Animation, Comics, Gaming) fandom site and has expanded into music, education, gaming, live streaming, and short dramas. It is listed on NASDAQ (BILI) and HKEX (9626.HK) with a market cap of approximately **$5-7 billion** as of 2024-2025, generating approximately **¥26.8 billion ($3.7B)** in FY2024 revenue.

The platform is renowned for its highly engaged user base — primarily Chinese millennials and Gen Z — who spend an average of 96 minutes per day on the platform. Its community culture, centered on "弹幕" (danmaku/bullet comments), "up主" (content creators), and a complex user ranking and badge system, is unlike any other major video platform globally. Unlike YouTube's broad demographic appeal, Bilibili is disproportionately concentrated among 18-35 year olds, with over 78% of its users born after 1990.

## Development Timeline

### 2009–2013: Founding and Anime Fandom Origins

Bilibili was originally launched as **Mikufans.cn** (Miku粉丝站) on June 26, 2009, as a fan site for the Japanese virtual singer Hatsune Miku. The name was inspired by the Japanese phrase "BiliBili," an onomatopoeia for the sound of swords clashing, chosen as a tribute to the anime *Fate/Stay Night*. In 2010, the site was renamed **Bilibili** after the anime *The Melancholy of Haruhi Suzumiya*, where the main character mentions the phrase.

Co-founder **Xu Yi** (also known online as "Mr. Helpful") was a 22-year-old graduate student in computer science when he built the site. His vision was to create a community platform where anime fans could watch videos together and interact through real-time bullet comments. The danmaku system was inspired by Niconico Douga in Japan, and Bilibili essentially brought the concept to China with localization.

The platform's early growth was almost entirely organic, driven by anime fans sharing fan-made content, AMVs (anime music videos), and subbed (fan-subtitled) anime episodes. Unlike major portals, Bilibili required users to pass a difficult anime knowledge quiz before being allowed to post comments — this barrier to entry created an insider culture that users found deeply validating.

### 2013–2016: Mobile App, Mobile Gaming, and NASDAQ Listing Prep

In 2011, Chen Rui (陈睿), a former executive at online gaming company Kingsoft (金山), joined Bilibili as a strategic advisor and later became Chairman and CEO. Chen Rui brought corporate discipline and a monetization vision to what had previously been a pure passion project.

In 2012, Bilibili launched its mobile app, which quickly became the dominant way users accessed the platform. The shift to mobile was critical for monetization — Bilibili had discovered that anime fans were also enthusiastic mobile gamers, and began publishing and distributing mobile games on its platform.

**Key game titles distributed by Bilibili:**

- **Fate/Grand Order (FGO):** Launched in 2016, this became one of Bilibili's most important games. A mobile RPG based on the popular Fate anime franchise, FGO was a massive revenue driver through in-app purchases. At peak, it generated over ¥1.5B per quarter for Bilibili.
- ** Azur Lane, Arknights, Genshin Impact (distribution), and other popular titles**

In 2014, Bilibili began its "Bilibili World" offline convention series, creating physical events for its online community. The company also launched its own live streaming category in 2014.

### 2016–2019: Video Platform Expansion and IPO

By 2016, Bilibili's user base had expanded well beyond anime into gaming, music, tech, education, and lifestyle content. The platform had over **50 million monthly active users**, with average daily time spent exceeding 70 minutes. However, advertising revenue was minimal — Bilibili's young user base had low commercial intent, and the platform resisted intrusive advertising that would damage the user experience.

**Gaming boom (2016-2018):** Bilibili's game publishing business became its primary revenue driver. In 2016, mobile games accounted for ~65% of total revenue. FGO and Azur Lane were extraordinarily successful. However, this created a structural vulnerability: Bilibili's revenue was heavily dependent on a small number of games.

In 2017, Bilibili launched its **"Super Camp" (超新星计划)** to develop original content, including its own animated series and documentaries. The company also began investing in Chinese anime production companies, acquiring minority stakes in studios to secure exclusive content.

In **March 2018**, Bilibili went public on NASDAQ at $10.50/share, raising $480 million. The IPO was priced at the bottom of the range due to concerns about gaming revenue concentration. At listing, Bilibili had **76.5 million MAU** and was generating ~¥2.4B in annual revenue.

Post-IPO, Bilibili accelerated its diversification: **advertising** was expanded with native ad formats; **value-added services** (VAS) including premium memberships, live streaming tips, and its **Electric (电竞)** esports business were scaled.

### 2019–2021: Diversification and "China's YouTube" Ambition

In 2019, Bilibili set a target of reaching **200 million MAU** by 2023 (achieved early in 2021). CEO Chen Rui articulated the vision of making Bilibili "the home of Chinese youth culture."

Key strategic moves:

- **Knowledge content (知识区):** In 2019, Bilibili became unexpectedly famous for hosting educational content. A single video of physics professor **Wu Yulu (吴有理)** solving physics problems went viral. Bilibili leaned into this, promoting "B站（Bilibili） is the best university in the world" narrative. By 2021, Bilibili had become a major platform for academic lectures, programming tutorials, and general knowledge content.

- **Live streaming expansion:** Bilibili's live streaming grew rapidly, particularly for gaming (League of Legends, Genshin Impact), virtual streamers (VTubers modeled after Japanese Virtual YouTubers), and entertainment (singing, chatting). In 2020, live streaming became a major revenue contributor alongside games.

- **Short video (Story Mode):** In 2021, Bilibili introduced a vertical short-video mode (Story Mode) to compete with Douyin/Kuaishou for mobile-first short-form content consumption.

- **"Blue City" acquisition (2021):** Bilibili acquired the storytelling platform **Shikicom (潮汐资讯)** and invested in short drama production companies, entering the burgeoning Chinese short drama market.

- **Anime and entertainment:** Bilibili invested in over 30 domestic anime production companies and became a significant force in Chinese animation, producing series including "Link Click" and "The King's Avatar" (which became one of Bilibili's most successful original series).

### 2021–2023: Regulatory Headwinds and Strategic Pivot

The Chinese government crackdown on the entertainment industry (2021) and new restrictions on gaming for minors significantly impacted Bilibili's gaming business:

- **Gaming restrictions (2021):** China's "anti-addiction" gaming regulations limited minors to 3 hours per week, reducing game revenue across the industry. FGO and other gacha games saw declines.

- **Entertainment crackdown:** New rules restricted "effeminate men" (娘炮) in entertainment content, affecting some Bilibili VTuber personalities and variety content.

Despite these headwinds, Bilibili continued growing its user base. In Q3 2021, Bilibili surpassed its 2023 target of 200M MAU, reaching **235 million MAU**. Revenue grew to ¥14.5B for FY2021 (+64% YoY), but net loss widened to ¥6.8B as the company invested heavily in content and marketing.

**2022 challenges:** The COVID-19 lockdowns in China reduced advertising revenue significantly. Bilibili also faced scrutiny over content moderation, particularly around political content and live streaming. In August 2022, Bilibili removed the "嘻嘻" (xixi) bullet comment system temporarily after regulators cited inappropriate content. The company also faced significant losses, with FY2022 net loss of ¥7.5B.

### 2023–2025: Path to Profitability and AI Integration

In 2023, Bilibili made a strategic pivot toward profitability while maintaining user growth:

- **Cost cutting:** Bilibili reduced its content costs, cut marketing spending, and optimized its workforce. The company also restructured its gaming publishing arrangements.

- **Advertising growth:** Bilibili's advertising business became its fastest-growing segment, reaching 28% of revenue in FY2024. Brand advertising, performance advertising, and native content deals all grew, helped by Bilibili's young, high-engagement user base that is valuable for consumer brands.

- **Gaming restructuring:** Bilibili shifted from publishing third-party games to developing more first-party titles. In 2023, it launched several self-developed games. The company also ended its publishing relationship with some titles that had underperformed.

- **AI integration (2024):** Bilibili integrated AI tools for content creation, including AI-assisted video editing tools, automated subtitle generation, and AI-generated content recommendations. The platform also became a major distribution channel for AI-related content, with AI tutorials and discussion becoming one of the fastest-growing categories.

- **Short dramas:** Bilibili entered the short drama market with "B站剧场" (Bilibili Theater), commissioning and distributing mini-series of 1-3 minutes each, competing with Douyin's Douyin Short Drama and platforms like ReelShort.

By Q4 2024, Bilibili had **347.5 million MAU** and **105 million DAU**, with daily time spent averaging **96 minutes**. The company achieved its first quarterly adjusted operating profit in Q4 2023 and maintained profitability in subsequent quarters.

FY2024 revenue reached **¥26.8B** (+19% YoY), with gross margin improving to ~25%. The company still reported net losses but significantly narrowed them from the ¥7.5B peak in 2022.

## Product Lines

### Core Video Platform

- **Long-form video (PUGC):** Professional user-generated content — the majority of Bilibili's viewership. Creators (up主) produce everything from anime analysis and gaming walkthroughs to science education and cooking tutorials. Bilibili has a unique "10,000-tier" creator system (up to 100,000 fans, then progression through ranks).
- **Short-form video (Story Mode):** Vertical video introduced in 2021 for mobile-first consumption, competing with Douyin.
- **Live streaming:** Both personal live streams (聊天、唱见、舞见) and gaming esports streams. Key revenue model: viewers purchase "B-coin" (B币) to send virtual gifts to streamers; Bilibili takes a 40-50% commission.
- **Bullet comments (弹幕/danmaku):** Bilibili's signature feature — floating comments visible to all viewers in sync with video playback, creating a shared, communal viewing experience. The intensity of danmaku on popular videos can reach thousands of comments per minute.

### Gaming

- **First-party games:** Self-developed titles including "Blue Archive" (if published), "Arknights" publishing, and other titles.
- **Third-party game publishing:** Bilibili historically distributed games including "Fate/Grand Order" (until contract ended ~2022), "Azur Lane," and "Genshin Impact" (promotion/distribution).
- **Esports (Bilibili Esports):** Bilibili owns esports teams including **Bilibili Gaming (BLG)** for League of Legends, which competes in the LPL (League of Legends Pro League).

### Value-Added Services (VAS)

- **Bilibili Premium (大会员):** Subscription for ad-free viewing, exclusive content, early access to series, and high-quality streaming. ~56 million paying members by 2024.
- **Live streaming tips/gifts:** The core monetization for live streamers.
- **Manga and comic reading (漫画):** Bilibili acquired and operates a manga reading platform.

### Advertising

- **Brand ads:** Display and video ads placed in the feed.
- **Performance ads:** Pay-per-click and CPM ads for e-commerce and app promotion.
- **Native content/UGC ads:** Content created by Bilibili up主 that incorporates brand messages (a significant and growing format).

### Short Drama (剧场版)

- Commissioned and licensed mini-series in the 1-5 minute format, targeting the same young female demographic that drives Kuaishou short drama consumption.

## Competitive Landscape

Bilibili occupies a unique niche — it's not a pure competitor to Douyin (short video, algorithm-driven) or YouTube (broad demographics, short-form). Its closest comparison is a combination of YouTube + Reddit + Twitch + Crunchyroll, with a distinctly Chinese ACG culture overlay.

- **vs. Douyin/ByteDance:** Douyin has 700M+ DAU and dominates short video; Bilibili competes in overlapping audiences but Douyin's algorithm-first approach and younger average user age make it a different product
- **vs. YouTube:** Bilibili's danmaku, community features, and creator culture make it more community-oriented; YouTube's global dominance and monetization infrastructure (Google Ads) are far superior
- **vs. Kuaishou:** Kuaishou targets lower-tier cities and older demographics; Bilibili is urban, young, and education/culture-oriented
- **vs. Tencent Video/iQiyi:** These are streaming VIP platforms (长视频/Netflix-style); Bilibili's model is ad-supported + freemium for user-generated content

## Key Learnings

- Bilibili's bullet comment (danmaku) system is a genuine innovation in video viewing experience — creating a sense of communal, synchronous viewing even for on-demand content. This has no direct equivalent in Western platforms and creates a powerful social viewing experience that drives repeat engagement.
- The platform's transition from pure anime fandom to mainstream entertainment while retaining its "insider" community culture is a remarkable case of brand evolution — Bilibili maintained its community identity while growing 5x in users by carefully managing creator status systems and community norms.
- Bilibili's gaming revenue concentration (65%→18% of revenue) required a decade of structural transformation — the lesson is that a platform depending on third-party game publishing has no durable competitive advantage; self-developed games and diversified revenue are necessary.
- The 2021 regulatory crackdown on entertainment showed Bilibili's vulnerability to policy changes — the company's anime-adjacent content and gaming focus made it disproportionately exposed to government crackdown on gaming and "feminine men" in media.
- Bilibili's knowledge content pivot (2019 onwards) was an unexpected strategic gift — when a physics professor's problem-solving videos went viral, the company leaned into educational content, turning what could have been a random virality moment into a platform-wide content category that attracted older, higher-income users.
