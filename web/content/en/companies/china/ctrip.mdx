# Trip.com Group (Ctrip)

Trip.com Group (携程集团), known internationally as Trip.com and in China as Ctrip (携程), is China's largest and the world's second-largest online travel agency (OTA), a position it achieved through decades of acquisitions and market consolidation. Founded in **1999** by James Liang (梁建章), Neil Shen (沈南鹏), and Fan Qiao (范敏) in Shanghai, Ctrip began as an online travel booking platform for corporate clients before expanding to leisure travelers. The company's early years were marked by the dot-com boom and bust, surviving the 2001 crash by pivoting to a call-center-assisted model and later re-emerging as a dominant OTA after the SARS pandemic in 2003. Today, Trip.com Group operates **Trip.com** (international), **Ctrip** (China domestic), **Qunar** (budget travel), and **Skyscanner** (global flight comparison) — serving over **500 million registered users** globally. The company is listed on NASDAQ (TCOM) and HKEX (9961.HK), with FY2024 revenue of approximately **¥46.6 billion (~$6.5B)** and a market cap of approximately **$30-40B** as of 2025.

## Development Timeline

### 1999–2003: Dot-Com Era and SARS Survival

Ctrip was founded in **1999** by three Stanford-educated entrepreneurs: James Liang (梁建章, CEO), Neil Shen (沈南鹏, CFO), and Fan Qiao (范敏, COO). The company initially targeted corporate travel clients, allowing businesses to book flights and hotels through a web platform backed by a call center. The dot-com bust in 2000-2001 devastated China's nascent internet sector, and Ctrip's early growth was slow.

In 2001, the company was forced to restructure. Instead of pursuing pure online bookings (which had very low adoption in China at the time), Ctrip pivoted to a **"鼠标+水泥" (mouse + cement)** model — combining an online platform with a massive offline call center to assist customers with bookings. This hybrid model proved critical in China, where credit card penetration was low and trust in online transactions was minimal.

**SARS turning point (2003):** The SARS epidemic devastated China's travel industry in 2003. While many travel agencies went bankrupt, the pent-up demand after SARS caused an explosive rebound in travel bookings. Ctrip, which had survived the downturn with disciplined cost management, was perfectly positioned to capture the surge. The SARS rebound became a formative experience — demonstrating that the Chinese travel market was fundamentally resilient and that consolidation opportunities would arise during downturns.

Ctrip went public on **December 9, 2003** on NASDAQ, raising approximately $65 million at $18/share. The stock doubled on its first day of trading.

### 2003–2010: Expansion and Market Leadership

Post-IPO, Ctrip used its capital and public currency to accelerate growth:

- **2003-2005:** Expansion from corporate travel to leisure travelers. Hotel booking and vacation packages added.
- **2004:** Launch of Ctrip's train ticket booking service, which became enormously popular in China where train travel is a dominant mode.
- **2005-2008:** Expansion through acquisitions — Ctrip acquired several regional travel agencies and tour operators to build out its supply chain and distribution network.
- **2008-2009 Financial Crisis:** While competitors struggled, Ctrip continued to grow by acquiring distressed competitors and investing in technology. This period established Ctrip's dominant market position.

### 2010–2015: Mobile, Qunar Competition, and International Expansion

The shift to mobile was transformative for Ctrip:

- **Mobile app launch (2010-2012):** As smartphone penetration accelerated in China, Ctrip invested heavily in its mobile app. By 2015, over 70% of Ctrip's transactions were made on mobile devices.
- **Qunar (去哪儿) competition:** In 2011-2014, Ctrip faced aggressive competition from **Qunar** (去哪网), a user-generated travel review and price comparison platform backed by Baidu. Qunar disrupted Ctrip's model by offering heavy discounts subsidized by Baidu, and by 2014 Qunar had overtaken Ctrip in some metrics. The competition was enormously expensive for both companies.

In **2015**, Ctrip merged with Qunar in a **$4 billion deal** — Ctrip issued new shares to Qunar's shareholders in exchange for 100% of Qunar. Baidu became the largest shareholder of the combined entity with approximately 25% stake. The merger effectively ended the price war and gave Ctrip ~80%+ of the Chinese OTA market.

### 2015–2019: Skyscanner Acquisition and Global Expansion

With domestic dominance secured, Ctrip pursued international expansion:

- **2016 Skyscanner acquisition:** Ctrip acquired **Skyscanner** for approximately **$1.7 billion** in cash. Skyscanner, founded in Edinburgh, Scotland in 2003, is a global flight comparison/search engine used by over 100 million people monthly across 30+ languages. The acquisition gave Ctrip global reach in the flight search market.

- **2017:** Launch of **Trip.com** as the international brand — a direct competitor to Booking.com, Expedia, and Agoda for outbound Chinese travelers and international travelers globally.

- **2019:** Acquired **MakeMyTrip** (India) minority stake and various other international investments. The company also invested in toll collection platforms and other travel infrastructure.

- **2019-2020:** COVID-19 pandemic severely impacted travel globally. Ctrip's revenue fell from ¥35.7B in 2019 to ¥18.3B in 2020. The company responded with aggressive cost cutting (laying off ~25% of its 50,000-person workforce) and by pivoting to the domestic travel recovery in China.

### 2020–2023: COVID Recovery and Qunar Restructuring

Post-COVID, domestic travel in China recovered faster than international travel:

- **2020-2022:** Domestic hotel bookings recovered to pre-pandemic levels by mid-2020. However, international travel remained severely restricted due to China's "zero-COVID" policy, which was only lifted in January 2023.
- **Qunar restructuring:** In 2023, Ctrip announced it would take Qunar private and delist it from NASDAQ, merging its operations into the main Ctrip platform. This was a recognition that maintaining two separate OTA brands was unnecessarily expensive.
- **International travel recovery (2023-2024):** As China's borders reopened in January 2023, international travel rebounded strongly. Ctrip's FY2023 revenue recovered to ¥44.5B, and FY2024 revenue reached ¥46.6B (+21% YoY), exceeding 2019 levels.

### 2024–2025: AI Integration and New Growth Vectors

- **AI-powered travel:** In 2024, Trip.com Group integrated large language models into its booking platform — enabling conversational travel planning, personalized recommendation, and automated customer service in multiple languages.
- **Short-video integration:** The platform expanded its content features, integrating short-video travel content to compete with Xiaohongshu (RED) and Douyin for travel inspiration and discovery.
- **2025 eLong merger:** In early 2025, Ctrip announced a merger with **eLong** (艺龙) — its original rival from the early 2000s — consolidating the Chinese OTA market further.
- **2025 share buyback:** The company announced a $400M share buyback program, signaling confidence in its financial position.

## Product Lines

### Main Platforms

- **Ctrip (携程):** China's dominant full-service OTA; book flights, hotels, trains, buses, vacation packages, tours, attractions, and visa services
- **Trip.com:** International platform serving global travelers in 30+ languages; competes directly with Booking.com and Expedia
- **Qunar (去哪儿):** Budget-focused travel platform with heavy emphasis on user reviews, price comparison, and deals; being merged into Ctrip platform by 2025
- **Skyscanner:** Global flight comparison search engine; 100M+ monthly users; remains a separate brand and platform

### Core Booking Services

- **Flights:** Domestic and international air ticketing; partnerships with 500+ airlines; feature includes price calendar, flexible date search
- **Hotels:** 1.2M+ hotels and properties globally; includes luxury, business, budget, and homestay options; direct contracts with major hotel chains
- **High-speed rail (火车票):** Dominant platform for China high-speed rail ticket booking; also covers conventional rail
- **Vacation packages:** Bundled flight + hotel + activities packages for domestic and outbound travel
- **Attractions & experiences:** Ticketing for tourist attractions, theme parks, museums, and local experiences

### Corporate Travel (TMC)

- **Trip.com for Business (企业差旅):** End-to-end corporate travel management platform for business clients; competes with others in the corporate travel management (TMC) space

## Competitive Landscape

- **vs. Meituan Travel:** Meituan (which acquired Dianping) is a strong #2 in China's OTA market, particularly for local services and hotel bookings near point-of-sale. Ctrip's full-service model (flights + hotels + packages) gives it advantage in comprehensive trip planning.
- **vs. Alibaba Fliggy:** Alibaba's Fliggy (飞猪) competes for OTA market share, backed by Alibaba's ecosystem and Alipay payment integration.
- **vs. Douyin/Xiaohongshu:** These platforms increasingly influence travel decisions through content (short videos, influencer posts), but conversion to bookings still often happens on traditional OTAs. Ctrip has responded by adding content features.
- **International (Trip.com vs. Booking/Expedia):** Trip.com is growing internationally but is still small vs. Booking.com (~$17B revenue) and Expedia (~$10B revenue) in the global OTA market. Skyscanner provides competitive advantage in flight search globally.

## Key Learnings

- Ctrip's "mouse + cement" (online + call center) model was a crucial insight for China in the early 2000s — recognizing that online payment and trust infrastructure was insufficient for travel bookings in China, and combining online convenience with human-assisted phone booking was the right solution
- The 2015 Ctrip-Qunar merger ($4B, mainly stock) was the decisive move that ended the OTA price war — Ctrip's willingness to issue shares to eliminate a well-funded competitor demonstrated that sometimes the most profitable move is to buy your enemy rather than beat them
- The SARS (2003) and COVID (2020) crises both proved to be net positives for Ctrip's long-term position — each crisis eliminated weaker competitors, and Ctrip's operational discipline during downturns allowed it to emerge stronger
- Skyscanner ($1.7B, 2016) was a premium price but gave Ctrip something it couldn't build organically: global flight search market share and a trusted international brand — the acquisition proved wise as Trip.com's international expansion relied heavily on Skyscanner's traffic
- Ctrip's mobile-first strategy (70%+ mobile transactions by 2015) was achieved by investing heavily in app development and user acquisition at a time when many competitors were still desktop-focused — the early mobile investment created a durable competitive advantage in user experience
