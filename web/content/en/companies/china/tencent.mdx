# Tencent

Tencent Holdings Ltd. (腾讯控股有限公司) is a Chinese multinational conglomerate and technology company headquartered in Shenzhen, founded on **November 11, 1998** by **Pony Ma** (马化腾, Ma Huateng), along with co-founders Zhang Zhidong, Xu Chenye, Charles Chen, and Zeng Liqing. It is one of the world's largest internet companies, operating the super-app **WeChat** (13+ billion messages sent daily) and its desktop counterpart **QQ**, while building a dominant position in gaming (via stakes in Riot Games, Epic Games, Supercell, Ubisoft), fintech (WeChat Pay), cloud services, and AI. With 2024 revenue of **CNY 660.3 billion (~$91.8 billion)**, Tencent is China's most profitable internet company and a top-10 global technology firm by revenue.

Tencent pioneered China's internet era with QQ in 1999 and created the defining mobile super-app with WeChat in 2011, achieving near-ubiquity in Chinese daily life. Its **WeChat ecosystem** — combining messaging, social media, mobile payments (WeChat Pay), mini-programs, and services — has over **1.3 billion monthly active users** and serves as a platform for millions of third-party applications (mini-programs).

## Timeline

### 1998–2001: Foundation and QQ's Emergence

- **November 11, 1998**: Tencent founded in **Shenzhen** by **Pony Ma** (Ma Huateng, 马化腾) along with four co-founders: **Zhang Zhidong**, **Xu Chenye**, **Charles Chen**, and **Zeng Liqing**. Initial capital: ~500,000 RMB (~$60,000). The company initially focused on providing internet value-added services — specifically, developing a **Pager system** (无线寻呼系统) for Chinese telecom companies.
- **1999 (February)**: Launched **QQ** (initially named **OICQ**, inspired by ICQ) — a desktop instant messaging application. QQ quickly became popular among Chinese internet users, especially students and young adults, who used it for text chat, file transfer, and later voice and video calls. Within a year, QQ had millions of registered users.
- **1999**: The company faced a crisis when **IDG Capital and Pacific Venture Partners** were unwilling to invest further after a failed attempt to sell the company (reportedly, Pony Ma tried to sell QQ for ~$600,000 but couldn't find a buyer). This near-death experience is a famous story in Chinese tech history.
- **2000**: **Naspers** (South African media conglomerate, now Tencent's largest shareholder) invested **$32 million** for a **46.5% stake** in Tencent — one of the most successful venture investments in history. Naspers' initial $32 million stake was reportedly worth over **$130 billion** by 2021 (though Naspers has since sold down some shares).
- **2000**: QQ reached **100 million registered users** — a major milestone for the young company.

### 2002–2004: Diversification and IPO

- **2002**: QQ launched **QQ Show** (QQ秀) — a virtual avatar and dress-up system that introduced **virtual goods monetization** to China. Users purchased virtual items (clothing, accessories) for their avatars using QQ coins. This became Tencent's first major revenue source beyond subscriptions and laid the foundation for China's virtual goods economy.
- **2003**: Launched **QQ Games** — entering online gaming, which would become one of Tencent's most profitable businesses.
- **2004 (June 16)**: Tencent **IPO on the Hong Kong Stock Exchange** (ticker: 00700.HK), raising ~$200 million. Valuation at IPO: ~$600 million. This gave Tencent public currency for acquisitions and provided liquidity for early investors.

### 2005–2010: Gaming Empire and WeChat Predecessors

- **2005**: Launched **QQ Music** — online music streaming and download service, later one of China's most popular music platforms.
- **2005**: Acquired **UCWeb** — China's leading mobile browser (later merged into Alibaba's business). The acquisition illustrated Tencent's strategy of buying competitive mobile assets early.
- **2008**: Tencent launched **QQ Fantasy** (QQ幻想) — its first large-scale MMORPG (massively multiplayer online role-playing game).
- **2009**: QQ had **~500 million active users** — making it one of the world's largest instant messaging platforms by users.
- **2010**: The **"3Q Duel"** — Tencent's competitor **360 Security** launched a product that conflicted with QQ's security features. In one of China's most famous tech battles, 360 and Tencent engaged in a fierce public dispute, with 360 accusing Tencent of collecting user data and Tencent responding by forcing users to choose between QQ and 360's software. The Ministry of Industry and Information Technology (MIIT) intervened, ordering both companies to stop the conflict. The incident prompted national discussion about user privacy in China.

### 2011: WeChat Launch and Riot Games

- **2011 (January 21)**: Launched **WeChat** (微信) — a mobile instant messaging app combining messaging, voice notes, video calls, and social feed ("Moments"). WeChat was initially positioned as a mobile-first alternative to QQ. Unlike QQ, WeChat used phone contacts as the social graph rather than QQ's buddy list. The **"Shake" and "Drift Bottle"** features drove viral adoption. WeChat grew from 0 to 100 million users in under a year.
- **2011**: Acquired a **majority stake in Riot Games** (originally ~70%) for approximately **$230 million** — a transformative acquisition that gave Tencent ownership of **League of Legends (LoL)**, the world's most-played PC game. League of Legends became a global esports phenomenon, generating billions in revenue through in-game purchases (skins, champions). Tencent gradually increased its stake in Riot Games to 100% by 2015.

### 2012–2014: International Investment Spree

- **2012**: Acquired a **minority stake in Epic Games** (developer of Fortnite, Unreal Engine) — at the time a relatively unknown US game developer. The stake was reportedly ~40%, making Tencent Epic's largest external shareholder. This investment proved extraordinarily valuable as Fortnite became a global phenomenon and Unreal Engine became the industry-standard game engine.
- **2012**: WeChat reached **100 million monthly active users**.
- **2013**: WeChat reached **300 million monthly active users** — establishing mobile messaging dominance in China.
- **2014**: Invested heavily in **JD.com** (formerly 360buy) — China's second-largest e-commerce company after Alibaba — taking a ~15% stake and providing WeChat's payment infrastructure (WeChat Pay) to power JD's checkout. This partnership gave JD a strategic advantage over Alibaba and established WeChat Pay as a major mobile payment player.
- **2014**: Invested in **Sogou** (搜狗) — China's second-largest search engine after Baidu — taking ~36% stake. Sogou was later delisted and merged into Tencent's ecosystem in 2021.
- **2014**: WeChat reached **500 million monthly active users** (January).

### 2015–2017: WeChat Ecosystem and Supercell

- **2015**: WeChat launched **WeChat Pay** (微信支付) mini-program features and enabled payment for offline merchants — beginning the transformation of WeChat from messaging app to super-app platform. WeChat Pay rapidly grew to compete with Alibaba's Alipay.
- **2016 (June)**: Acquired **84.3% of Supercell** (Finnish game developer of Clash of Clans, Clash Royale, Brawl Stars) for **$8.6 billion** — Tencent's largest gaming acquisition. Supercell's games had hundreds of millions of players globally. This cemented Tencent as the world's largest video game company by revenue.
- **2016**: WeChat crossed **800 million monthly active users**.
- **2017 (September)**: WeChat reached **980 million monthly active users**. The app had become indispensable in Chinese daily life — used for messaging, payments, reading news, hailing taxis, booking appointments, and more.
- **2017**: WeChat launched **Mini Programs** (小程序) — a platform within WeChat allowing third-party developers to build lightweight apps accessible within the WeChat ecosystem without requiring users to download separate apps. Mini Programs became a massive platform for e-commerce, games, and services, with millions of apps by 2024.

### 2018–2020: Growth, Ant Group Plans, and Regulatory Pressure

- **2018**: Tencent's market capitalization crossed **$500 billion** — joining Alibaba as one of the first Chinese tech companies to reach this milestone.
- **2018**: Tencent played a key role in the planned **IPO of Ant Financial** (Alibaba's fintech affiliate, operator of Alipay) — reportedly holding a small stake in Ant Group. The Ant Group IPO was later halted by Chinese regulators in November 2020 in a dramatic reversal.
- **2019**: WeChat reached **1.1 billion monthly active users**.
- **2020**: WeChat and Tencent's other platforms faced increased scrutiny as the US government considered **restricting WeChat** in the US (Executive Order signed by President Trump in August 2020). The order was later blocked by courts. WeChat US had relatively few users compared to China, but the threat affected investor sentiment.

### 2021–2022: Regulatory Crackdown

- **2021**: China's government launched a sweeping **tech regulatory crackdown** — targeting monopolistic practices, data security, and "platform capitalism." Tencent was not the primary target (Alibaba faced the first major action with the Ant Group halt in late 2020), but Tencent was affected:
  - Tencent's music streaming arm was required to end **exclusive music licensing** deals (antitrust remedy)
  - Tencent was fined for M&A violations
  - Gaming approvals were suspended for months (the "video game freeze") — affecting Tencent's massive games business
- **2021**: WeChat introduced stricter **data compliance** features in response to Chinese cybersecurity regulations.
- **2022**: Tencent acquired a **stake in Ubisoft** (France, maker of Assassin's Creed, Far Cry) — increasing its stake to ~11.5%, becoming Ubisoft's largest shareholder. Ubisoft has faced pressure from Tencent (and other shareholders) regarding its strategic direction and stock performance.
- **2022**: Tencent's market cap was significantly reduced from its 2021 peak due to regulatory pressures and slower growth.

### 2023–2024: Recovery and AI Focus

- **2023**: Tencent recovered strongly as gaming approvals resumed and regulatory concerns eased. Revenue grew, driven by gaming recovery and advertising growth.
- **2023**: Launched **Tencent Hunyuan** — a large language model (LLM) AI foundation model, signaling Tencent's entry into the generative AI race alongside Baidu (Ernie Bot), ByteDance, and others.
- **2024**: WeChat continued to grow — reaching **1.3 billion monthly active users** globally (including 700+ million in China).
- **2024 (March)**: Tencent reported **2024 full-year revenue of CNY 660.3 billion (~$91.8 billion)** and net profit of CNY 194 billion — reflecting strong recovery in gaming and advertising.
- **2024**: WeChat launched improved **AI features** including AI-powered search and content recommendations powered by Tencent Hunyuan.
- **2024**: Tencent announced significant **buybacks** of its shares — repurchasing billions of dollars worth of stock as its cash pile grew, returning capital to shareholders.

## Key Products and Business Segments

### Social Platforms
- **WeChat** (微信) — super-app with messaging, social feed (Moments), mini-programs, WeChat Pay, and services; 1.3B+ MAU globally
- **QQ** — desktop and mobile IM platform; hundreds of millions of users (primarily younger demographic in China)
- **WeChat Channels** (视频号) — short-video and live-streaming feature within WeChat, competing with Douyin; rapidly growing

### Gaming (via subsidiaries and stakes)
- **Riot Games** (acquired 2011, 100% owned) — League of Legends, Valorant, Teamfight Tactics
- **Supercell** (acquired 2016, 84.3%) — Clash of Clans, Clash Royale, Brawl Stars
- **Epic Games** (~40% stake, 2012) — Fortnite, Unreal Engine
- **Ubisoft** (~11.5%, 2022) — Assassin's Creed, Far Cry series
- **TiMi Studio Group** (Tencent's internal studio) — Honor of Kings, PUBG Mobile (via licensing), Genshin Impact is co-developed with miHoYo (Tencent is investor)

### Fintech
- **WeChat Pay** (微信支付) — second-largest mobile payment platform in China after Alipay; integrated into WeChat ecosystem
- **Tencent Financial Technology** — handles wealth management, micro-loans, and insurance services within WeChat

### Cloud and Enterprise
- **Tencent Cloud** (腾讯云) — cloud computing services competing with Alibaba Cloud and Huawei Cloud; third-largest in China
- **Tencent Meeting** (腾讯会议) — video conferencing platform, surged during COVID-19
- **QQ Mail** — email service with large Chinese user base

### Entertainment and Content
- **Tencent Music Entertainment** (TME) — streaming music platforms (QQ Music, Kugou, Kuwo); ~80% of Chinese music streaming market
- **Tencent Video** (腾讯视频) — video streaming platform competing with iQIYI, Youku, Bilibili; major investment in Chinese dramas and variety shows
- **WeRead** — e-book reading platform
- **Tencent Sports** — sports content and streaming

### AI
- **Hunyuan** — Tencent's large language model (LLM) foundation model (launched 2023)
- AI integration across WeChat, cloud services, and enterprise products

## Corporate Structure

- **Chairman & CEO**: **Pony Ma** (马化腾) — co-founder, has led since founding
- **President**: **Martin Lau** (刘炽平) — since 2006; the key dealmaker who executed most of Tencent's major acquisitions and investments
- **Major shareholder**: **Naspers** (South Africa, ~26.9% stake as of 2024)
- **Headquarters**: Shenzhen, Guangdong, China
- **Employees**: ~108,000 (2024)
- Listed on Hong Kong Stock Exchange (00700.HK) since 2004
