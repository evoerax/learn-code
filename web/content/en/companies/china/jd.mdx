# JD.com

JD.com, Inc. (京东集团) is a Chinese multinational e-commerce company headquartered in Beijing, founded in **1998** by **Liu Qiangdong** (刘强东, also known as Richard Liu). What began as a small optical products retail shop (京东多媒体) in Zhongguancun, Beijing, grew into China's second-largest e-commerce company by revenue (after Alibaba) and the country's undisputed leader in logistics infrastructure and supply chain management. Unlike Alibaba's marketplace model where third-party merchants handle their own logistics, JD.com built and operates its own nationwide fulfillment network — **JD Logistics** — with 1,600+ warehouses, same-day and next-day delivery in over 200 cities, and 90%+ of orders delivered via JD's own last-mile delivery staff (over 250,000 couriers). This asset-heavy, vertically integrated model makes JD unique globally and delivers the highest customer satisfaction ratings in Chinese e-commerce. With **580 million active customer accounts** and FY2024 revenue of **¥1.08 trillion (~$153 billion)** (making it larger than Alibaba by revenue in FY2024), JD is a pillar of Chinese commerce and one of the world's most technologically advanced supply chain companies.

## Timeline

### 1998–2004: Founding and Optical Retail Origins

- **1998 (June)**: Liu Qiangdong opened **京东多媒体** (JD Multimedia) — a small retail shop selling magneto-optical products (VCDs, DVDs) in Zhongguancun, Beijing's "China Silicon Valley" technology district. The shop was successful from the start, generating ¥12 million in revenue in its first year.
- **2001**: JD became the largest retailer of consumer electronics in Beijing, with multiple physical stores. Liu began contemplating transitioning to online retail as the Internet boom accelerated.
- **2003 (SARS)**: The SARS epidemic forced all physical stores to close temporarily. JD's stores were devastated by the lack of foot traffic. Liu and his staff ran the business entirely online through QQ (the dominant instant messenger at the time) and through a makeshift website — this experience convinced Liu that online retail was the future.
- **2004**: Liu closed all 12 physical stores and transitioned entirely to **JD.com** as an online retailer. This was a bold decision at a time when few Chinese consumers trusted buying expensive electronics online. JD initially focused on 3C (computer, communications, consumer electronics) products.

### 2005–2009: 3C Dominance and Early Growth

- **2005**: JD.com began selling products beyond electronics, expanding into general consumer goods. The company leveraged its reputation for genuine products and fast delivery in the 3C category to build trust across categories.
- **2007**: JD made a critical strategic decision to transition to a **full-SKU model** (rather than just electronics) — stocking its own inventory and managing its own fulfillment. This was expensive but differentiated JD's delivery speed and quality. This model became JD's defining competitive advantage.
- **2008**: JD raised its first significant venture funding — **10 million USD** from IDG Capital. The capital funded warehouse construction and expansion beyond electronics.
- **2009**: JD became the **#1 online retailer of 3C products in China**. The company operated warehouses in Beijing, Shanghai, and Guangzhou, enabling faster delivery to major population centers.

### 2010–2014: Logistics Infrastructure and IPO Preparation

- **2010**: JD launched **211限时达** (211 Standard) — orders placed before 11:00 AM delivered same day, orders before 11:00 PM delivered next day. This service became a hallmark of JD's brand and set a new standard for e-commerce delivery in China.
- **2010**: JD raised **$150 million** from Highland Capital Partners and DST Global (Yuri Milner), validating its model and providing capital for massive logistics infrastructure build-out.
- **2011**: JD raised **$1.5 billion** in a round led by DST Global, Tiger Global, and other investors — one of the largest e-commerce funding rounds in history at the time. This funded warehouse expansion to 30 cities.
- **2012**: JD launched **JD Logistics** as a standalone business, offering third-party merchants access to JD's warehouse and delivery network — this was the precursor to JD's logistics-as-a-service model.
- **2013**: JD launched its **mobile app** and expanded into **JD Fresh** (fresh produce and groceries), competing with Tmall Supermarket. Also launched **JD International** for cross-border imported goods.
- **2014 (May)**: JD completed its **NYSE IPO** at $19 per ADS, raising **$1.78 billion** — the largest Chinese e-commerce IPO at the time (until Alibaba later that year). Valuation at IPO: ~$26 billion.

### 2015–2019: Non-Electronics Expansion and Technology

- **2015**: JD became China's **largest retailer by revenue** (a significant milestone). The company launched **JD Finance** (later JD Technology/JD DigiTech) as a separate entity — offering supply chain finance, consumer credit, and payment services (JD Pay).
- **2016 (June)**: JD and **Walmart** formed a strategic partnership: Walmart sold its Yihaodian (一号店) e-grocery business to JD in exchange for 5% equity ($7.6 billion valuation). Walmart also invested $1.5 billion for additional 5% stake. This gave JD leadership in online grocery and Walmart access to JD's logistics.
- **2017**: JD launched **"Jingxi"** (惊喜) — a social commerce platform targeting lower-tier cities and price-sensitive consumers, competing with Pinduoduo. Also launched **JD无人超市 (JD Go)** — unmanned convenience stores using RFID and computer vision.
- **2017**: JD announced **"Retail as a Service"** (零售即服务) — offering its supply chain, logistics, and technology capabilities to offline retailers. JD became the technology partner for **7Fresh** (supermarket chain) and various department store chains.
- **2018**: JD announced a **$500 million partnership with Google** — Google took a 0.93% stake. This was intended to help JD expand internationally and leverage Google's technology for JD's overseas operations.
- **2019**: JD launched **JD Business** (企业购) — B2B procurement services for enterprise and government clients. JD became the dominant online platform for government and corporate procurement in China.

### 2020–2022: COVID Resilience and New Infrastructure

- **2020 (January-March)**: JD's proprietary logistics network proved critical during COVID-19 lockdowns — JD was one of the few e-commerce platforms that could deliver essential goods (masks, sanitizers, food) reliably, gaining significant brand trust. JD's drone and robot delivery programs were accelerated by pandemic contactless delivery needs.
- **2020**: JD Logistics completed its **HKEX listing** (HK: 2618), raising $3.2 billion. JD retained 50%+ ownership. JD Logistics operated 900+ warehouses with 200+ cities same/next-day delivery.
- **2021**: JD became the **first Chinese e-commerce company** to offer **enterprise-level distributed ledger / blockchain supply chain tracking** — customers could trace the origin of products (especially premium goods like baby formula, imported beef) on the blockchain. This became a trust differentiator.
- **2021**: JD reached **500 million active customer accounts**. Revenue hit ¥951.6 billion (~$148 billion).
- **2022**: JD launched **JD Curated** (京东特卖) — a flash-sale and deal platform competing more directly with Pinduoduo. Also expanded **Jingxi** into a standalone app with group-buying features.

### 2023–2025: AI Integration and Global Expansion

- **2023**: JD launched **JD AI** — integrating large language models into JD's e-commerce platform (customer service chatbots, personalized recommendations, logistics optimization). The company also launched **Jingxi** as a mass-market social commerce platform with over 100 million users.
- **2023**: JD announced **"Jingxi 3.0"** strategy — targeting lower-tier Chinese cities and rural markets with an expanded product assortment and lower prices. This was a direct response to Pinduoduo's dominance in those markets.
- **2024**: JD reached **580 million active customer accounts**. FY2024 revenue was **¥1.08 trillion (~$153 billion)** — making JD larger than Alibaba Group's FY2024 revenue (¥941.1 billion). JD Logistics operated **1,600+ warehouses** covering all of China's prefecture-level cities.
- **2024**: JD announced a strategic partnership with **Meituan** for cross-platform delivery integration — JD users could order Meituan delivery services through the JD app.
- **2025**: JD launched **Jingxi Max** — a premium version of Jingxi targeting middle-class consumers in tier-2 and tier-3 cities. Also announced expansion of **JD Express** (即时配送) — 30-minute delivery service in major cities competing with Meituan's flash sales business.

## Key Products and Business Segments

### JD Retail (Core E-Commerce)
- Full SKU model with 900+ million product SKUs; operates 1,600+ warehouses; 90%+ of orders delivered by JD's own delivery staff
- **JD.com** main platform: electronics, appliances, general merchandise
- **Jingxi**: social commerce targeting lower-tier cities and price-sensitive consumers
- **JD Fresh**: fresh produce, groceries, and cold-chain delivery
- **JD Global**: cross-border import platform for premium international brands

### JD Logistics
- China's most advanced logistics network; 1,600+ warehouses; 90%+ same/next-day delivery; 250,000+ delivery staff; 100% proprietary last-mile network in major cities
- **JD Supply Chain**: integrated logistics-as-a-service for third-party merchants
- **JD Express**: 30-minute flash delivery competing with Meituan
- **JD Cold Chain**: specialized cold-storage and fresh-food logistics

### JD Technology (JD DigiTech / JD Cloud)
- **JD Cloud**: public cloud computing services competing with Alibaba Cloud and Huawei Cloud
- **JD AI**: LLM-powered customer service, logistics optimization, fraud detection
- **Blockchain**: JD's supply chain traceability platform — widely used for premium import goods

### JD Finance
- **JD Pay**: integrated payment within JD app
- **JD Baitiao**: consumer credit product (BNPL before that term was widely used)
- **Supply Chain Finance**: credit for suppliers based on JD's order data

## Corporate Governance

- **Founder & CEO**: Liu Qiangdong (刘强东, Richard Liu) — visionary entrepreneur who built JD's unique asset-heavy model; stepped back from day-to-day operations 2020 following personal controversy but remains controlling shareholder
- **CEO (Retail)**: Xin Lijun (辛利军) — leads JD Retail
- **Headquarters**: Beijing, China
- **Listed**: NASDAQ (JD, since 2014), HKEX (9618.HK, since 2020)
- **Employees**: ~450,000 (2024, including 250,000+ JD Logistics couriers)
- **Key shareholding**: Liu Qiangdong ~15% (Class B shares with 80% voting rights); Walmart 9.4%; Tencent 17%+ (historically, reduced over time)
