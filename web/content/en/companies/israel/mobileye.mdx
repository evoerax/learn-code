# Mobileye

Mobileye N.V. (NYSE: MBLY) is an Israeli autonomous driving and advanced driver assistance systems (ADAS) company headquartered in Jerusalem, founded in **1999** by **Amnon Shashua**, **Norio Ichihashi**, and **Ziv Aviram**. Mobileye pioneered the concept of **vision-based collision avoidance systems** — using cameras and AI algorithms instead of radar or lidar as the primary sensor for vehicle perception. The company's breakthrough came when it demonstrated that a single camera chip could perform lane keeping, collision warning, and pedestrian detection at a fraction of the cost of competing approaches, convincing major automakers to adopt its technology as standard equipment. By 2024, Mobileye's **EyeQ chips** power ADAS features in vehicles from **BMW, Ford, Nissan, Volkswagen, Geely, and 27+ other automakers**, and the company's technology has been deployed in over **150 million vehicles** worldwide. Mobileye went public on the NYSE in 2014 (raising $890 million), was acquired by Intel for **$15.3 billion** in 2017 (the largest Israeli tech acquisition at the time), and re-listed on Nasdaq in 2022 (raising $861 million) while Intel retained an **88.3% ownership stake**. With **$1.65 billion in revenue** in FY2024 but operating losses of $3.2 billion (driven by heavy R&D investment in autonomous driving), Mobileye represents one of the most ambitious bets on self-driving technology and the intersection of automotive safety, AI, and autonomy.

## Timeline

### 1999–2007: Origins and Early Struggles

- **1999**: **Amnon Shashua**, a computer vision professor at Hebrew University, co-founds Mobileye with **Norio Ichihashi** (a former classmate from MIT) and **Ziv Aviram**. Shashua's academic research in computer vision and machine learning forms the technical foundation. The name "Mobileye" is a portmanteau of "Mobile" (automotive) and "Eye" (vision). The company is initially funded by venture capital and angel investors.
- **2001**: Mobileye launches its first product — a **collision warning system** using a single forward-facing camera and vision processing software. The system could detect vehicles ahead and provide audio and visual warnings to drivers.
- **2004**: Mobileye achieves a major design win — **BMW** agrees to evaluate Mobileye's technology for a production vehicle. This validation from a premium OEM is critical for credibility.
- **2007**: **General Motors** selects Mobileye's technology for its **Lane Keep Assist** feature across multiple GM models — Mobileye's first major production contract with a US automaker.

### 2008–2014: EyeQ Chip and the ADAS Revolution

- **2008**: Launches **EyeQ1** — Mobileye's first integrated system-on-chip (SoC) combining image capture, image processing, and decision algorithms on a single chip. The EyeQ architecture was revolutionary: rather than requiring a powerful general-purpose processor, Mobileye designed a custom chip optimized specifically for the vision processing pipelines required for driving perception. EyeQ1 powered the first generation of production ADAS features.
- **2010**: **Volkswagen** becomes a major customer, adopting Mobileye's EyeQ chips for adaptive cruise control and lane keeping across its volume brands (VW, Audi, Skoda).
- **2011**: Mobileye achieves significant scale — over **1 million EyeQ chips** shipped cumulatively since launch, establishing the company as the dominant player in vision-based ADAS.
- **2013**: EyeQ3 launches — a significantly more powerful chip capable of supporting more complex ADAS features including traffic sign recognition, automatic high beams, and forward collision warning with pedestrian detection. Multiple automakers adopt EyeQ3 simultaneously.
- **2014 (July)**: **Goes public on NYSE**, raising **$890 million** at a market cap of ~$5.3 billion. The IPO was one of the largest Israeli tech IPOs at the time and validated over a decade of Shashua's vision-first approach to autonomous driving. Shares priced at $25, closed at $37 on day one.

### 2015–2017: Tesla Partnership, Growth, and Intel Acquisition

- **2015 (October)**: **Tesla** announces that Mobileye's EyeQ3 will power the first generation of **Autopilot** features on the Tesla Model S and Model X — including automatic lane keeping, adaptive cruise control, and collision warning. The partnership with Tesla (led by Elon Musk) brought Mobileye into the public spotlight as the "brain" behind Tesla's Autopilot. The partnership also included Mobileye providing HD mapping data through its **REM (Road Experience Management)** platform.
- **2016 (January)**: Mobileye and **BMW** announce a partnership to develop a fully autonomous vehicle platform by 2021, combining Mobileye's vision systems with BMW's automotive engineering.
- **2016 (May)**: A **fatal accident** occurs when a Tesla Model S operating in Autopilot mode struck a white truck crossing the road — the driver was killed. The accident, later determined to be partly due to the limitations of Mobileye's EyeQ3 camera-based system (which couldn't distinguish a white truck trailer from the sky), led Mobileye to publicly **end its partnership with Tesla** in September 2016. This was a dramatic and highly public separation — Shashua stated Mobileye's technology was "not designed to handle crossing traffic." The incident significantly affected Mobileye's reputation in the short term but proved Shashua's commitment to not overstating his technology's capabilities.
- **2017 (March)**: **Intel** acquires Mobileye for **$15.3 billion** ($63.54 per share, 35% premium to pre-announcement share price) — the **largest acquisition of an Israeli technology company** at the time. Intel CEO Brian Krzanich described the deal as combining Mobileye's "best-in-class computer vision capabilities" with Intel's leading Xeon processors and 5G technology to create "the leading autonomous driving company." Following the acquisition, Mobileye operates as an Intel subsidiary while retaining its brand and Jerusalem headquarters. Shashua becomes President and CEO of Mobileye and a senior VP at Intel.

### 2018–2021: Post-Acquisition Expansion and New Architectures

- **2018**: Launches **EyeQ4** — a significantly more powerful chip capable of supporting Level 3 autonomous features (conditional automation where the driver can take their hands off the wheel in specific conditions). EyeQ4 is adopted by **Nissan, Volkswagen, Ford, and BMW** for their next-generation ADAS platforms.
- **2019**: Mobileye announces its **Responsibility-Sensitive Safety (RSS)** model — an open, formal mathematical model for defining "safe driving" behavior in autonomous vehicles. RSS defines a set of logic rules that an autonomous vehicle's decision system must follow, providing a rigorous safety framework that can be audited and verified, as opposed to the black-box nature of neural network-based driving systems.
- **2019**: **Geely** (China's largest private automaker, parent of Volvo, Polestar, Lotus, and others) announces a major partnership to use Mobileye's technology across its brands. This positions Mobileye as a key technology supplier for the Chinese automotive market.
- **2020**: Launches **Mobileye SuperVision** — a Level 2+ (hands-on) autonomous driving system using 11 cameras (surround vision) + EyeQ5 chips, enabling automated lane changes, highway pilot, and urban navigation in some conditions. The first production implementation was in the **Zeekr** brand (Geely) in China.
- **2021**: Mobileye achieves a milestone — its technology is deployed in vehicles from **27+ automakers** and has been shipped in **over 100 million vehicles** cumulatively. The company also announces the **EyeQ6** chip development, targeting Level 4 autonomous capabilities.

### 2022: Re-IPO on Nasdaq

- **2022 (October)**: Mobileye **re-lists on Nasdaq** (ticker: MBLY), raising **$861 million** in an IPO that valued the company at $17 billion. Intel retained an **88.3% ownership stake** after the IPO, continuing to consolidate Mobileye's financials. The IPO was smaller than Intel had initially planned (originally targeting a $30B valuation in early 2022) as market conditions deteriorated in the tech downturn. Shares fell 7.7% on the first day of trading.

### 2023–2024: EyeQ6, SuperVision Expansion, and Autonomous Driving Push

- **2023**: Launches **EyeQ6 Ultra** — the most advanced EyeQ chip to date, designed specifically for Level 4 autonomous driving systems (vehicles that can drive themselves in most conditions without driver intervention). EyeQ6 Ultra delivers 176 TOPS (tera operations per second) of AI performance while consuming only 45 watts — an impressive efficiency figure for a high-performance autonomous driving chip.
- **2023**: **Mobileye Drive** — a Level 4 autonomous driving system using 13 cameras, 3 long-range lidars, 6 short-range lidars, and radar — begins commercial deployments in autonomous robotaxi fleets. Mobileye's approach uniquely relies primarily on cameras ("vision-first") while adding lidars for redundancy in its Drive product (unlike the SuperVision product which is camera-only).
- **2023**: **Mobileye Chauffeur** — a Level 3/4 system for premium vehicles enabling eyes-off, hands-off autonomous driving on highways — is announced with production commitments from multiple OEMs.
- **2024**: Mobileye's technology powers vehicles in **50+ countries**. The company's **REM (Road Experience Management)** platform — which crowdsources real-time road data from millions of vehicles equipped with Mobileye cameras — has mapped **over 10 billion kilometers** of roads globally, creating a massive proprietary data asset.
- **2024**: Revenue reaches **$1.65 billion** — up from ~$1.3B in 2023. However, the company posts an **operating loss of $3.2 billion** and **net loss of $3.1 billion**, reflecting massive R&D spending on Level 4/5 autonomous driving development. The losses are largely driven by Mobileye's investments in its Drive robotaxi platform, next-generation chips, and mapping operations.

### 2025–2026: Restructuring, Mentee Acquisition, and Autonomy Milestones

- **2025**: Mobileye announces **layoffs of approximately 200 employees** (~2% of workforce) — a modest but notable reduction reflecting the company's need to manage costs while continuing heavy autonomous driving R&D investment amid a challenging capital markets environment.
- **2025**: **Geely** expands Mobileye SuperVision deployment across additional vehicle models under the Zeekr, Polestar, and Volvo brands, with plans for a new generation of vehicles using Mobileye's next-generation platform.
- **2026 (January)**: Mobileye announces the acquisition of **Mentee Robotics** for **$900 million** — an AI robotics company developing mobile manipulation robots. Mentee was founded by professors from Carnegie Mellon and Tel Aviv University and specializes in learning-based robot control. This acquisition signals Mobileye's intent to expand its autonomy technology beyond automotive into general robotics applications.
- **2026**: **Mobileye's Level 4 autonomous vehicle (Drive)** is expected to be deployed commercially in **multiple new markets**, including expansion from initial robotaxi deployments in Israel and Germany into additional countries.

## Key Products and Technology

### EyeQ Chip Family
- **EyeQ1** (2008): First-generation SoC, Level 1 ADAS features
- **EyeQ2** (2010): Improved processing, wider adoption
- **EyeQ3** (2014): First to support Tesla Autopilot, traffic sign recognition
- **EyeQ4** (2018): Level 2+ support, adopted by VW, Ford, Nissan, BMW
- **EyeQ5** (2020): Level 2++ and early Level 3, 10 TOPS per chip
- **EyeQ6 Ultra** (2023): Level 4 design, 176 TOPS, 45W TDP — flagship autonomous chip

### Autonomous Driving Platforms
- **Mobileye SuperVision**: Camera-only Level 2+ (hands-on) system using 11 surround cameras + EyeQ5. Enables highway pilot, automated lane changes, and parking. Deployed in Geely/Zeekr vehicles in China and select global markets.
- **Mobileye Drive**: Level 4 autonomous driving system (eyes-off, hands-off in defined domains) using 13 cameras + 3 long-range lidars + 6 short-range lidars + radar. Targets robotaxi and autonomous shuttle fleets. Deployed commercially in Israel (via POETS — a joint venture with Volkswagen and Champion Motors) and in Germany (via a similar joint venture).
- **Mobileye Chauffeur**: Level 3/4 system for premium consumer vehicles — enables eyes-off highway autonomous driving. In development with multiple OEM production commitments expected 2025-2026.

### Mapping and Data
- **REM (Road Experience Management)**: A crowdsourced HD mapping system where every vehicle equipped with Mobileye cameras contributes real-time road data (lane markings, traffic signs, road geometry) to a continuously updated global map. Over **10 billion kilometers** mapped, used by Mobileye's autonomous driving systems and licensed to partners.
- **True Redundancy™**: Mobileye's approach to sensor fusion — using two independent perception systems (camera-only and lidar/radar-based) that each can independently drive the vehicle, with a higher-level fusion system selecting the best response.

### Safety Framework
- **RSS (Responsibility-Sensitive Safety)**: An open mathematical model for autonomous vehicle safety decision-making. RSS defines formal rules for safe following distance, proper right-of-way yielding, and hazard response — creating a verifiable, auditable safety case that can be explained to regulators and insurers. Mobileye has advocated for RSS to become an industry standard.

### Mobileye after Intel: Corporate Structure
- **Parent**: Intel Corporation (88.3% ownership post-2022 IPO)
- **Listed**: NYSE: MBLY (since October 2022 re-IPO)
- **Headquarters**: Jerusalem, Israel (with major engineering centers in Israel, the US, Germany, China, and Japan)
- **CEO**: **Amnon Shashua** (President & CEO, also Intel Senior VP)
- **Chairman**: **Saf Yeboah-Amankwah**
- **Employees**: ~5,000+ (2025, up from ~3,000 pre-IPO due to autonomous driving hiring)
- **Customers**: 27+ automakers including BMW, Volkswagen, Audi, Ford, Nissan, Geely, Volvo, Polestar, and others

- Mobileye's massive 2024 operating loss ($3.2B) is partly due to $2.5B in acquisition-related amortization charges from Intel's 2017 purchase — not purely operational losses. Stripping out these accounting charges, the underlying business is closer to break-even.
- EyeQ chip unit economics are strong — chips are sold as part of ADAS packages where Mobileye earns royalties on each vehicle, creating high-margin recurring revenue per vehicle

## Key Learnings

- **Vision-first approach as a long-term bet**: Shashua's decision in 1999 to pursue camera-only perception (rather than lidar or radar) was deeply controversial. Lidar proponents argued that cameras couldn't achieve the reliability required for safety-critical systems. Shashua's counter-argument: cameras are the only sensor that can read lane markings, traffic signs, and road text — the primary semantic information needed for driving. The debate continues today, with Tesla (Mobileye's former customer) fully committed to vision-only while most other autonomous driving companies use multi-sensor fusion.
- **Tesla partnership ending (2016)**: The public breakup with Tesla after the fatal crash was both a short-term setback and a validation of Shashua's integrity — he refused to overstate his technology's capabilities and chose to end the relationship rather than compromise on safety claims. The incident also revealed the tension between Mobileye's Tier 2 supplier model (where automakers integrate Mobileye's technology) and Tesla's vertical integration approach.
- **Intel acquisition as the right home**: The $15.3B Intel acquisition (2017) gave Mobileye the massive R&D budget and manufacturing scale needed to develop Level 4/5 systems. Intel's foundry capabilities also positioned Mobileye to eventually manufacture its own chips rather than relying solely on external fabs (STMicroelectronics for EyeQ4 and TSMC for EyeQ6).
- **Mentee Robotics acquisition ($900M, 2026)**: Mobileye's expansion into general robotics via Mentee suggests Shashua believes the computer vision and decision-making algorithms developed for autonomous driving are transferable to other robotics domains — a plausible but still speculative thesis.
