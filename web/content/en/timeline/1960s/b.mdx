## Origin Story

B was created by Ken Thompson at Bell Labs in 1969, initially for the newly developed PDP-11 minicomputer. Thompson had been using BCPL on the PDP-7 and wanted a similar but simpler language for the new hardware. He stripped BCPL down to its essentials and created B.

The name itself was a logical progression: BCPL came before B, and B was the second letter in the alphabet—though some joke it stood for "Basic" or was simply the letter before C. B was heavily used in early UNIX development at Bell Labs, particularly for writing the UNIX operating system itself and its utilities.

## Design Philosophy

<Callout type="default" emoji="💡">
  "B was BCPL stripped down to the bare minimum, designed to fit in the limited memory of the PDP-11."
</Callout>

Key characteristics:

- **Minimalism**: Thompson removed features to make it fit in tight memory
- **Integer-only types**: Like BCPL, B used a single type for all data
- **Memory efficiency**: Programs were compact, suitable for the 24KB PDP-11
- **Interpreted execution**: Early B was interpreted, not compiled
- **Simple syntax**: Carried forward BCPL's expression-based, brace-delimited style

## Influence & Legacy

**Inspired by:** BCPL

**Influenced:** C, early UNIX ecosystem

B's significance lies primarily in what it led to. Dennis Ritchie took B and evolved it into C in the early 1970s, adding struct types, better type safety, and compiled execution. C went on to become one of the most important languages in computing history, used for operating systems, embedded systems, and countless applications.

B also directly influenced the development of the UNIX operating system. Early UNIX was written in B and assembly, and the combination of UNIX written in C and C written on UNIX created a bootstrapping ecosystem that shaped modern computing.
